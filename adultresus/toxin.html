Toxins and Antidotes: Emergency Department Management

Common Toxins and Their Antidotes

Toxin Signs and Symptoms Antidote
Acetaminophen Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, confusion, liver failure N-acetylcysteine (NAC)
Aspirin Tinnitus, confusion, nausea, vomiting, metabolic acidosis Sodium bicarbonate, activated charcoal, hemodialysis
Benzodiazepines Sedation, respiratory depression, coma Flumazenil
Carbon Monoxide Headache, nausea, dizziness, confusion, loss of consciousness Oxygen therapy, hyperbaric oxygen therapy
Cyanide Anxiety, confusion, coma, seizures, cardiac arrest Sodium nitrite, sodium thiosulfate, hydroxocobalamin
Lead Abdominal pain, anemia, confusion, seizures Dimercaprol (BAL), EDTA, succimer
Organophosphates Sweating, miosis, bradycardia, respiratory depression, coma Atropine
Tricyclic antidepressants Confusion, tachycardia, hypotension, seizures, coma Sodium bicarbonate

Emergency Department Management of Toxins

When managing a patient with a suspected toxin exposure, it is important to:

  • Obtain a thorough history and physical exam to identify the toxin and the severity of symptoms
  • Remove the patient from the source of the toxin, if possible
  • Perform appropriate decontamination measures, such as removing contaminated clothing and washing the skin or eyes with water
  • Administer antidotes as appropriate
  • Provide supportive care, such as oxygen, intravenous fluids, and monitoring of vital signs and cardiac rhythm
  • Consider consultation with a toxicologist or poison control center for further management guidance
  • Observe the patient for delayed or recurrent symptoms and potential complications, such as multi-organ failure or electrolyte imbalances
