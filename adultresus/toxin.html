1. Initial ED Approach to Suspected Poisoning

  1. Stabilise – ABCDE
    • Airway: protect early if reduced GCS, seizures, or risk of aspiration.
    • Breathing: oxygen, support ventilation; beware respiratory depression (opioids, benzos, sedatives).
    • Circulation: IV access, fluids as needed, monitor BP, HR, rhythm.
    • Disability: GCS, pupils, capillary glucose (hypoglycaemia can mimic overdose).
    • Exposure: temperature, check for patches, injection sites, containers in pockets/bag.
  2. Decontamination (only if safe & appropriate)
    • Remove contaminated clothing, wash exposed skin/eyes with copious water.
    • Activated charcoal: only under senior/toxicology guidance (usually within 1 hour, protected airway).
  3. Look for a toxidrome
    • Opioid: CNS depression, pin-point pupils, hypoventilation.
    • Sympathomimetic: agitation, tachycardia, hypertension, dilated pupils, sweating.
    • Anticholinergic: tachycardia, hyperthermia, dry skin, dilated pupils, urinary retention, delirium.
    • Cholinergic (organophosphates): SLUDGE/DUMBELS, bronchorrhoea, bronchospasm.
  4. Early investigations
    • VBG/ABG, U&E, LFTs, glucose, FBC, ECG.
    • Drug-specific levels where relevant & available (e.g. paracetamol).
Red flags – call senior/toxicology and consider ICU:
  • Airway compromise or persistent hypoxia
  • Shock, arrhythmia, or significant ECG changes
  • Recurrent seizures or reduced GCS not improving
  • Severe metabolic acidosis or rising lactate
  • Suspected massive ingestion of cardiotoxic drugs (e.g. TCAs, beta-blockers, CCBs)

2. Common Toxins and Their Antidotes

This table highlights key ED toxins. Always confirm doses with local protocol / toxicology service.

Toxin / Group Key Features Antidote / Key Therapy
Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) Nausea, vomiting, RUQ pain; initially well then hepatic failure 24–72 h later. N-acetylcysteine (NAC) – start early, do not wait for LFT derangement. Use nomogram if time of ingestion known.
Aspirin / Salicylates Tinnitus, vomiting, tachypnoea, metabolic acidosis, mixed respiratory alkalosis. Sodium bicarbonate infusion (alkalinise serum & urine), activated charcoal (if appropriate), haemodialysis in severe cases.
Opioids CNS depression, pin-point pupils, hypoventilation, bradycardia. Naloxone IV/IM/IN – titrate to adequate ventilation, not full arousal. Beware short half-life vs long-acting opioids.
Benzodiazepines Drowsiness, ataxia, respiratory depression (especially with co-ingestants). Supportive care, airway protection as needed.
Flumazenil is generally avoided in mixed overdoses or chronic users due to seizure risk – specialist decision only.
Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs) Confusion, seizures, hypotension, wide QRS, arrhythmias. Sodium bicarbonate boluses/infusion for QRS widening/hypotension; early intubation in severe cases, continuous ECG monitoring.
Organophosphates / Carbamates Cholinergic toxidrome: salivation, lacrimation, urination, diarrhoea, vomiting, bronchorrhoea, bronchospasm, bradycardia. Atropine (large, repeated doses) for secretions & bronchospasm;
Oximes (e.g. pralidoxime) if available and indicated; early airway control.
Carbon Monoxide Headache, nausea, dizziness, confusion, collapse; normal SpO₂ on pulse oximetry. High-flow oxygen (non-rebreather); consider hyperbaric oxygen in selected severe cases according to protocol.
Cyanide Rapid collapse, seizures, lactic acidosis, cardiovascular collapse (often in fire victims or industrial exposures). Hydroxocobalamin (if available) or cyanide antidote kit (e.g. nitrites + thiosulfate) as per local guidelines, plus aggressive supportive care.
Lead & Heavy metals Abdominal pain, neuropathy, anaemia, encephalopathy (chronic exposures). Chelation therapy: Dimercaprol (BAL), EDTA, or succimer – specialist/toxicology guided.
Beta-blockers Bradycardia, hypotension, hypoglycaemia, shock. Supportive care, atropine, fluids; consider glucagon, high-dose insulin euglycaemic therapy (HIET) per protocol, vasopressors; early senior input.
Calcium Channel Blockers Hypotension, bradycardia, hyperglycaemia, shock. Calcium (chloride/gluconate), HIET, vasopressors, lipid emulsion in selected cases – ICU-level management.

3. General ED Management Principles

  • Take a focused history: time and amount of ingestion, co-ingestants, intent (accidental vs deliberate self-harm), past medical history and meds.
  • Search for evidence: pill bottles, blister packs, chemical containers, family/witness information.
  • Provide supportive care: oxygen, IV fluids, temperature control, seizure control, anti-emetics.
  • Repeat clinical assessment frequently – many overdoses evolve over hours.
  • Safety first: protect staff from contaminated patients (PPE, decontamination area where possible).

Senior tip: antidotes are powerful but limited – most toxicology is supportive care, good observation, and early recognition of deterioration.
