Acute Abdomen – ED Checklist

Use for adults with significant abdominal pain

Aim: recognise surgical abdomen early, resuscitate, investigate appropriately, and refer timeously. Always start with ABCDE.

Tick as you assess. A cluster of positives, especially with abnormal vitals, should trigger early senior/surgical review.

Red Flags – Treat as Surgical Emergency

  • Shock or persistent tachycardia, hypotension, or rising lactate
  • Rigid, board-like abdomen or marked rebound/guarding
  • Severe, sudden onset pain (e.g. ruptured aneurysm, perforation)
  • Pain out of proportion to findings (consider mesenteric ischaemia)
  • Bile/faeculent vomiting or complete obstipation with distension
  • Haematemesis, melaena, or fresh PR bleeding with abdominal pain
  • Positive pregnancy test with abdominal pain and/or collapse

Any red flag + abnormal vitals = early call to senior and surgeon.

Initial ED Management

  1. Resuscitate – ABCDE first
    • Give oxygen if hypoxic; monitor sats and cardiac rhythm.
    • Establish IV access (2 × large bore if shocked); take bloods at the same time.
    • Assess for sepsis and treat according to sepsis/shock protocols.
  2. Analgesia & Symptom Control
    • Give adequate analgesia – do not withhold analgesia for fear of “masking” the diagnosis.
    • Options: titrated IV opioids, IV paracetamol; add anti-emetic (e.g. ondansetron).
    • Keep patient nil by mouth (NBM) until cause is clear.
  3. Fluid Resuscitation
    • Start IV fluids if dehydrated or in shock (e.g. isotonic crystalloid boluses).
    • Insert urinary catheter if concerned about shock or for strict fluid balance.
  4. Focused History & Examination
    • Onset, site, character, radiation, associated symptoms, previous surgery, meds.
    • Examine: general appearance, vitals, abdomen (inspection, auscultation, percussion, palpation), hernial orifices, PR and, when appropriate, pelvic exam.

Key Investigations

  • Bloods: FBC, U&E, LFTs, CRP, amylase/lipase, lactate, glucose, clotting.
  • Group & save / crossmatch in any potentially surgical or unstable patient.
  • β-hCG in all women of child-bearing potential.
  • Urine: dipstick, microscopy, culture as indicated.
  • Imaging:
    • Plain X-ray if perforation/obstruction suspected (depending on local practice).
    • Ultrasound for biliary pathology, AAA, free fluid, gynaecological causes.
    • CT abdomen/pelvis (with contrast if not contraindicated) where available for unclear or high-risk cases.

Common Differentials by Pain Location (Adults)

Epigastric

  • Bowel obstruction

When to Call the Surgeon / Senior Early

  • Any red flag features or peritonitis.
  • Haemodynamic instability or shock despite initial fluids.
  • Suspected perforation, strangulated hernia, bowel ischaemia, or AAA.
  • Obstruction with persistent vomiting, severe pain, or systemic toxicity.
  • Unclear diagnosis but very unwell patient or rising lactate/CRP.

Junior rule of thumb: if you are worried enough to ask, you should call early – stabilise, start basic workup, then involve a senior.
