Acute Severe Asthma – ED Checklist

Use for adults with moderate–life-threatening asthma

Aim: recognise life-threatening features early, start aggressive bronchodilation and steroids, support oxygenation/ventilation, and decide early on ICU/intubation with senior help.

Danger Signs / Life-Threatening Features

Any of the following should trigger immediate senior review and preparation for intubation.

  • Decreased level of consciousness, agitation, confusion, or drowsiness
  • Silent chest or markedly reduced air entry
  • Exhaustion (unable to speak, or only single words)
  • Bradycardia, hypotension, or arrhythmias
  • Rising or high PaCO2 on ABG / VBG despite tachypnoea
  • Pulsus paradoxus, cyanosis, or saturations not improving with oxygen

Immediate ED Management

  1. ABC & Monitoring
    • High flow oxygen to maintain SpO2 ≥ 94% (or 88–92% if CO2 retainers/COPD overlap).
    • Attach cardiac monitor, BP, and continuous pulse oximetry.
    • Establish IV access; consider second line if very unwell.
  2. Bronchodilation – First Line
    • Start continuous or back-to-back nebulised β2-agonist (e.g. salbutamol).
    • Add ipratropium bromide nebs in moderate–severe attacks.
    • Use oxygen-driven nebulisers in hypoxic patients if available.
  3. Systemic Steroids – Give Early
    • Give systemic steroids as soon as possible (e.g. IV hydrocortisone or oral prednisone, per local protocol).
    • Steroids will not work immediately but are essential to prevent relapse and ongoing inflammation.
  4. Magnesium Sulphate
    • Give IV magnesium sulphate in adults with severe/life-threatening asthma not responding to initial therapy.
    • Example: 2 g IV over 20–30 minutes (check local guideline for exact dose and dilution).
    • Involve a senior/medicine early once escalating to IV magnesium or beyond.
  5. Adrenaline (Epinephrine)
    • Consider IM adrenaline in:
      • Suspected anaphylaxis, or
      • Life-threatening asthma not responding to maximal therapy (per local protocol).
    • Use standard IM doses according to local resuscitation guidelines.
  6. Fluids & Support
    • Give IV fluids if hypotensive or dehydrated, but avoid fluid overload.
    • Consider early ICU referral for persistent hypoxia, hypercapnia, or exhaustion.

Important Considerations & Senior Tips

  1. Do not delay intubation in a patient with multiple danger signs (↓ LOC, silent chest, rising CO2, bradycardia, hypotension).
  2. Use the largest appropriate endotracheal tube you can safely pass – reduces airway resistance and allows suction of mucus plugs.
  3. These patients can deteriorate rapidly. Reassess every few minutes, not hours.
  4. Repeated β2-agonist nebs can lower potassium – check U&E and supplement if significantly low.
  5. Most patients with life-threatening features will do better in ICU – decide early and start the referral process.
  6. In ventilated patients, allow longer expiratory time, low respiratory rates, and accept some permissive hypercapnia to avoid barotrauma.

Emergency Investigations

  • Bedside CXR
    • Do in the resus area – these patients should not go off monitor to X-ray if unstable.
    • Look for pneumothorax, consolidation, collapse, lobar collapse, or alternative diagnoses.
  • Bloods
    • Baseline FBC, U&E, CRP, glucose; consider troponin if chest pain or older patient.
    • Check potassium (K+) if multiple nebs or on diuretics.
  • ABG or VBG
    • pH: Normal or low pH in a working-of-breath asthmatic suggests severity and fatigue.
    • PaCO2 (kPa):
      • Low PaCO2 is expected early due to hyperventilation.
      • Normal or rising PaCO2 despite high RR is an ominous sign – think impending respiratory failure.
    • Lactate: Rising lactate suggests poor perfusion and/or β-agonist effect – reassess circulation and treatment.

Rule of thumb: a tired asthmatic with a “normal” PaCO2 is far sicker than a tachypnoeic asthmatic with low PaCO2. Call a senior early.
