ECG Patterns Not to Miss

Aimed at juniors: these patterns may look subtle but carry big consequences.

1. Wellens Syndrome – Critical LAD

  • Pattern: Deeply inverted or biphasic T waves in V2–V3 (sometimes V1–V4) in a pain-free patient.
  • Context: Recent chest pain that has resolved.
  • Why it matters: Critical proximal LAD stenosis – high risk of large anterior MI within days.
  • ED action: Treat as high-risk ACS, no stress testing, early cardiology and angiography.

2. de Winter T-Waves – STEMI Equivalent

  • Pattern: Up-sloping ST depression in V1–V6 with tall, peaked symmetric T waves; slight ST elevation in aVR.
  • Why it matters: Equivalent to acute anterior STEMI (proximal LAD occlusion).
  • ED action: Activate STEMI pathway – needs emergent reperfusion (PCI).

3. Posterior MI

  • Pattern: Horizontal ST depression in V1–V3 ± tall R waves and upright T waves.
  • Why it matters: Represents ST elevation in the posterior leads – often missed.
  • ED action: Do posterior leads (V7–V9). If STE present, treat as STEMI.

4. aVR ST Elevation with Widespread ST Depression

  • Pattern: ST elevation in aVR (≥ 1 mm) with widespread ST depression in multiple leads.
  • Differential: Left main / triple vessel disease, severe proximal LAD, diffuse subendocardial ischaemia.
  • ED action: Treat as high-risk ACS; urgent cardiology discussion and often early angiography.

5. Hyperacute T Waves

  • Pattern: Tall, broad-based T waves that are disproportionate to QRS in a regional distribution.
  • Why it matters: Very early sign of STEMI, often before ST elevation.
  • ED action: Serial ECGs; treat as evolving MI if symptoms consistent.

6. Extras to Be Aware Of

  • Brugada pattern: Coved ST elevation in V1–V3 with RBBB-like pattern – risk of sudden death.
  • Long QT: Risk of torsades de pointes (check drug lists, electrolytes).
  • Massive PE pattern: Sinus tachycardia, S1Q3T3, RBBB, right axis, or T inversion V1–V4.

When in doubt, save the ECG, get a second pair of eyes (senior / cardiology), and always correlate with the patient, not just the tracing.
