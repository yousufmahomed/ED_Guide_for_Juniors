Emergency Hyperkalaemia

Emergency Management of Hyperkalaemia

For use in adults in the ED. Always follow your local drug doses and protocols and involve a senior early.

K⁺ emergency pathway
Treat as an emergency if:
  • K⁺ ≥ 6.5 mmol/L (or rapidly rising)
  • Any K⁺ level with ECG changes compatible with hyperkalaemia
  • Weakness, arrhythmia, or haemodynamic instability

Senior tip: You don’t need to wait for the “perfect” lab result if the ECG is screaming hyperkalaemia. Treat the ECG, not just the number.

One-screen algorithm

Stepwise ED Approach

  1. Confirm & Monitor
    • ABC assessment, attach monitor (BP, pulse oximeter, ECG).
    • Obtain IV access (preferably 2 lines), consider arterial / VBG for quick K⁺.
    • If sample is clearly haemolysed and patient is well with a normal ECG → repeat first, but keep a high index of suspicion.
  2. Stabilise the Myocardium – Calcium
    • Indications: ECG changes of hyperkalaemia or severe K⁺ elevation.
    • Give IV calcium (e.g. calcium gluconate 10% – see drug table below) as per local protocol.
    • Re-check ECG after 5–10 minutes; repeat dose if changes persist.
  3. Shift K⁺ into Cells
    • Insulin + dextrose: standard first-line to shift K⁺ intracellularly.
    • Nebulised salbutamol: adjunct, especially useful if IV access is delayed.
    • Sodium bicarbonate: consider if there is significant metabolic acidosis or refractory hyperkalaemia, following local guidance.
  4. Remove K⁺ from the Body
    • Optimise circulation and urine output – IV fluids if hypovolaemic.
    • Consider loop diuretics (e.g. furosemide) if overloaded and kidneys still producing urine.
    • Discuss K⁺ binders / resins if available, as per local protocol (slower onset).
    • Involve nephrology/ICU early if there is AKI, anuria, or refractory hyperkalaemia – dialysis may be required.
  5. Identify & Treat the Underlying Cause
    • Renal failure, crush injury/rhabdomyolysis, DKA, medications (ACEi, ARBs, spironolactone, NSAIDs, trimethoprim, etc.).
    • Stop offending drugs where possible and treat precipitating conditions.

ECG & Red Flag Features

Typical ECG changes (often progressive as K⁺ rises):

  • Peaked, narrow-based T waves
  • Shortened QT interval
  • Widened QRS, prolonged PR, loss of P waves
  • Sine-wave pattern → pre-arrest

Senior tip: changes can be subtle. If there is any doubt, treat and repeat the ECG rather than ignoring a suspicious tracing.

Drug quick reference – Adults

Emergency Hyperkalaemia Drugs (Typical Doses)

These are common ED adult doses; always cross-check with your local protocol, especially in renal failure, pregnancy, and extremes of age or weight.

Drug Typical Dose Purpose / Notes
Calcium Gluconate 10% 10 mL IV over 5–10 min Stabilises cardiac membrane. Indicated if ECG changes or very high K⁺. May repeat after 5–10 min if ECG changes persist.
Insulin + Dextrose 10 units short-acting insulin IV
+ 50 mL 50% dextrose IV (or equivalent 25 g glucose) 		Shifts K⁺ into cells. Check glucose before and at 30–60 min and monitor for hypoglycaemia.
Nebulised Salbutamol 5–10 mg via nebuliser over 10–15 min Adjunct to insulin/dextrose. Use with caution in tachyarrhythmias or severe ischaemic heart disease.
Sodium Bicarbonate e.g. 50 mmol IV over 30 min (as per protocol) Consider in metabolic acidosis and severe hyperkalaemia; avoid overcorrection/volume overload.
Loop Diuretic (e.g. Furosemide) 20–40 mg IV (titrate as needed) Use if volume overloaded and kidneys are producing urine. Avoid in severe hypovolaemia or anuric AKI.

Senior tip: Calcium buys you time, insulin/dextrose and salbutamol shift K⁺, and dialysis/removal fixes the problem. Don’t rely on a single intervention.

Monitoring & Disposition

  • Continuous cardiac monitoring until K⁺ is safely reduced and ECG normalises.
  • Repeat K⁺ and glucose at 1 hour (sooner if very unstable) and then as per response.
  • Admit to ICU/HDU if:
    • Refractory hyperkalaemia or need for dialysis
    • Significant ECG changes or arrhythmias
    • Associated shock, severe acidosis, or multi-organ failure
  • Consider ward admission for monitoring even after apparent resolution if there is underlying renal failure or high recurrence risk.

Document times and doses of all K⁺-lowering therapies, repeat lab results, ECG changes and discussions with seniors/ICU.
