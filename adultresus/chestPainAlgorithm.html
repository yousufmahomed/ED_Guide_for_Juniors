Chest Pain – Rapid Assessment & Rule-Out

Rule out the killers first

1. Triage & Immediate Threats

  • Assess ABC, attach monitor, obtain vitals, oxygen if needed.
  • Look for red flags:
    • Hypotension, tachycardia or bradycardia
    • Severe distress, diaphoresis, syncope
    • Features of PE, aortic dissection, tension pneumothorax, tamponade

If unstable → resus bay and senior review immediately.

⬇

2. 12-Lead ECG & Bedside Tests (within 10 min)

  • 12-lead ECG: look for STEMI, NSTEMI changes, arrhythmias.
  • Repeat ECGs if pain persists or recurs.
  • Point-of-care tests:
    • Glucose, troponin (high-sensitivity if available), ABG/VBG if unwell.
⬇

3. Consider Life-Threatening Diagnoses

  • ACS / MI
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Aortic dissection
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Pericardial tamponade
  • Oesophageal rupture

Do not anchor on “musculoskeletal” without a proper exclusion of the serious causes.

⬇

4. Chest Pain Risk Stratification (Conceptual)

  • High risk: ischaemic ECG, troponin rise, haemodynamic instability, known CAD.
  • Intermediate risk: concerning history, risk factors, but non-diagnostic ECG / first troponin negative.
  • Low risk: atypical pain, normal ECG, normal serial troponins, no major risk factors.

You can map this to HEART/TIMI scores if you want to formalise it later.

⬇

5. Troponin Strategy (High-Sensitivity or Conventional)

  • 0-hour troponin on arrival.
  • Repeat at 1–3 hours (depending on assay and local protocol).
  • Rule-out generally requires:
    • Non-ischaemic ECG AND
    • Low baseline troponin AND
    • No significant delta change between serial samples.
⬇

6. Disposition

  • High risk / confirmed ACS: admit to CCU/high care; involve cardiology.
  • Intermediate risk: admit/observe with serial troponins, possible stress imaging/CTCA.
  • Low risk: consider discharge with clear safety-net advice and outpatient follow-up.

Write safety-net advice clearly: when to come back, red-flag symptoms, and who to contact.
