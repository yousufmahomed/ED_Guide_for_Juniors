Burns and Emergency Department Management

Burn Classification

Type of Burn Description Treatment
First-degree (Superficial) Affects only the epidermis; redness, mild swelling, and pain; no blisters Cool the burn, apply aloe or moisturizing cream, and use over-the-counter pain relief
Second-degree (Partial Thickness) Affects the epidermis and dermis; redness, blisters, and severe pain; may appear wet or shiny Cover the burn with a sterile, non-adherent dressing, elevate the affected area, and consider pain relief and antibiotics
Third-degree (Full Thickness) Destroys all layers of the skin; may appear white, black, or charred; often painless due to nerve damage Requires immediate medical attention; cover with a sterile, non-adherent dressing and elevate the affected area; fluid resuscitation and pain management
Fourth-degree Extends beyond the skin layers to underlying tissue, muscle, and bone; black or charred appearance; often painless due to nerve damage Requires immediate medical attention; may need surgical intervention, including debridement or amputation

Additional Considerations

  • Chemical burns: Remove the chemical by flushing the area with copious amounts of water, while taking care not to contaminate other body areas or caregivers. Follow specific protocols for the chemical involved.
  • Electrical burns: Disconnect the power source, if possible, and treat any associated injuries, such as fractures or dislocations. Be aware of potential cardiac complications from electrical injuries.
  • Smoke inhalation: Monitor for signs of airway compromise, such as stridor or wheezing, and be aware of potential carbon monoxide or cyanide poisoning.

Emergency Department Management of Burns

  • Assess airway, breathing, and circulation (ABCs)
  • Administer supplemental oxygen if needed
  • Monitor vital signs and provide pain relief
  • Assess burn extent and depth using the burn classification system
  • Initiate fluid resuscitation if necessary based on burn size and patient's weight
  • Remove any constricting clothing or jewelry near the burn area
  • Cover the burn with a sterile, non-adherent dressing or clean cloth
  • Consider tetanus prophylaxis and antibiotic administration based on severity and potential for infection
  • Arrange for appropriate transfer or referral to a specialized burn center if needed
