Burns Management – Emergency Department

Adult & Paediatric Initial ED Assessment

Burn Classification (Depth)

Type Description ED Management
Superficial (1st degree) Red, painful, no blisters; epidermis only Cool water 20 min, aloe/cream, oral analgesia
Partial Thickness – Superficial Red, blistered, moist; very painful Non-adherent dressing, elevate, analgesia
Partial Thickness – Deep Waxy, mottled, reduced pain Specialist referral; dressing + analgesia
Full Thickness (3rd degree) White/charred; painless; no cap refill Urgent referral; fluids; dressings; analgesia
Fourth degree Extends to muscle/bone Immediate surgical referral; may require amputation

Special Burn Types

  • Chemical burns: Remove contaminated clothing; irrigate copiously for ≥20 min. Brush off dry powder first.
  • Electrical burns: Look for hidden deep tissue damage. ECG + cardiac monitoring mandatory.
  • Inhalation injury: Singed hair, hoarse voice, soot, enclosed-space fire → early intubation.
  • Tar/bitumen: Cool the tar, do NOT peel; allow later debridement.
  • Paediatric burns: Suspect NAT (non-accidental trauma) with immersion lines or inconsistent history.

Emergency Department Management

  1. ABCDE First – burns are trauma until proven otherwise.
  2. Airway – Consider early intubation for:
    • Enclosed-space burns
    • Carbonaceous sputum
    • Hoarse voice / stridor
    • Facial burns
  3. Breathing – 100% oxygen; consider CO/Cyanide toxicity.
  4. Circulation – Establish IV access in unburnt skin if possible.
  5. Cool the burn for 20 minutes (not ice).
  6. Remove jewellery; cut around adhered clothing.
  7. Analgesia – prompt and adequate.
  8. Fluid resuscitation using Parkland formula if:
    • ≥10% TBSA in children
    • ≥15–20% TBSA in adults
  9. Dress with sterile, non-adherent layer.
  10. Tetanus status – update if needed.
  11. Document TBSA (%), depth, mechanism, photos if possible.

Burns Drug Quick Reference (Adults)

Drug Typical ED Dose Notes
Analgesia – Morphine 2–5 mg IV q5–10 min titrated Burns are extremely painful; titrate to effect.
Ketamine (analgesic) 0.2–0.3 mg/kg IV Useful for large burns or debridement.
Fluid Resuscitation
Parkland Formula		 4 mL × kg × %TBSA Give ½ in first 8 hours from time of burn, not ED arrival.
Avoid over-resuscitation (“fluid creep”).
Antibiotics Not routinely indicated Only for clear infection—not prophylaxis.
Tetanus Toxoid Per protocol Major burns = tetanus-prone wounds.
Cyanide toxicity treatment Hydroxocobalamin 5 g IV Consider in enclosed-space fires with metabolic acidosis.

Senior tip: Under-resuscitation worsens shock; over-resuscitation causes compartment syndrome and pulmonary oedema.

When to Refer to a Burns Centre

  • Full-thickness burns ≥5% TBSA
  • Partial-thickness burns ≥10% TBSA
  • Burns to face, hands, feet, genitalia, perineum, major joints
  • Chemical, electrical, lightning burns
  • Inhalation injury
  • Paediatric burns beyond local capability
  • Suspicion of non-accidental injury

If you are unsure — call early. Burns centres prefer early involvement over late transfers.
