ED Antidote & Toxin Dosing Quick Reference

Antidote & Toxin Dosing – Quick Ref

Adult dosing guide for time-critical toxicology situations in the ED.

Always confirm with local protocol

Doses below are typical adult starting regimens. Adjust for local guidelines, renal/hepatic impairment, and patient factors. When in doubt, contact toxicology or a senior.

Key ED Antidotes & Therapies

Drug / Therapy Indication Typical Adult Regimen Comments
Critical N-acetylcysteine (NAC) Paracetamol (acetaminophen) overdose Follow local IV NAC protocol (e.g. staged infusion over 21 h or simplified 2-bag regimen). Start as soon as overdose suspected; use nomogram if time of ingestion known. Watch for anaphylactoid reactions.
Critical Naloxone Suspected opioid toxicity with respiratory depression 0.04–0.1 mg IV, titrate every 2–3 min to adequate ventilation (up to 2 mg). If no IV access: IM/IN alternatives as per protocol. Aim for adequate respiratory effort, not full wakefulness. Short half-life – consider infusion for long-acting opioids.
Important Sodium bicarbonate TCA toxicity (QRS widening, hypotension), significant salicylate poisoning TCA: 1–2 mmol/kg IV bolus, repeat as needed, then infusion (e.g. 150 mmol in 1 L D5W). Salicylate: infusion to alkalinise serum & urine. Requires monitoring of pH, sodium, fluid status. Use with senior input.
Important Atropine Organophosphate / carbamate poisoning (cholinergic toxidrome) 1–3 mg IV bolus, then double every 3–5 min until secretions dry & ventilation adequate. May require very large total doses. Target is drying of bronchial secretions, not heart rate alone. Often combined with oxime therapy.
Pralidoxime (2-PAM) Organophosphate poisoning (if available) Example: 30 mg/kg IV over 30 min, then infusion (e.g. 8–10 mg/kg/h), as per local protocol. Works best when given early. Availability varies; follow national guidance.
Hydroxocobalamin Suspected cyanide toxicity 5 g IV over 15 min; a second 5 g dose may be given if needed. Can cause red discolouration of skin/urine and interfere with some lab tests.
Calcium gluconate 10% / Calcium chloride Hyperkalaemia with ECG changes, CCB toxicity Hyperkalaemia: 10 mL of 10% calcium gluconate IV over 5–10 min, repeat as needed. CCB toxicity: higher / repeated doses as per protocol, often via central line if calcium chloride used. Stabilises myocardium. Monitor ECG. Avoid extravasation, especially with calcium chloride.
Critical HIET (High-dose insulin euglycaemic therapy) Severe beta-blocker or calcium channel blocker toxicity with shock Example regimen (confirm locally): • Bolus: 1 unit/kg IV insulin with 25 g IV dextrose (50 mL of 50%) • Infusion: start at 0.5–1 unit/kg/h IV, titrate up (often up to 10 units/kg/h) • Concurrent dextrose infusion to maintain euglycaemia. ICU-level therapy. Requires frequent glucose and potassium monitoring. Combine with vasopressors and calcium as needed.
Important Intravenous lipid emulsion (Intralipid) Lipophilic drug toxicity (e.g. local anaesthetic systemic toxicity, some cardio-toxic overdoses) Example regimen (20% lipid, confirm protocol): • Bolus: 1.5 mL/kg IV over 1 min • Infusion: 0.25 mL/kg/min for 30–60 min • May repeat bolus and increase infusion rate if cardiovascular collapse persists. Reserve for life-threatening toxicity or cardiac arrest due to lipophilic agents. Follow local toxicology guidance.
Caution Flumazenil Reversal of isolated benzodiazepine sedation in carefully selected patients 0.2 mg IV over 15 s, then 0.1 mg every 60 s as needed (max 1 mg), if used. Avoid in mixed overdoses, seizure disorders, chronic benzo users, or co-ingestion of proconvulsant drugs. Usually specialist-only.

Senior tip: for any complex dosing (NAC, HIET, lipid, cyanide kits), use a calculator or printed protocol at the bedside – these are not drugs to “eyeball”.
