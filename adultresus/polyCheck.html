Polytrauma Checklist

Polytrauma in the Emergency Department

Think in terms of ATLS (ABCDE), haemorrhage control, and early transfer / theatre. Use this checklist to avoid missing time–critical injuries.

Step 0 – Immediate red flags

When to Call a Trauma Alert / Theatre Early

Red flags that should trigger immediate senior / trauma team involvement:
  • SBP < 90 mmHg or MAP < 65 mmHg despite fluids
  • GCS ≤ 8 or rapid neurological deterioration
  • Respiratory distress, saturations < 90% despite oxygen, or obvious chest wall instability
  • Massive external haemorrhage or pelvic instability
  • Penetrating trauma to head, neck, chest, abdomen, or pelvis
  • Need for massive transfusion (suspected major haemorrhage)

Senior tip: Don’t wait for all investigations. If your gut says “this patient looks too sick”, call for help and activate the trauma pathway.

Step 1 – Does this meet “polytrauma”?

Polytrauma Definition & Triggers Checklist

YES NO Finding Clinical Implication
Multiple injuries in ≥ 2 anatomical regions (excluding isolated limb injuries) Higher risk of occult injuries and physiological collapse – treat as polytrauma.
Estimated Injury Severity Score (ISS) > 15 (or clearly severe mechanism) Severe trauma – early referral / transfer to trauma centre recommended.
Head injury (GCS < 13, focal neurology, seizures) Consider early CT brain and neurosurgical discussion.
Spinal cord injury (motor or sensory deficit) Strict spinal precautions, early imaging, and neurosurgical/orthopaedic input.
Chest injury (flail chest, tension pneumothorax, haemothorax, pulmonary contusion) High risk of hypoxia and decompensation – chest drains, analgesia, ventilatory support.
Abdominal injury (suspected intra-abdominal haemorrhage or organ injury) FAST / CT abdomen, early surgical input; low threshold for laparotomy in unstable patients.
Pelvic injury (open or unstable fractures, pubic symphysis / SI joint diastasis) Apply pelvic binder early; consider massive haemorrhage and interventional radiology / theatre.
Extremity injury with ischaemia or compartment syndrome Limb-threatening – urgent orthopaedic / vascular input; fasciotomy if compartment syndrome.
History of anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy Consider reversal agents and early CT imaging for head / torso injuries.
Suspected or confirmed pregnancy Positioning (left lateral tilt), fetal monitoring, and obstetric consultation.

If several boxes are ticked, treat as polytrauma with a low threshold for trauma team activation and transfer to a higher-level centre if available.

Step 2 – Primary survey & resuscitation (ATLS)

Initial Management – Primary Survey

  • A – Airway with C-spine control
    • Clear airway, suction as needed, insert airway adjuncts.
    • Maintain in-line cervical spine immobilisation where indicated.
    • Prepare for early intubation if compromised GCS, airway burns, or major facial trauma.
  • B – Breathing & ventilation
    • High-flow oxygen; monitor SpO₂ and respiratory effort.
    • Look for tension pneumothorax, open pneumothorax, flail chest, haemothorax – treat immediately (needle decompression, chest drains, occlusive dressings).
  • C – Circulation with haemorrhage control
    • Control external bleeding (direct pressure, haemostatic dressings, tourniquets where appropriate).
    • Large-bore IV access; consider IO if IV difficult.
    • Draw baseline bloods: FBC, U&E, clotting, crossmatch, lactate.
    • Start warmed crystalloid judiciously; move early to blood products if ongoing shock (massive transfusion protocol if available).
    • Consider tranexamic acid (if within time window and no contra-indications).
    • Apply pelvic binder for suspected pelvic fractures.
  • D – Disability (neurological)
    • Check GCS, pupils, limb movements, blood glucose.
    • Look for lateralising signs; consider early CT brain if indicated.
  • E – Exposure & environment
    • Fully expose the patient to look for other injuries, while preventing hypothermia (warmed fluids, blankets, warm room if possible).
Step 3 – Resus room checklist & secondary survey

Secondary Management (Within the First Hour)

  • Resus room setup
    • Continuous monitoring: ECG, BP, SpO₂, respiratory rate, temperature.
    • Analgesia (IV opioids, ketamine where appropriate) – pain control improves breathing and cooperation.
    • Antiemetics, tetanus prophylaxis, and antibiotics for open fractures / penetrating injuries as per protocol.
  • Secondary survey
    • Head-to-toe examination once life-threatening issues are addressed.
    • Log-roll with spinal precautions to examine the back.
    • Check for missed wounds (scalp, perineum, axillae, skin folds).
  • Imaging strategy
    • Unstable patients: bedside CXR, pelvic X-ray, and FAST / E-FAST to identify immediate surgical pathology.
    • Stable patients: CT “pan-scan” (head/neck/chest/abdomen/pelvis) depending on resources and local practice.
    • Reassess after imaging – injuries evolve.
  • Specialist involvement
    • Trauma surgeon / general surgeon, orthopaedics, neurosurgery, anaesthetics / ICU, and obstetrics (if pregnant).
    • Consider early transfer to a higher-level trauma centre if needed and feasible.
  • Prophylaxis
    • DVT prophylaxis (mechanical initially, pharmacologic when bleeding risk acceptable).
    • Stress-ulcer prophylaxis in patients likely to require ICU / ventilatory support.
Step 4 – After the dust settles

Tertiary Survey & Ongoing Care

  • Tertiary survey (usually within 24 hours)
    • Repeat full examination and review all imaging to detect missed injuries.
    • Re-evaluate pain, function, and neuro status.
  • Rehabilitation & follow-up
    • Early involvement of physiotherapy, occupational therapy, and social work.
    • Plan outpatient follow-up with relevant surgical and rehab services.
  • Psychosocial support
    • Psychological support for patient and family (trauma, PTSD risk, grief in fatal collisions etc.).

Senior tip: Polytrauma is a team sport. Clear leadership, closed-loop communication, and structured handovers (ATMIST / SBAR) dramatically reduce errors and missed injuries.

Step 5 – Documentation & handover

Documentation & Handover Essentials

  • Record time of arrival, mechanism of injury, pre-hospital interventions, and vital trends.
  • Document primary and secondary survey findings clearly (including negatives).
  • List all procedures (lines, intubation, chest drains, reductions) with times and operators.
  • Use a structured handover (e.g. ATMIST: Age, Time, Mechanism, Injuries, Signs, Treatment).
  • Ensure copies of imaging reports and key blood results go with the patient if transferred.
