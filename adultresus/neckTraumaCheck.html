Penetrating Neck Trauma Checklist

Penetrating Neck Trauma

Focus on airway, major bleeding, and vascular/aerodigestive injury. Do not probe wounds or remove impaled objects in A&E.

Step 1 – Do they need immediate theatre?

Hard & Soft Signs of Serious Neck Injury

YES NO Finding Implication
Expanding / pulsatile haematoma Likely major vascular injury – urgent theatre / vascular consult
Massive external haemorrhage Immediate pressure + theatre, consider REBOA / damage control (major centre)
Airway compromise (stridor, gurgling, obvious airway distortion) Immediate airway control (preferably in theatre with ENT/anaesthetics)
Massive subcutaneous emphysema / air bubbling from wound Suspected laryngotracheal or pharyngo-oesophageal injury – theatre
Shock not explained by other injuries Consider major vascular injury – urgent surgical control
Neurological deficit compatible with carotid/vertebral injury High suspicion of arterial injury – urgent imaging / surgery
Non-expanding but significant haematoma Requires CT angiography and ENT/vascular review
Hoarseness, dysphonia, dysphagia, odynophagia Possible laryngeal or oesophageal injury
Haemoptysis or haematemesis Possible airway or aerodigestive tract injury
Focal neck tenderness along major vessels Consider vascular imaging
Subcutaneous emphysema limited in extent Suggests contained airway/aerodigestive injury – image & observe closely

If any hard sign is present: treat as a surgical emergency – airway, haemorrhage control and rapid theatre / transfer.

Step 2 – ED priorities

ED Priorities – What to Do Immediately

  • Airway – early senior involvement (anaesthetics / ENT / surgery).
    • Prepare for a difficult airway; avoid multiple attempts.
    • If airway is clearly threatened, aim for controlled intubation in theatre where possible.
    • If airway is lost or cannot be secured from above → emergency surgical airway (front-of-neck access).
  • Breathing
    • High-flow oxygen, monitor SpO₂ continuously.
    • Look for associated chest injuries (pneumothorax, haemothorax) and treat per ATLS.
  • Circulation
    • Direct pressure to bleeding sites; use haemostatic dressings if available.
    • Do not blindly clamp vessels (risk of nerve/tracheal/oesophageal damage).
    • Large-bore IV access, bloods, group & save / crossmatch. Activate massive transfusion if needed.
  • Cervical spine
    • Maintain manual inline stabilisation if mechanism suggests C-spine injury.
    • Rigid collar may obscure wounds – prioritise airway and haemorrhage control; use blocks/tape if needed.
Step 3 – Stable patient work-up

Imaging & Definitive Assessment (Stable Patients)

  • Haemodynamically stable and airway safe:
    • CT angiography neck +/- CT chest – evaluate carotid, vertebral, and major venous injury.
    • Consider contrast swallow / endoscopy if oesophageal injury suspected (local protocols).
    • Laryngoscopy / bronchoscopy where airway injury suspected (ENT / anaesthetics).
  • Important: Penetrating injury that breaches the platysma should generally be imaged or explored – do not treat as “minor”.
  • Document:
    • Wound location (distance from landmarks), size, direction (if known).
    • Presence of impaled objects – do not remove in ED, stabilise in situ.

Senior tip: In a stable patient with soft signs, CT angiography is usually the investigation of choice – it guides whether you can safely observe versus refer for surgery.

Step 4 – Pitfalls to avoid

Practical Do & Don’t in Penetrating Neck Trauma

  • DO
    • Resuscitate using ATLS principles (ABCDE, trauma call if available).
    • Control haemorrhage with direct pressure / packing while preparing for theatre.
    • Get early help: surgery, ENT, anaesthetics, ICU as appropriate.
    • Give tetanus prophylaxis and broad-spectrum antibiotics as per local protocol.
  • DO NOT
    • Probe wound tracks or blindly insert instruments into the wound.
    • Remove impaled objects in A&E (unless they directly obstruct the airway and you are forced to intervene).
    • Delay airway control in a deteriorating patient while waiting for imaging.
