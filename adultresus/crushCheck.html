Crush Syndrome – ED Checklist

For patients with prolonged compression, entrapment, or severe muscle injury

Aim: anticipate rhabdomyolysis and hyperkalaemia, start early fluids (ideally before release), protect kidneys, and treat life-threatening electrolyte disturbances.

High-risk in disasters, building collapses, prolonged “found down” patients, and severe limb trauma.

Key Clinical & Laboratory Features

Use this during initial assessment. Any combination of prolonged compression + dark urine + hyperkalaemia should trigger aggressive management and senior involvement.

YES NO Feature
History of prolonged compression (>1–2 hours) or being trapped
Pain, swelling, tense/firm compartment in affected limb
Restricted movement, weakness, or inability to move limb
Numbness or paraesthesia in affected area (possible compartment syndrome)
Shock (hypotension, tachycardia, cool peripheries)
Dark, tea-coloured urine (suspected myoglobinuria)
Elevated CK (rhabdomyolysis)
Rising creatinine / urea (acute kidney injury)
Hyperkalaemia on labs (K⁺ elevated)
Hypocalcaemia (low Ca²⁺) on labs
Metabolic acidosis on ABG/VBG
ECG changes of hyperkalaemia (peaked T waves, wide QRS, sine wave)

Red Flags – Treat as Time-Critical

  • ECG changes of hyperkalaemia
  • Shock, hypotension, or arrhythmia
  • Anuric/oliguric with dark urine and rising creatinine
  • Tense, painful compartments with pain out of proportion
  • Severe metabolic acidosis

If present → call senior early, start hyperkalaemia protocol, and discuss with ICU/nephrology.

Initial ED Management

  1. ABCDE & Monitoring
    • Airway: protect if decreased consciousness or severe acidosis/shock.
    • Breathing: oxygen, treat associated chest trauma.
    • Circulation: establish IV access (preferably 2 large-bore lines).
    • Attach cardiac monitor, BP, pulse oximeter; get a 12-lead ECG.
  2. Before / At Release of Compression
    • If patient is still trapped: start IV fluids before extrication if possible.
    • Have hyperkalaemia treatment and defib available at the time of release.
  3. Fluid Resuscitation
    • Begin vigorous IV isotonic saline – avoid potassium or lactate-containing fluids initially.
    • Typical starting point: 1–1.5 L bolus, then 250–500 mL/h, titrated to BP and urine output.
    • Target urine output ~1–2 mL/kg/h (if not oliguric/anuric from established AKI).
  4. Treat Hyperkalaemia Early (if present or strongly suspected)
    • ECG first; if changes or K⁺ high → start hyperkalaemia protocol (see drug quick reference).
    • Continuous cardiac monitoring during treatment.
  5. Analgesia & Limb Care
    • Give strong IV analgesia (e.g. opioids); consider ketamine in trauma setting.
    • Elevate affected limbs if possible.
    • Watch for signs of compartment syndrome and involve orthopaedics early.
  6. Investigations
    • Bloods: FBC, U&E, CK, LFTs, CRP, coagulation, VBG/ABG (lactate, pH, K⁺).
    • Urinalysis: colour, dipstick (blood without RBCs suggests myoglobin).
    • Baseline CXR if indicated; imaging for associated trauma.
  7. Nephrology / ICU Involvement
    • Early discussion if:
      • Rising creatinine / oliguria / anuria
      • Severe hyperkalaemia or acidosis
      • Haemodynamic instability despite fluids
    • Consider dialysis if AKI progresses or refractory hyperkalaemia/acidosis develops.

Practical Do & Don’t

  • DO start fluids as early as possible (ideally pre-release) and repeat ECGs / K⁺ checks.
  • DO call senior / ICU / nephrology early if there is any concern about deterioration.
  • DON’T give potassium-containing fluids in the acute phase.
  • DON’T delay treatment while “waiting for all the bloods” if hyperkalaemia is strongly suspected.
  • DON’T routinely correct asymptomatic hypocalcaemia early – it often self-corrects once rhabdomyolysis is controlled. Reserve IV calcium for hyperkalaemia with ECG changes or symptomatic hypocalcaemia.

Drug Quick Reference – Crush Syndrome (Adults)

Typical ED adult doses – always confirm with your local protocol, particularly for hyperkalaemia management.

Drug Typical Dose Notes
Normal Saline (0.9%) 1–1.5 L bolus, then 250–500 mL/h (adjust to BP & U/O) Aim to start before/at release of compression. Avoid K⁺ and lactate-containing fluids initially.
Calcium Gluconate 10% 10 mL IV over 5–10 min Cardiac membrane stabilisation in hyperkalaemia with ECG changes. Repeat as needed per protocol.
Insulin + Dextrose 10 units regular insulin IV + 50 mL 50% dextrose (or equivalent) Shifts K⁺ intracellularly. Check glucose and K⁺ regularly afterwards.
Nebulised Salbutamol 5 mg nebulised Adjunctive treatment to shift K⁺ into cells; monitor heart rate.
Sodium Bicarbonate e.g. 50 mmol IV over 30 min (per protocol) Consider in severe metabolic acidosis or hyperkalaemia; avoid causing alkalosis/volume overload.
Analgesia (e.g. Morphine) 2–5 mg IV, titrated q5–10 min Severe pain is common; titrate slowly, monitor BP and consciousness.

Senior tip: fluids and hyperkalaemia management save lives in crush – start early and reassess frequently. Dialysis is not a failure; it is a treatment.

Ongoing Monitoring

  • Vitals: BP, HR, RR, SpO₂, temperature, mental status.
  • Urine output: aim for adequate diuresis; catheterise if indicated.
  • ECG: continuous monitoring if K⁺ abnormal or at high risk.
  • Repeat U&E, CK, lactate, and acid-base status as clinically indicated.
  • Observe for evolving compartment syndrome – escalating pain, paraesthesia, pallor, paralysis.

Document times of extrication, fluids given, and key lab results clearly – this information is vital for the receiving ICU/nephrology team.
