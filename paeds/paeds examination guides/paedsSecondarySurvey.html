Trauma Secondary Survey for Pediatric Patients

The trauma secondary survey is a systematic assessment of a pediatric patient who has experienced trauma. It is performed after the primary survey, which focuses on identifying and addressing any life-threatening injuries.

General Assessment

During the general examination, carefully observe the pediatric patient's overall appearance, level of consciousness, and any visible signs of trauma. Pay close attention to the following:

  • General Appearance and Level of Consciousness: Assess the child's overall appearance, responsiveness, and level of consciousness. Look for signs of distress, agitation, lethargy, or altered mental status. Pediatric patients may display age-specific behaviors that indicate discomfort or distress.
  • Skin Examination: Perform a thorough inspection of the child's skin for any signs of trauma, including bruising, lacerations, abrasions, or contusions. Be mindful of different skin tones and consider using a systematic approach to ensure no areas are missed.
  • Head and Neck Examination: Carefully assess the head and neck for any signs of injury. Look for signs of skull fractures, facial trauma, or cervical spine injury. Palpate the scalp for tenderness, and examine the ears and eyes for any signs of blood or cerebrospinal fluid leakage.
  • Chest Examination: Examine the chest for signs of injury, such as flail chest (paradoxical movement of the chest wall), asymmetrical chest movement, or crepitus. Assess for pneumothorax and hemothorax by observing respiratory effort and percussion sounds.
  • Abdominal Examination: Evaluate the abdomen for signs of injury or tenderness. Look for distension, guarding, and rebound tenderness, which can indicate internal injuries or intra-abdominal bleeding. Palpate gently to assess for areas of pain or rigidity.
  • Pelvic Examination: Perform a gentle pelvic examination to assess for instability or pain with palpation. Suspect pelvic fractures if the patient has difficulty bearing weight, exhibits pain upon movement, or displays signs of internal bleeding.
  • Extremity Examination: Examine each extremity for signs of injury, including fractures, dislocations, and soft tissue injuries. Assess for deformities, swelling, or tenderness. In younger pediatric patients, be aware of non-ambulatory injuries, such as spiral fractures suggestive of abuse.

Neurological Assessment

Evaluate the pediatric patient's neurological status, including their level of consciousness, orientation, memory, pupillary response, and movement and sensation in all extremities. It's important to consider age-appropriate assessments and interactions to establish trust and comfort with the child:

  • Level of Consciousness, Orientation, and Memory: Assess the child's responsiveness, awareness, and memory. Determine if the child is alert, able to respond to questions appropriately, and oriented to person, place, and time. Abnormal findings may include confusion, disorientation, or unresponsiveness.
  • Pupillary Response and Size: Examine the child's pupils for their size and reaction to light. Normal pupillary response includes constriction when exposed to bright light and dilation when light is removed. Anisocoria (unequal pupil size) or abnormal reactions could indicate neurological dysfunction.
  • Movement and Sensation in All Extremities: Evaluate the child's motor function and sensory responses in all extremities. Ask the child to move their arms and legs, and assess for symmetry, strength, and coordination. Abnormal findings might include weakness, paralysis, or involuntary movements.
  • Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) Score: Use an appropriate pediatric GCS assessment tool to evaluate the child's neurological function. The GCS assesses eye opening, verbal response, and motor response. Keep in mind that GCS scores can vary based on age and developmental stage:

Apgar Scoring for Newborns

The Apgar score is a quick and simple assessment tool used to evaluate the overall health and well-being of a newborn immediately after birth. It provides a snapshot of the newborn's condition and helps healthcare providers determine if any immediate interventions are necessary.

Components of the Apgar Score:

The Apgar score is calculated based on five key components, each assigned a score of 0, 1, or 2:

  • A: Appearance (Skin Color) - Assess the newborn's skin color. A score of 0 is given for blue or pale skin, 1 for body pink and extremities blue, and 2 for completely pink skin.
  • P: Pulse (Heart Rate) - Check the newborn's heart rate. A score of 0 is given for absent pulse, 1 for a heart rate below 100 bpm, and 2 for a heart rate above 100 bpm.
  • G: Grimace (Reflexes) - Observe the newborn's response to stimulation, such as a gentle pinch. A score of 0 is given for no response, 1 for minimal response or grimace, and 2 for prompt response with a sneeze, cough, or vigorous cry.
  • A: Activity (Muscle Tone) - Evaluate the newborn's muscle tone. A score of 0 is given for limp and flaccid muscles, 1 for some flexion of limbs, and 2 for active motion and flexion.
  • R: Respiration (Breathing) - Note the newborn's breathing pattern. A score of 0 is given for absent breathing, 1 for slow or irregular breathing, and 2 for strong and vigorous crying or breathing.

Interpretation of Apgar Scores:

The scores for each component are added together to give a total Apgar score ranging from 0 to 10. The Apgar score is typically assessed at 1 and 5 minutes after birth. An Apgar score of 7 to 10 indicates that the newborn is in good condition, while a score of 4 to 6 suggests that some medical assistance may be needed. A score of 3 or below indicates that the newborn requires immediate medical attention.

Importance of the Apgar Score:

The Apgar score is a valuable tool for healthcare providers to quickly assess a newborn's condition and determine if resuscitation or other interventions are necessary. It aids in communication between healthcare team members and helps guide initial care for the newborn.

Additional Assessments and Tests

In addition to the general and neurological assessments, the trauma secondary survey for pediatric patients may involve various tests and diagnostic tools to further evaluate the child's condition and identify potential injuries:

  • Diagnostic imaging, such as X-rays, CT scans, and ultrasound, to visualize internal injuries and fractures
  • Age-appropriate lab tests, including complete blood count (CBC), blood typing, coagulation studies, and arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis, to assess the child's overall health and identify potential complications
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) or heart rate monitoring to evaluate the child's heart function and identify any cardiac abnormalities
  • Age-appropriate assessments such as urinalysis to screen for urinary tract injuries, infection, or other issues
  • Continuous monitoring of vital signs to track the child's response to interventions and detect any changes in their condition

Documentation and Ongoing Monitoring

Thoroughly document all findings from the trauma secondary survey in the pediatric patient's medical record. Accurate and detailed documentation is crucial for maintaining a complete record of the child's condition, treatment, and progress. Utilize age-appropriate assessment tools and consider involving the child's parents or guardians in the documentation process, ensuring they are informed and engaged in their child's care.

After completing the secondary survey, closely monitor the pediatric patient's condition, and continue to reevaluate them as needed. Any changes in the child's condition should be promptly communicated to the healthcare team, and the treatment plan should be adjusted accordingly.

Summary

The trauma secondary survey is a critical step in evaluating and managing pediatric patients who have experienced trauma. By systematically assessing general and neurological aspects, as well as using appropriate tests and monitoring techniques, healthcare providers can identify injuries, monitor the child's condition, and maintain accurate documentation. Always remember that the secondary survey is conducted after addressing life-threatening injuries during the primary survey.
