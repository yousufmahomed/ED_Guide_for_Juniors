Primary Survey of a Pediatric Emergency Patient

Introduction

The primary survey is the initial assessment of an emergency pediatric patient, following the ABCDE approach tailored to children:

  • A: Airway
  • B: Breathing
  • C: Circulation
  • D: Disability
  • E: Exposure/Environment

Airway

Clinical features: Stridor, retractions, nasal flaring, inability to vocalize

Symptoms: Shortness of breath, difficulty speaking, drooling

Causes: Airway obstruction, foreign body, infection

Relevant interventions: Head tilt-chin lift, use of appropriate airway adjuncts, suctioning

Airway Intubation for Pediatric Patients

Intubation in pediatric patients is a critical intervention to secure the airway and provide mechanical ventilation when needed. Pediatric airways have unique anatomical and physiological considerations that require specialized techniques.

Indications for Intubation:

  • Severe respiratory distress with impending failure
  • Apnea or impending respiratory failure
  • Protecting the airway in unconscious or sedated patients
  • Facilitating mechanical ventilation

Confirmatory Tests for Placement:

  • End-tidal CO2 monitoring
  • Chest X-ray
  • Direct laryngoscopy
  • Bronchoscopy
  • Auscultation
  • Chest rise and fall
  • Pulse oximetry
  • Capnography
  • Ultrasound

Reasons for Desaturation:

  • Airway obstruction
  • Hypoventilation
  • Inadequate oxygenation or ventilation
  • Pneumothorax
  • Aspiration
  • Tube dislodgement
  • Tension pneumothorax
  • Equipment malfunction

Intubation in pediatric patients should be performed by experienced healthcare providers who are familiar with the size-appropriate equipment and techniques. Adequate sedation, positioning, and monitoring are essential to ensure patient comfort and safety during the procedure.

Breathing

In pediatric patients, assessing breathing is crucial to evaluate their respiratory status and ensure proper oxygenation. Children's respiratory systems are particularly vulnerable, and signs of respiratory distress can be indicative of various conditions that warrant prompt attention.

Clinical Features:

  • Tachypnea: Rapid breathing is a common sign of respiratory distress in children and can indicate underlying lung or cardiac issues.
  • Grunting: Grunting during expiration is a protective mechanism in children with respiratory difficulties, attempting to maintain positive end-expiratory pressure.
  • Nasal Flaring: Widening of the nostrils during breathing indicates increased work of breathing and potential respiratory distress.
  • Intercostal Retractions: Visible sinking of the tissues between the ribs during inspiration signals increased respiratory effort.

Symptoms:

  • Shortness of Breath: Children may not be able to express this symptom verbally, but they might exhibit restlessness, agitation, or discomfort.
  • Wheezing: High-pitched whistling sounds on expiration can indicate conditions such as asthma or bronchiolitis.
  • Coughing: Persistent or severe coughing suggests airway irritation or infection.
  • Chest Discomfort: Children might display signs of discomfort, such as clutching their chest or avoiding certain positions.

Causes:

Respiratory issues in children can arise from various conditions, including:

  • Respiratory Distress: Conditions such as pneumonia, bronchiolitis, or severe allergic reactions can lead to difficulty breathing.
  • Pneumonia: Inflammation of lung tissue can cause breathing difficulties and may result from bacterial or viral infections.
  • Asthma: An inflammatory condition of the airways can lead to recurrent episodes of wheezing and shortness of breath.
  • Bronchiolitis: Viral infection of the small airways can cause respiratory distress in infants and young children.

Relevant Interventions:

Managing breathing difficulties in pediatric patients involves:

  • Oxygen Administration: Supplemental oxygen is provided to maintain adequate oxygen saturation levels.
  • Assisted Ventilation: In severe cases of respiratory distress, assisted ventilation using bag-valve-mask devices or mechanical ventilators may be necessary.
  • Continuous Monitoring: Regular monitoring of respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, and general appearance helps assess treatment effectiveness and detect any deterioration.

Circulation

The assessment of circulation in pediatric patients is crucial for detecting signs of inadequate blood flow and ensuring proper cardiovascular function. Pediatric patients can experience rapid physiological changes, making it essential to promptly identify and address circulatory issues.

Clinical Features:

  • Tachycardia: An elevated heart rate can indicate increased cardiovascular demand, stress, or inadequate oxygen delivery.
  • Weak Peripheral Pulses: Diminished or weak peripheral pulses might suggest decreased cardiac output.
  • Delayed Capillary Refill: Prolonged capillary refill time can be indicative of poor peripheral perfusion.

Symptoms:

  • Fatigue: Children with compromised circulation may exhibit fatigue, weakness, and difficulty maintaining normal activity levels.
  • Dizziness: Older children might complain of dizziness or feeling lightheaded.
  • Altered Level of Consciousness: Severe circulatory compromise can lead to altered mental status.

Causes:

Circulatory issues in pediatric patients can result from a variety of factors:

  • Dehydration: Insufficient fluid intake or excessive fluid loss can lead to reduced blood volume and circulatory compromise.
  • Shock: Hypovolemic, septic, or cardiogenic shock can result from various causes and lead to poor tissue perfusion.
  • Sepsis: Widespread infection can trigger systemic inflammatory responses that affect circulation.
  • Arrhythmia: Irregular heart rhythms can impact cardiac output and circulatory function.

Relevant Interventions:

Effective management of circulatory issues in pediatric patients involves:

  • Fluid Resuscitation Tailored to Weight and Clinical Status: Administering appropriate fluids to restore intravascular volume and perfusion.
  • Inotropic Support if Needed: In cases of severe circulatory compromise, medications may be used to enhance cardiac contractility and improve perfusion.

Disability

Assessing the neurological status of pediatric patients is crucial to detect any changes in their mental state or neurological function. Pediatric patients may have unique responses to neurological insults, and prompt assessment can help identify potential issues that require immediate intervention.

Clinical Features:

  • Altered Mental Status: Changes in consciousness, confusion, lethargy, or unresponsiveness are critical signs that warrant immediate attention.
  • Focal Neurologic Deficits: Symptoms such as weakness on one side of the body, slurred speech, or vision changes can suggest localized neurological issues.
  • Pupil Abnormalities: Unequal or unreactive pupils can indicate neurologic dysfunction.

Symptoms:

  • Headache: Children who can express themselves may complain of headaches, which could be a sign of increased intracranial pressure.
  • Weakness: Altered motor function or inability to move limbs normally may be indicative of neurological problems.
  • Numbness and Tingling: Sensory disturbances can also signify neurological issues.

Causes:

Neurological issues in pediatric patients can result from various causes:

  • Trauma: Head injuries or concussions can lead to altered mental status or focal neurological deficits.
  • Stroke: In rare cases, stroke can occur in pediatric patients, leading to neurological symptoms.
  • Seizure: Seizures can result in altered mental status and focal neurological symptoms during or after the episode.
  • Drug Overdose: Certain medications or substances can lead to altered neurological function.

Relevant Interventions:

Assessing and managing neurological issues in pediatric patients involves:

  • Glasgow Coma Scale Assessment: This standardized tool evaluates eye, verbal, and motor responses to assess neurological status.
  • Neurological Exam: Assessing motor strength, reflexes, and sensory function to identify focal deficits.
  • Treatment of Underlying Cause: Treating the primary issue, such as addressing trauma or seizures.

Exposure/Environment

Assessing the exposure and environment of pediatric patients is essential to ensure their comfort, safety, and protection from adverse conditions. Children's vulnerability to temperature changes and environmental hazards requires careful evaluation and appropriate interventions.

Clinical Features:

  • Hypothermia: Cold exposure can lead to hypothermia, causing shivering and potentially affecting vital signs.
  • Flushed Skin: Skin that is red or flushed may indicate fever, heat exposure, or an allergic reaction.
  • Rash: Skin rashes can indicate various underlying conditions, including infections or allergic reactions.
  • Signs of Trauma: Children may present with signs of recent or ongoing trauma that require immediate attention.

Symptoms:

  • Shivering: Children may exhibit shivering as a response to cold temperatures.
  • Discomfort: Uncomfortable expressions, restlessness, or agitation may indicate exposure-related discomfort.
  • Pain: Children may show signs of pain or distress due to environmental factors.

Causes:

Environmental issues in pediatric patients can result from a range of factors:

  • Environmental Exposure: Extremes of temperature, exposure to inclement weather, or inadequate clothing can lead to issues.
  • Burns: Thermal, chemical, or electrical burns can result in skin injuries.
  • Trauma: Accidents or injuries can lead to exposure-related issues.

Relevant Interventions:

Managing exposure and environmental issues in pediatric patients includes:

  • Temperature Management: Providing appropriate clothing, blankets, or temperature control measures to prevent hypothermia or overheating.
  • Wound Care: Treating burns, abrasions, or other skin injuries to prevent infection and promote healing.
  • Protection from Further Exposure: Ensuring the patient is sheltered from adverse environmental conditions and potential hazards.
