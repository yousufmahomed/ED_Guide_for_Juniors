Pediatric History Taking in the ED

Main Complaint

Start by asking the parent/guardian about the reason for the visit. Encourage them to provide as much information as possible, including the duration of the problem and any aggravating or relieving factors. It is important to document the patient's own words.

  • Ask open-ended questions to elicit additional information
  • Use age-appropriate language and communication strategies
  • Consider cultural and social factors that may impact the patient's presentation

History of Present Illness

Ask about the details of the current problem, including:

  • Onset
    • Was the problem sudden or gradual?
  • Location and radiation
    • Ask the parent/guardian to point to the affected area, if possible
  • Duration of symptoms
    • When did the symptoms start?
    • Has the problem been continuous or intermittent?
    • Have the symptoms improved or worsened over time?
  • Severity
    • How severe is the problem?
    • Has the severity changed over time?
    • Is the problem interfering with the child's daily activities?
  • Associated symptoms
    • Are there any other symptoms present?
    • What makes the symptoms worse?
    • What makes the symptoms better?

Past Medical History

Ask about the child's past medical history, including:

  • Chronic illnesses
  • Previous surgeries or hospitalizations
  • Immunizations
  • Developmental milestones
  • Birth history

Be sure to ask about any medications the child is currently taking, as well as any medications they have taken in the past.

Family History

Ask about the child's family medical history, including:

  • Chronic illnesses in parents or siblings
  • Birth defects
  • Genetic disorders
  • History of sudden infant death syndrome (SIDS)
  • Any history of early childhood deaths in the family

It may be helpful to ask about the ethnicity and ancestry of the child and their family, as certain genetic disorders are more prevalent in specific populations.

Social History

Ask about the child's social history, including:

  • Living situation
  • Caregivers
  • Education and daycare
  • Nutrition and feeding habits
  • Exercise and activity level
  • Screen time
  • Substance use or exposure

Be sure to ask about any recent changes in the child's routine or environment that may be contributing to their current problem.

Review of Systems

Ask about any additional symptoms or problems the child may be experiencing, including:

  • Fever
  • Weight loss or gain
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Diarrhea or constipation
  • Headache
  • Cough or difficulty breathing
  • Ear pain or discharge
  • Eye redness or discharge
  • Skin rash or lesions
  • Joint pain or stiffness
  • Urinary symptoms
  • Behavioral or emotional changes

Summary and Plan

Summarize the information you have gathered and discuss your plan with the parent/guardian.

  • Discuss any necessary tests or procedures
  • Explain the rationale behind any treatments or medications
  • Provide clear instructions for follow-up care
  • Address any concerns or questions the parent/guardian may have

Remember to always communicate clearly and compassionately with the child and their parent/guardian throughout the history-taking process.

Here are some additional tips to keep in mind:

  • Use age-appropriate language when speaking with children
  • Ask open-ended questions to encourage the parent/guardian to provide more detailed information
  • Take the time to establish rapport with the child and their parent/guardian
  • Be aware of any cultural or religious considerations that may impact the history-taking process
  • Always prioritize the safety and well-being of the child

Remember that history-taking is a crucial component of the diagnostic process and can greatly impact the quality of care the child receives. By following a structured approach and using good communication skills, you can gather the information you need to provide the best possible care.
