Pediatric Abdominal Exam

Introduction

An abdominal examination is an important part of the physical exam for pediatric patients presenting with abdominal pain or other gastrointestinal symptoms. This exam involves a systematic approach to inspecting, auscultating, palpating, and percussing the pediatric patient's abdomen to assess for any abnormalities or signs of underlying disease.

Preparation

Before beginning the examination, ensure that the patient is comfortable and properly positioned. The patient should be lying flat on their back with their arms at their sides and their legs extended. It may be necessary to provide a pillow or blanket for support or warmth. Wash your hands thoroughly and put on gloves before beginning the examination.

Inspection

Begin the examination by visually inspecting the patient's abdomen. Look for any visible abnormalities such as scars, hernias, or distention. Observe the patient's respiratory rate and pattern as well, as this can provide clues to underlying abdominal pathology.

Auscultation

Use a stethoscope to listen to the patient's bowel sounds. The bowel sounds should be audible in all four quadrants of the abdomen and should be regular and normal in tone. Absent or hyperactive bowel sounds may indicate intestinal obstruction or ileus.

Palpation

Palpate the patient's abdomen for any areas of tenderness, masses, or abnormal organ enlargement. Use a light touch to assess for surface tenderness, then gradually increase pressure to assess for deeper tenderness. It may be necessary to perform a deep palpation to assess for any masses or organ enlargement.

During palpation, also assess for any signs of guarding, rigidity, or rebound tenderness, as these may indicate peritonitis or other intra-abdominal pathology. Additionally, assess for the presence of a hernia by performing a cough test or asking the patient to perform a Valsalva maneuver.

Percussion

Percuss the patient's abdomen to assess for areas of dullness or tympany. Dullness may indicate organ enlargement, fluid accumulation, or a mass, while tympany may indicate the presence of gas in the intestines.

Differential Diagnosis for Pediatric Abdominal Pain

The location of abdominal pain can provide clues to the underlying cause of the pain in pediatric patients. Here are some common differential diagnoses for pain in different regions of the pediatric abdomen:

Right Upper Quadrant

Abdominal pain in the right upper quadrant of pediatric patients can be indicative of various conditions. Some common pediatric-specific conditions to consider include:

  • Gallstones or cholecystitis:

    • Gallstones can cause pain when they block the bile ducts or cause inflammation in the gallbladder (cholecystitis). Pediatric patients with gallstones may experience episodes of sharp, crampy pain in the right upper quadrant that may radiate to the back or shoulder. Nausea, vomiting, and a bloated feeling might also be present.

  • Hepatitis:

    • Hepatitis is inflammation of the liver. In pediatric patients, viral hepatitis (such as hepatitis A, B, or C) can cause symptoms like jaundice (yellowing of the skin and eyes), dark urine, pale stools, fatigue, and abdominal discomfort. Depending on the specific virus, transmission routes and clinical features may vary.

  • Pancreatitis:

    • Pancreatitis is inflammation of the pancreas. Pediatric patients with pancreatitis might experience severe abdominal pain that radiates to the back. Other symptoms may include nausea, vomiting, and tenderness in the upper abdomen. Pancreatitis can be caused by various factors, including gallstones or certain medications.

  • Alcoholic hepatitis:

    • While uncommon in pediatric patients, alcoholic hepatitis can occur due to heavy alcohol consumption. Symptoms might include jaundice, hepatomegaly, abdominal pain, and fever.

  • Nonalcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD):

    • NAFLD refers to the accumulation of fat in the liver not caused by alcohol. It is increasingly recognized in pediatric patients, often associated with obesity and metabolic syndrome. Elevated liver enzymes, hepatomegaly, and fatigue might be observed.

  • Autoimmune hepatitis:

    • In autoimmune hepatitis, the immune system mistakenly attacks the liver. Pediatric patients might present with symptoms like jaundice, fatigue, abdominal pain, and joint pain. Laboratory tests might show elevated liver enzymes and autoantibodies.

Left Upper Quadrant

Abdominal pain in the left upper quadrant of pediatric patients can be associated with a range of conditions. Some pediatric-specific conditions to consider in this area include:

  • Splenic conditions:

    • Spleen-related issues in pediatric patients can include splenomegaly (enlarged spleen) due to infections like infectious mononucleosis or underlying conditions like sickle cell disease. Enlarged spleen might cause discomfort or tenderness in the left upper quadrant. In some cases, splenic rupture might occur due to trauma or other causes, leading to severe abdominal pain and potentially requiring immediate medical attention.

  • Gastritis or peptic ulcer disease:

    • Gastritis and peptic ulcer disease can cause upper abdominal pain that may be felt in the left upper quadrant. In pediatric patients, factors like infection with Helicobacter pylori or nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drug (NSAID) use might contribute to these conditions. Symptoms might include burning pain, nausea, vomiting, and occasionally, bleeding leading to dark stools or vomiting blood (hematemesis).

  • Spleen-related infections:

    • Infections such as bacterial infections, viral infections, or parasitic infections can affect the spleen in pediatric patients. Symptoms might include fever, fatigue, and left upper quadrant pain. Different infections may have varying clinical presentations and treatment approaches.

  • Left lower lobe pneumonia:

    • Pneumonia involving the left lower lobe of the lung can cause referred pain to the left upper quadrant. Pediatric patients with pneumonia might have symptoms like cough, fever, difficulty breathing, and in some cases, abdominal pain.

  • Left-sided kidney conditions:

    • Conditions affecting the left kidney, such as kidney infections or stones, can sometimes cause pain in the left upper quadrant. In pediatric patients, kidney infections might cause fever, abdominal pain, and urinary symptoms like urgency and frequency.

Right Lower Quadrant

Abdominal pain in the right lower quadrant of pediatric patients can be associated with a variety of conditions. Some pediatric-specific conditions to consider in this area include:

  • Appendicitis:

    • Appendicitis is a common cause of right lower quadrant pain in pediatric patients. Symptoms often include abdominal pain starting around the belly button and migrating to the right lower quadrant, along with nausea, vomiting, fever, and localized tenderness.

  • Diverticulitis:

    • While less common in pediatric patients, diverticulitis can cause right lower quadrant pain. It may be associated with fever, abdominal tenderness, and changes in bowel habits.

  • Salpingitis or ovarian cyst:

    • Gynecological conditions like salpingitis (inflammation of the fallopian tubes) or ovarian cysts can cause lower abdominal pain in the right quadrant in adolescent females. Symptoms may include pelvic pain, fever, and abnormal menstrual bleeding.

  • Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis:

    • Inflammatory bowel diseases like Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis can present with abdominal pain, especially in the right lower quadrant. Pediatric patients might also experience diarrhea, weight loss, and fatigue.

  • Right lower lobe pneumonia:

    • Pneumonia affecting the right lower lobe of the lung can cause referred pain to the right lower quadrant. Pediatric patients with pneumonia might have symptoms like cough, fever, and abdominal pain.

Left Lower Quadrant

Abdominal pain in the left lower quadrant of pediatric patients can be associated with various conditions. Some pediatric-specific conditions to consider in this area include:

  • Diverticulitis:

    • Though less common in pediatric patients, diverticulitis can cause left lower quadrant pain. It may be associated with fever, abdominal tenderness, and changes in bowel habits.

  • Salpingitis or ovarian cyst:

    • Gynecological conditions like salpingitis (inflammation of the fallopian tubes) or ovarian cysts can cause lower abdominal pain in the left quadrant in adolescent females. Symptoms may include pelvic pain, fever, and abnormal menstrual bleeding.

  • Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis:

    • Inflammatory bowel diseases like Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis can present with abdominal pain, especially in the left lower quadrant. Pediatric patients might also experience diarrhea, weight loss, and fatigue.

  • Left lower lobe pneumonia:

    • Pneumonia affecting the left lower lobe of the lung can cause referred pain to the left lower quadrant. Pediatric patients with pneumonia might have symptoms like cough, fever, and abdominal pain.

  • Renal or ureteral conditions:

    • Conditions affecting the left kidney or ureter, such as infections or stones, can cause pain in the left lower quadrant. Pediatric patients might have symptoms like fever, abdominal pain, and urinary symptoms.

Appendicitis

Clinical Feature Description
Abdominal pain Pain typically begins around the belly button and migrates to the lower right quadrant of the abdomen.
Nausea and vomiting Patients may experience nausea and vomiting in response to the pain and inflammation associated with appendicitis.
Loss of appetite Patients may experience a decreased desire to eat due to the abdominal pain and discomfort associated with appendicitis.
Fever A low-grade fever is often present in patients with appendicitis.
Tenderness and rigidity Physical examination may reveal tenderness and rigidity in the lower right quadrant of the abdomen, particularly when pressure is applied.
Rebound tenderness Patients may experience pain when pressure is released from the lower right quadrant of the abdomen, a phenomenon known as rebound tenderness.
Guarding Patients may involuntarily tense the muscles of the abdomen in response to pain and inflammation, a phenomenon known as guarding.
Diarrhea or constipation Changes in bowel movements may occur in patients with appendicitis due to inflammation and obstruction of the appendix.
Malaise and fatigue Patients may experience a general feeling of illness, fatigue, and malaise in response to the inflammation and infection associated with appendicitis.

Conclusion

An abdominal examination is a crucial aspect of assessing pediatric patients with abdominal symptoms. By employing a systematic approach, healthcare providers can effectively diagnose and manage pediatric abdominal conditions, ensuring the well-being of young patients.
