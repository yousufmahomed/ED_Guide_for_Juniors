Amniotic Fluid Embolus

Amniotic fluid embolus is the release of amniotic fluid into maternal circulation, often during labor or uterine manipulation. This rare event can have severe consequences for the mother.

Foundations

  • Amniotic fluid embolus can occur during labor, induced abortions, miscarriages, second/third trimesters, amniocentesis, or abruptio placentae.
  • It is a rare but leading cause of cardiovascular collapse during labor, with high maternal mortality.

Clinical Features

  • Suspect amniotic fluid embolus during the second/third trimester, especially with uterine manipulation.
  • Key clinical features include sudden hypotension, hypoxia, and coagulopathy due to an immunologic response.
  • Triggers for this condition include histamine, endothelin, leukotrienes, which lead to DIC, respiratory distress, and ventricular dysfunction.
  • Approximately 20% of cases present with an initial seizure, while 50% develop a bleeding diathesis and DIC.

Differential Diagnoses

  • Consider other conditions like pulmonary embolus, drug-induced anaphylaxis, or septic shock.
  • Eclampsia also presents with seizures, but it is associated with hypertension, not cardiovascular collapse.

Diagnostic Testing

  • Diagnostic testing involves obtaining a complete blood cell count, coagulation studies, arterial blood gas, and chest radiograph.
  • Urine output should be monitored after urinary catheter placement.
  • Definitive diagnosis is often made at autopsy, where fetal hairs, squamous cells, and debris in maternal circulation can be detected.
  • The clinical syndrome matches the typical diagnosis along with characteristic findings.

Management

  • Management is based on anecdotal evidence and animal studies due to the rarity of amniotic fluid embolus.
  • Initial steps include administering high-flow oxygen and intubation for ventilation/oxygenation support.
  • Aggressive fluid resuscitation and inotropic cardiovascular support are essential.
  • Managing consumptive coagulopathy is a critical component of treatment.
  • Invasive hemodynamic monitoring is often required in the intensive care unit.

Amniotic fluid embolus is a rare but life-threatening event during pregnancy, leading to rapid cardiovascular collapse. Recognizing the clinical features and promptly providing supportive measures is crucial for improving maternal outcomes.
