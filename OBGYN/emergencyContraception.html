Emergency Contraception

Introduction

Emergency contraception, often referred to as the "morning-after pill," is a time-sensitive medical therapy designed to prevent pregnancy following unprotected or inadequately protected sexual intercourse. It includes various oral formulations and the copper intrauterine device (IUD).

Available Options

  • Three oral formulations: ulipristal acetate, levonorgestrel, and combined oral contraceptives.
  • Copper IUD, which inhibits sperm function, fertilized egg transport, and implantation.

Levonorgestrel

  • Most common regimen: single dose of 1.5 mg or two doses of 0.75 mg spaced 12 hours apart.
  • One-time dose of 1.5 mg recommended due to simplicity and effectiveness.
  • Effectiveness up to 72 hours after intercourse.
  • Consider potential decreased effectiveness in obese individuals with higher BMI.

Ulipristal Acetate

  • Single prescription tablet of 30 mg.
  • Effective up to 120 hours after intercourse.
  • Preferred choice beyond the 72-hour window.
  • Maximal effectiveness within 24 hours.

Combined Oral Contraceptives

  • Consisting of 100 Î¼g ethinyl estradiol and 0.5 to 1.0 mg levonorgestrel.
  • Less favored due to simplicity and success of levonorgestrel alone.

Adverse Effects

  • Common adverse effects include nausea and headache.
  • Combined oral contraceptive regimen may cause higher rates of nausea.
  • Irregular menstrual bleeding can occur but resolves without intervention.

Copper IUD

  • Highly effective when placed within 5 days of intercourse, possibly effective up to 10 days.
  • Carries a risk of uterine perforation but provides ongoing contraceptive benefit.
  • Barrier: Requires clinic appointment or visit with a qualified provider within the specified timeframe.

Mechanisms of Action

  • Levonorgestrel and ulipristal delay or inhibit ovulation.
  • Copper IUD prevents fertilization.
  • Emergency contraception is not equivalent to medical abortion and does not terminate preexisting pregnancies.

Follow-Up and Alternative Contraception

  • Emergency contraception does not affect an established pregnancy.
  • Patients should use alternative contraception and consider a pregnancy test if menstruation is delayed for over 3 weeks.

Patient Counseling

  • Educate patients on the available options, their mechanisms of action, and the importance of timely use.
  • Encourage consideration of the copper IUD for ongoing contraceptive benefit.

Multidisciplinary Collaboration

Collaborate with gynecologists and healthcare providers to ensure timely access to emergency contraception.
