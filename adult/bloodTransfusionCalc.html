Blood Transfusion Reactions

Overview: Blood transfusion reactions can vary in severity and require prompt recognition and management. This section provides a guide to identify and manage different types of reactions.

Types of Reactions

  • Acute Hemolytic: Caused by ABO incompatibility. Symptoms include fever, back pain, and hemoglobinuria.
  • Allergic: Mild to severe allergic responses, including anaphylaxis.
  • Febrile Non-Hemolytic: Most common, characterized by fever and chills.

Severity Classification

Reactions are classified from mild (Class I) to severe (Class IV), based on symptoms and clinical impact.

Management Protocols

Immediate Actions: Stop the transfusion, maintain IV access, and monitor vital signs.

Treatment: The management of transfusion reactions is dependent on the type and severity of the reaction.

  • For Mild Allergic Reactions: Administration of antihistamines such as diphenhydramine can alleviate symptoms like itching and hives. Observation and supportive care are also essential.
  • For Anaphylactic Reactions: Immediate administration of epinephrine is critical, followed by antihistamines and corticosteroids. Airway management may be necessary in severe cases.
  • For Acute Hemolytic Reactions: This is a medical emergency. Stop the transfusion immediately, maintain intravenous access with saline, and initiate high-flow oxygen. Diuretics may be administered to prevent acute kidney injury. Blood samples should be rechecked for compatibility, and further blood products should be withheld until the cause is determined.
  • For Febrile Non-Hemolytic Reactions: Antipyretics like acetaminophen can be used to manage fever. Monitoring and supportive care are recommended.
  • For Transfusion-Related Acute Lung Injury (TRALI): Supportive care, including oxygen therapy and mechanical ventilation, may be required. Corticosteroids can be considered, although their effectiveness is not well established.
  • For Transfusion-Associated Circulatory Overload (TACO): The patient should be positioned upright, and diuretics can be administered. Oxygen support and monitoring of cardiac function are important.
In all cases, immediate cessation of the transfusion and clinical assessment are crucial first steps. Further treatment should be guided by the specific symptoms and severity of the reaction.

Reporting: All reactions should be reported to the blood bank and documented for future reference.

Platelet Transfusion

Indications: Used in patients with thrombocytopenia or platelet dysfunction. Commonly indicated for counts <10x10^9/L in asymptomatic patients, <20x10^9/L in febrile patients, or <50x10^9/L in actively bleeding patients or pre-surgery.

Precautions: Ensure correct blood type match, monitor for transfusion reactions. Use cautiously in patients with a history of transfusion reactions.

Administration: Administered intravenously. Dosage and rate depend on the patient's condition and platelet count.

Note: Administer only under senior authorization.

Fresh Frozen Plasma (FFP)

Indications: Used to treat bleeding caused by clotting factor deficiencies, in liver disease, massive transfusions, or warfarin reversal.

Precautions: Risk of volume overload, allergic reactions, and transfusion-related acute lung injury (TRALI). Monitor patient closely.

Administration: Administered intravenously. Dosage based on patient’s weight and clinical condition.

Note: Administer only under senior authorization.

Cryoprecipitate Transfusion

Indications: Used for managing bleeding in patients with low fibrinogen levels, Hemophilia A, von Willebrand disease, and DIC.

Precautions: Risk of viral transmission, allergic reactions, and TRALI. Appropriate cross-matching is required.

Administration: Administered intravenously. Dosage depends on fibrinogen level and patient’s clinical condition.

Note: Administer only under senior authorization.

Factor 8 or 9 for Hemophiliac Patients

Indications: Used in patients with Hemophilia A (Factor VIII deficiency) or Hemophilia B (Factor IX deficiency) for bleeding episodes or prophylaxis.

Precautions: Monitor for development of inhibitors, allergic reactions, and thrombotic events.

Administration: Administered intravenously. Dosage depends on the severity of deficiency, extent of bleeding, and patient’s body weight.

Note: Administer only under senior authorization.
