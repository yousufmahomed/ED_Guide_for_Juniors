Primary Survey

Primary Survey of the Emergency Patient

Introduction

The primary survey is the first, rapid assessment to identify and treat immediately life-threatening problems. It follows the standard ABCDE approach:

  • A – Airway with cervical spine protection
  • B – Breathing and ventilation
  • C – Circulation with haemorrhage control
  • D – Disability (neurological status)
  • E – Exposure / Environment (undress but prevent hypothermia)

Treat life-threatening problems as you find them. Do not wait to “finish” the survey before intervening.

A – Airway

Clinical features of airway compromise: inability to speak in full sentences, stridor, gurgling, snoring, use of accessory muscles, cyanosis.

Symptoms: shortness of breath, sensation of obstruction, hoarseness, choking.

Common causes: foreign body, trauma (facial/neck), decreased level of consciousness, anaphylaxis, infection (epiglottitis, abscess), inhalation injury.

Immediate interventions:

  • Manual manoeuvres: head tilt–chin lift or jaw thrust (if C-spine concern, jaw thrust only).
  • Suction of secretions or vomitus.
  • Airway adjuncts: oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway if appropriate.
  • Prepare for definitive airway (intubation) if ongoing compromise.

Apnoea or impending respiratory failure (rising CO2, tiring patient). ETCO2, chest X-ray (tip 3–5 cm above carina). Hypoventilation, incorrect tube depth (mainstem or oesophageal).
Need to protect airway in decreased GCS (e.g. GCS ≤ 8, major head injury, overdose). Direct visualisation of tube between vocal cords at intubation. Pneumothorax / tension pneumothorax, tube dislodgement, kinked or blocked tube.
To facilitate mechanical ventilation or control of oxygenation/ventilation in severe illness. Auscultation (bilateral breath sounds, no gastric sounds), SpO2 trend. Equipment failure (oxygen supply, ventilator), inadequate pre-oxygenation.

Always have a clear back-up plan (e.g. supraglottic airway, emergency surgical airway) before attempting intubation in a difficult airway.

B – Breathing

Clinical features: tachypnoea or bradypnoea, shallow or laboured respirations, asymmetrical chest movement, use of accessory muscles, audible wheeze/stridor, cyanosis.

Symptoms: shortness of breath, chest pain, tight chest, cough, inability to speak full sentences.

Common causes: asthma/COPD, pneumonia, pneumothorax, flail chest, pulmonary embolism, pulmonary oedema.

Key interventions:

  • High-flow oxygen (or titrated O2 if CO2 retainers).
  • Bag-valve-mask ventilation if inadequate respirations.
  • Needle decompression or chest drain for tension/simple pneumothorax as indicated.
  • Nebulised bronchodilators, steroids and other condition-specific treatments.

C – Circulation

Clinical features: tachycardia or bradycardia, hypotension, weak or thready pulses, cool clammy peripheries, delayed capillary refill, obvious external bleeding.

Symptoms: weakness, dizziness or syncope, chest pain, shortness of breath, altered mental state.

Common causes: hypovolaemia (haemorrhage, dehydration), cardiogenic shock (MI, arrhythmia), obstructive shock (tamponade, PE, tension pneumothorax), distributive shock (sepsis, anaphylaxis).

Key interventions:

  • Control external haemorrhage (direct pressure, tourniquet where appropriate).
  • Establish IV/IO access; send urgent bloods including cross-match.
  • Fluid resuscitation (balanced crystalloids; blood products if haemorrhagic shock).
  • Condition-specific therapy: vasopressors, inotropes, antiarrhythmics, antibiotics, etc.

D – Disability (Neurological)

Clinical features: altered level of consciousness, confusion, agitation, seizures, abnormal pupil size or reactivity, focal neuro-deficits.

Symptoms: headache, limb weakness, numbness or tingling, visual changes, speech difficulty.

Common causes: head injury, stroke, seizures/post-ictal state, hypoglycaemia, intoxication/overdose, metabolic disturbances.

Key interventions:

  • Rapid AVPU or GCS assessment and pupil examination.
  • Check bedside glucose and correct hypoglycaemia immediately.
  • Protect airway in patients with depressed consciousness or seizures.
  • Arrange urgent imaging (e.g. CT brain) when indicated.

E – Exposure / Environment

Clinical features: hidden injuries revealed on full exposure (rashes, wounds, burns, deformities), temperature abnormalities (hypo/hyperthermia).

Symptoms: pain, numbness, shivering, feeling very hot or cold.

Common causes: environmental exposure (heat/cold), burns, chemical or radiation exposure, concealed trauma.

Key interventions:

  • Fully expose the patient to examine front and back, maintaining dignity as far as possible.
  • Prevent heat loss: warm blankets, warmed IV fluids, increase ambient room temperature.
  • Remove wet clothing or contaminated materials (chemicals, etc.).
  • Initiate burn care or decontamination as per local protocols.

After the primary survey and resuscitation, proceed to a structured secondary survey (head-to-toe examination and detailed history).
