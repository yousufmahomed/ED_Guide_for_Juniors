Chest X-ray Analysis for Junior Doctors

When analyzing a chest x-ray, it is essential to follow a systematic approach. This can help ensure that no significant findings are missed. The following steps can be used as a guide:

  1. Check patient details and image quality:
    • Ensure the patient's name, date of birth, and study date are correct.
    • Confirm the image is of adequate quality (e.g., penetration, rotation, and inspiration).
  2. Assess the bones and soft tissues:
    • Look for fractures, dislocations, or bone lesions.
    • Examine the soft tissues for masses, swelling, or gas collections.
  3. Examine the lung fields for any abnormalities:
    • Inspect for consolidation, masses, nodules, or cavities.
    • Identify any signs of atelectasis or pneumothorax.
    • Assess the lung parenchyma for interstitial changes or fibrosis.
  4. Inspect the mediastinum and hilum:
    • Evaluate the size and shape of the heart and great vessels.
    • Check for any mediastinal or hilar masses or lymphadenopathy.
  5. Look for any pleural or diaphragmatic abnormalities:
    • Search for pleural effusions, thickening, or calcifications.
    • Examine the diaphragm for elevation, eventration, or herniation.

Common Chest X-ray Diagnoses and Their Radiological Features

Pneumonia
Feature Description
Consolidation Increased opacity in the affected area, usually lobar or segmental
Air bronchogram Air-filled bronchi visible within areas of consolidation
Silhouette sign Loss of normal anatomical borders due to adjacent consolidation
A comparison of a normal lung and a lung with left middle lobe pneumonia
Pleural Effusion
Feature Description
Blunted costophrenic angles Loss of the sharp angle between the diaphragm and chest wall
Fluid layering Horizontal fluid level within the pleural space, visible in lateral decubitus or erect films
Meniscus sign Crescent-shaped opacity at the lung base, representing fluid in the pleural space
A lung with a left sided Pleural Effusion
Pneumothorax
Feature Description
Visceral pleural line Thin white line representing the edge of the collapsed lung
Absence of lung markings No vascular markings peripheral to the visceral pleural line
Deep sulcus sign Increased depth and lucency of the costophrenic angle, suggesting pneumothorax in supine films
A lung with a right sided pneumothorax
Heart Failure
Feature Description
Cardiomegaly Enlarged cardiac silhouette
Kerley B lines Short, horizontal lines at the lung bases, representing interstitial edema
Upper lobe diversion Increased vascular congestion in the upper lung zones
Peribronchial cuffing Thickening of bronchial walls due to fluid accumulation
A Picture of a typical CCF CXR
Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD)
Feature Description
Hyperinflated lungs Increased lung volume with flattened diaphragm
Bullae Round, thin-walled, air-filled spaces within the lung parenchyma
Narrowed mediastinum Mediastinal structures appear compressed due to lung hyperinflation
The four stages of COPD Typical COPD findings on CXR
Lung Cancer
Lung Cancer Type Radiological Features
Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) Solitary nodule or mass, irregular margins, spiculated, cavitation
Small cell lung cancer (SCLC) Central mass or hilar mass, rapid growth, mediastinal or hilar lymphadenopathy
Adenocarcinoma Peripheral nodule or mass, ground-glass opacity, lepidic growth pattern
Squamous cell carcinoma Central mass or nodule, cavitation, hilar lymphadenopathy
Large cell carcinoma Peripheral or central mass, rapid growth, cavitation
Bronchial carcinoid tumor Well-defined, round or oval nodule, central or peripheral, slow growth
Metastases to the lung Multiple nodules, varying sizes, well-defined margins, random distribution, possible cavitation
