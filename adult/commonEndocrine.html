DKA – Emergency Management Summary (Adult)

Use this as a teaching aide. Always follow your local DKA protocol.

Typical Diagnostic Triad (adult)

  • Glucose > 11 mmol/L (or known diabetes)
  • Ketonaemia (blood ketones ≥ 3 mmol/L or moderate/large urine ketones)
  • Metabolic acidosis (pH < 7.3 and/or HCO₃⁻ < 15 mmol/L)

ED Priorities (ABCDE)

  1. Airway & Breathing – high-flow O₂ if hypoxic, treat any precipitating cause (e.g. infection).
  2. Circulation – IV access, monitor BP, HR, urine output, cardiac monitoring.
  3. Baseline tests – bedside glucose, ketones, VBG/ABG, U&E, FBC, cultures if indicated, ECG, pregnancy test if appropriate.

Fluids, Insulin & Potassium (Adults)

Step Typical ED Action Key Points
1. IV Fluids 0.9% NaCl; initial 1 L over 1 hour (slower in elderly / cardiac failure) Reassess BP, urine output, lungs, and adjust rate. Avoid fluid overload.
2. Potassium Replace once urine output established and K⁺ known K⁺ < 3.5: senior review, replacement before starting insulin.
K⁺ 3.5–5.0: add ~20–40 mmol KCl to each litre (as per protocol).
K⁺ > 5.0: no K⁺ initially, recheck frequently.
3. Insulin Fixed-rate insulin infusion (FRII) – usually 0.1 u/kg/hour IV Start after first litre of fluid and only if K⁺ ≥ 3.5 mmol/L. Aim glucose fall 3–5 mmol/L/hour.
4. Dextrose Add 5–10% dextrose when glucose < ~14 mmol/L Continue insulin to clear ketones; run dextrose to prevent hypoglycaemia.
Call a senior early for: shock, severe acidosis (pH < 7.1), K⁺ < 3.5 or > 6, altered GCS, pregnancy, renal or cardiac failure, or any uncertainty.

Thyroid Storm vs Myxoedema Coma – Quick Reference

Feature Thyroid Storm Myxoedema Coma
Typical patient Known / suspected hyperthyroid, often younger–middle-aged Long-standing hypothyroidism, elderly or frail
Temperature High fever (> 38.5–39°C) Often hypothermic or normal
Heart rate Marked tachycardia, AF common Bradycardia or inappropriately normal
Mental state Agitated, delirious, psychotic, seizures Lethargic, stupor, coma
BP / CVS High output failure, wide pulse pressure, may progress to shock Hypotension, low output failure
Other features Diarrhoea, vomiting, sweating, tremor Hyponatraemia, hypoglycaemia, hypoventilation

ED Drug Management (Outline Only – follow local guidelines)

Condition Key Drugs Comments
Thyroid Storm
  • Beta-blocker (e.g. Propranolol)
  • Thionamide (e.g. PTU or carbimazole)
  • Iodine solution (given after thionamide)
  • Hydrocortisone
 Control HR and fever, treat trigger (infection, surgery, etc.), consider ICU. Sequence is important: thionamide → iodine → steroids.
Myxoedema Coma
  • IV Levothyroxine ± liothyronine (as per protocol)
  • Hydrocortisone (stress dose)
  • Cautious IV fluids
 Support airway and ventilation, correct hypoglycaemia and electrolytes, active but gentle warming, treat sepsis. ICU care recommended.
Both conditions are ICU-level emergencies. In the ED: stabilise, start first-line therapy if protocol is clear, and discuss with senior / endocrinology / ICU urgently.

“Sliding Scale” vs Correctional Insulin

Teaching summary – not a full insulin protocol.

Key message: A “sliding scale only” (short-acting insulin based purely on current glucose, with no basal insulin) is unreliable and can be unsafe. A safer approach is:
  • Appropriate basal insulin (e.g. Protaphane/NPH or long-acting analogue)
  • ± mealtime insulin if eating
  • Plus a small dose of correctional insulin when capillary glucose is high

Example Adult Correctional Insulin (Rapid / Short-acting)

Use in addition to the patient’s usual basal / meal insulin. Adjust for insulin sensitivity, renal function and frailty.

Capillary Glucose (mmol/L) Suggested Correction Dose* Comments
7.1 – 10 0–1 units SC No extra insulin needed in most stable patients.
10.1 – 14 2 units SC Re-check glucose in 2–3 hours.
14.1 – 18 4 units SC Consider infection or missed doses if persistent.
> 18 6 units SC and discuss with senior Exclude DKA/HHS; may need IV insulin protocol.

*Example only. Always use your hospital’s insulin charts and protocols.

Glycaemic Emergencies – ED Algorithms (Adult)

1. DKA – at a Glance

  1. Confirm DKA (glucose, ketones, pH/HCO₃⁻).
  2. ABC, oxygen if needed, IV access, monitoring, catheter if indicated.
  3. Start 0.9% NaCl; take bloods (U&E, FBC, cultures if sepsis suspected).
  4. Check K⁺, correct as above, then start fixed-rate IV insulin.
  5. Add dextrose when glucose < ~14 mmol/L but continue insulin.
  6. Identify and treat trigger (infection, MI, omission of insulin, etc.).
  7. Admit to high-care/ICU or appropriate monitored bed.

2. HHS (Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State)

  • Very high glucose (often > 30 mmol/L), high osmolality, minimal ketones, often older and dehydrated.
  1. ABC, IV access, careful fluid resuscitation (slower than DKA, high risk of overload and cerebral oedema).
  2. 0.9% NaCl as main fluid; aim to correct osmolality gradually.
  3. Delay insulin until after initial fluid resuscitation unless ketones are significant.
  4. Low-dose IV insulin infusion if needed, with close glucose and electrolyte monitoring.
  5. Treat underlying cause (infection, MI, stroke, drugs, etc.).
  6. Admit to monitored bed / high-care; discuss with senior early.

3. Severe Hypoglycaemia (Adult)

Definition: BG usually < 3.0 mmol/L with neuroglycopaenic symptoms (confusion, seizures, coma) OR any symptomatic low sugar needing help.

a) If IV access available

  • Give IV dextrose:
    • e.g. 50 mL of 50% dextrose (25 g) OR 150–200 mL of 10% dextrose.
  • Re-check glucose after 10–15 minutes; repeat if still low.
  • Once awake and able to swallow: give long-acting carb snack / meal.

b) If NO IV access

  • Glucagon 1 mg IM/SC (if available) AND urgent IV access.
  • Once IV is in, follow IV dextrose steps above.
Always look for and treat the cause of hypoglycaemia (excess insulin or SU, missed meals, renal failure, sepsis, liver disease, alcohol, etc.) and observe the patient – rebound hypoglycaemia is common, especially with sulfonylureas.
