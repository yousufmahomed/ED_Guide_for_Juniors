Spousal / Partner Physical Abuse

Emergency Department Red Flags

Consider possible intimate partner / spousal abuse in any adult with injuries where the history does not fully fit. The following features should increase your level of concern:

  • Unexplained or poorly explained injuries (bruises, cuts, fractures, burns, strangulation marks).
  • Injuries inconsistent with the described mechanism.
  • Repeated ED visits for injuries, often with varying explanations.
  • Signs of emotional distress, fear, shame or marked anxiety.
  • History of prior injuries, “falls” or accidents documented in the record.
  • Direct or indirect disclosure of physical, emotional, or sexual abuse.
  • Reluctance to speak, especially when the partner is present.
  • Controlling or overbearing partner (insists on answering for the patient, refuses to leave the room, monitors conversations).

Atypical or Subtle Features

Abuse may be present even when injuries are minor or absent.

  • Inconsistencies between history and clinical findings (e.g. pattern bruising, repeated “accidents”).
  • Delay in seeking care for significant injuries.
  • Reluctance to answer questions openly, frequent glances at the partner.
  • Patient verbalises feeling unsafe at home but is hesitant to act.
  • Partner attempts to control examination, refuses separation, or becomes angry when you ask about safety.

Key Steps in the ED

  1. Immediate Safety
    If there is an imminent threat to life or serious harm:
    • Involve hospital security and, where appropriate, law enforcement.
    • Move the patient to a safe area if possible.
  2. Private, Respectful Environment
    • Politely request that the partner waits outside for at least part of the consultation.
    • Explain that you always speak to adult patients alone about sensitive health issues.
  3. Trauma-Informed Interview
    • Use open, non-judgmental questions (e.g. “Do you feel safe at home?”).
    • Acknowledge the difficulty of talking about violence (“Many people find this hard to talk about, but you are not alone.”).
    • Avoid blaming language; validate the patient’s experience.
  4. Thorough Medical Examination
    • Assess and treat all injuries and medical conditions.
    • Document injuries clearly (location, size, shape, colour, pattern, and whether they are tender).
    • Take photographs if local policy allows and with informed consent, following protocol.
  5. Documentation
    • Record the patient’s own words in quotation marks where possible.
    • Document any threats, weapons used, and whether children witnessed the abuse.
    • Note who accompanied the patient and any concerning behaviour by the partner.
  6. Safety Assessment & Planning
    • Ask if the patient feels safe to return home.
    • Discuss options such as shelters, staying with trusted family/friends, or other safe accommodation.
    • Help develop a basic safety plan (important contacts, safe escape routes, where documents/medications are kept).
  7. Medical Treatment
    • Provide analgesia, wound care, fracture management and any other indicated treatment.
    • Consider prophylaxis (e.g. tetanus, where applicable) and mental health support.
  8. Psychosocial Support
    • Offer to involve a social worker, psychologist, or counsellor if available.
    • Normalise help-seeking and emphasise that abuse is never the patient’s fault.
  9. Reporting and Legal Considerations
    • Explain your local legal obligations and limits of confidentiality in simple language.
    • For competent adults, reporting to law enforcement is often guided by patient choice; follow your national law and hospital protocol.
    • If children, older persons, or other vulnerable individuals are at risk, mandatory reporting may apply – follow local regulations.
  10. Provide Information & Resources
    • Give details of local shelters, helplines, social services, counselling and legal aid (ideally in written form the partner cannot easily see).
    • Where safe, encourage the patient to store key numbers under neutral names on their phone.
  11. Follow-Up and Continuity of Care
    • Arrange follow-up appointments (medical and psychosocial) where possible.
    • Ensure clear handover to primary care or another service if ongoing support is needed.
  12. Empower Patient Choice
    • Respect the patient’s decisions about reporting and next steps, provided there is no immediate legal obligation to act differently.
    • Emphasise that support remains available, even if they are not ready to leave the relationship or press charges now.

Always follow your hospital’s gender-based violence / domestic violence protocol and national legislation. When in doubt, seek advice from a senior clinician, social worker or legal department.

Remember: your primary responsibility is the safety and well-being of the patient. Approach all suspected cases of spousal or partner abuse with empathy, sensitivity, and a commitment to providing safe, confidential support.
