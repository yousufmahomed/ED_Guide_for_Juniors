Commonly Used Antipsychotics and Neuroleptics in the Emergency Department

⚠ Important ED Safety Notes:
  • Always exclude hypoxia, hypoglycaemia, meningitis, intoxication, and head injury before sedation.
  • Avoid IM Olanzapine within 1 hour of IV benzodiazepines → risk of respiratory depression.
  • QT prolongation risk ↑ with Haloperidol, Droperidol, Risperidone.
  • NMS risk: rigidity, fever, autonomic instability → stop all antipsychotics immediately.
  • Use lower doses in elderly patients or those with renal/hepatic impairment.
  • Always consult a senior for: pregnancy, HIV neuro involvement, severe agitation requiring repeat dosing, or medically unstable patients.
Antipsychotic / Neuroleptic Typical ED Dose Route Notes
Haloperidol 2.5–10 mg (may repeat every 30–60 min) IM / IV Risk of QT prolongation; good for psychosis; combine with benzo for severe agitation.
Olanzapine 5–10 mg (IM) or 5–10 mg (PO) IM / Oral Do NOT give IM within 1 hour of IV benzos. Good in elderly or medically fragile patients.
Quetiapine 25–50 mg Oral Useful for delirium or mild–moderate agitation; onset slower.
Risperidone 1–2 mg Oral Useful for chronic psych disorders; slower onset; avoid in QT prolongation.
Droperidol 2.5–5 mg (may repeat once) IM / IV Excellent sedative effect; monitor QT; good in violent agitation or meth intoxication.

Rapid Chemical Sedation Options (ED)

Regimen Typical Dose Use Case
Haloperidol + Lorazepam Haldol 5 mg IM + Lorazepam 2 mg IM/IV Severe agitation, psychosis
Droperidol 5–10 mg IM/IV Violent agitation, stimulant intoxication
