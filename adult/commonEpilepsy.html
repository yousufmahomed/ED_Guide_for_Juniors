Commonly Used Anti-Epileptic Medications

Note: Doses below are typical adult maintenance ranges. Always adjust for renal/hepatic function, drug levels, local protocols and senior guidance – especially for loading doses in status epilepticus.

Medication Typical Adult Daily Dose Route of Administration
Carbamazepine 200–1200 mg/day in divided doses Oral
Lamotrigine 50–400 mg/day (usually divided BID) Oral
Levetiracetam 500–3000 mg/day in divided doses Oral or intravenous
Phenytoin 200–300 mg/day (usually divided) Oral or intravenous
Sodium Valproate 500–2000 mg/day in divided doses Oral (IV preparations available in some centres)

Emergency Department Tips

  • Always check current therapy: Ask about existing anti-epileptic drugs (AEDs), doses, compliance and recent changes.
  • Status epilepticus: Use your local status epilepticus protocol for IV benzodiazepines and loading doses (e.g. phenytoin / valproate / levetiracetam).
  • Do not abruptly stop chronic AEDs unless there is a clear acute toxicity or serious reaction and a senior has been consulted.
  • Drug levels: Consider checking phenytoin / valproate levels if available in cases of poor control, suspected toxicity or non-adherence.
  • Admit or discuss with senior / neurology: first seizure, status epilepticus, recurrent seizures in ED, new focal neurology, head trauma, pregnancy, or suspected CNS infection.

Common Side Effects & Cautions (Quick Reference)

Medication Common Side Effects Key Cautions
Carbamazepine Drowsiness, dizziness, hyponatraemia, rash Avoid in significant hyponatraemia; watch for serious rash (e.g. SJS/TEN); many drug interactions (enzyme inducer).
Lamotrigine Headache, nausea, dizziness, insomnia Start low and go slow – risk of serious skin reactions (SJS/TEN), especially with valproate or rapid dose escalation.
Levetiracetam Somnolence, dizziness, mood changes, irritability Watch for behavioural / psychiatric symptoms; adjust in renal impairment.
Phenytoin Ataxia, nystagmus, sedation, gum hypertrophy (chronic) Narrow therapeutic index; risk of arrhythmias and hypotension with rapid IV administration; many drug interactions (enzyme inducer).
Sodium Valproate Weight gain, tremor, hair changes, GI upset Avoid in pregnancy where possible (teratogenic); caution in liver disease and thrombocytopenia; monitor LFTs and platelets.

Remember: Dosing and choice of AED should follow your hospital / neurology guidelines. When in doubt – discuss with a senior or on-call neurologist.
