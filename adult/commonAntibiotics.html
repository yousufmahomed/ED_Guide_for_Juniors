Antibiotic Cheat Sheet

Important Cautions When Prescribing Antibiotics

  • Always follow local guidelines: Use this table as a quick reference only. Choice of antibiotic, dose, and duration should follow your hospital / provincial protocols and microbiology advice.
  • Adjust for renal and hepatic impairment: Many agents (e.g. gentamicin, ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone in severe renal disease) need dose adjustments or avoidance in renal or liver dysfunction. Check eGFR/creatinine and LFTs.
  • Check allergies carefully: Clarify the nature of any “penicillin allergy” (rash vs anaphylaxis). Avoid beta-lactams in true anaphylaxis and use appropriate alternatives.
  • Beware QT prolongation: Fluoroquinolones (e.g. ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin) and macrolides (e.g. azithromycin) may prolong the QT interval – use cautiously in patients with cardiac disease, electrolyte abnormalities, or on other QT-prolonging drugs.
  • High C. diff risk drugs: Clindamycin, broad-spectrum cephalosporins and fluoroquinolones are associated with C. difficile infection; avoid prolonged or unnecessary courses.
  • Dose by weight where appropriate: For drugs like gentamicin and other aminoglycosides, use mg/kg dosing and ideal body weight where indicated. Consider drug levels if available.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: Avoid or use with caution: tetracyclines, certain fluoroquinolones and others depending on trimester. Check a reliable pregnancy-safe reference or senior advice.
  • Route matters: Use IV only when necessary (e.g. sepsis, poor gut absorption). Step down to oral as soon as the patient is clinically stable and able to tolerate PO.
  • Duration of therapy: Avoid “just in case” prolonged courses. Use the shortest effective duration as per local protocols (e.g. 5–7 days for many common infections).
  • Escalate or de-escalate based on cultures: Start empiric therapy early in sick patients, but narrow the spectrum once culture results and sensitivities are available.

This table is a quick aide-mémoire for junior doctors in the Emergency Department and is not a substitute for senior review, microbiology input, or your hospital’s antibiotic stewardship programme.
