History Structure

Main Complaint

This is a brief statement by the patient that describes the reason for seeking medical attention. It is important to document the patient's own words.

  • Try and elicit as many symptoms as you can, as this will help you in diagnosis
  • Be open to listening, the patient is not a health professional generally, and you may have to read between the lines, consider social and cultural aspects, and socio-economic impacts, to fully understand why the person is in front of you for this consultation
  • This may sound intimidating, but as you take more histories and practice, it can be accomplished in a very short time
  • It is also important to understand the circumstances surrounding the illness, for example
    • There is a big difference between two patients who have an injured ankle, where the first slipped on a wet floor and the second felt dizzy, fainted with a chest pain and woke up with a swollen ankle!!
    • Your approach to investigating them will be completely different!

History of Present Illness

This is a detailed description of the current problem.

  • Onset
    • Is this an index(first) presentation or a recurring problem
    • If it is recurring, try and determine if this episode is similar to others, and if not, how does it differ?
  • Duration of symptoms
    • If it is a problem that has started more than 24 hours ago :- What prompted the patient to seek help now?
      • The factors that can make someone come in
        • Change in severity
        • Alleviating methods no longer helping
        • Patient may be struggling with an abusive partner?
        • While it is not necessary to take hours interrogating a person, just asking a few basics can create a picture surrounding the current problem and address your patient holistically!
  • Severity (Use a pain scale if needed)
    • Has the severity changed, & if so, over what time-frame
    • Does anything aggravate or alleviate the severity

      • See the Pain Scale here

Past Medical & Co-Morbid History

This includes a summary of the patient's past illnesses, surgeries, hospitalizations, and chronic conditions.

For a more comprehensive look at this aspect please go to this Section dedicated to its discussion

Medications

Document all current medications the patient is taking.

It is also important to note medications that were taken for long periods before, but have now stopped and why.

While most patients may struggle to know doses, you should try and get a clear picture from them about frequency of use (twice a day, three times a day etc) and which illnesses were being treated.

Hint!! If your patient has brought their medication along (or a prescription or list), thank them for their thoughtfulness, I have found it builds instant rapport and makes the rest of the consultation go much easier.

In fact, any opportunity to show appreciation for the actions of the patient or caregiver or parent, makes a Huge impact on their impression of you, and makes you seem empathetic and sympathetic simultaneously!!

Go to this Page for a list of commonly used medications and dosages to assist you.

Allergies

Document any known allergies the patient has.

Especially important in the ED are allergies to :-

  • Penicillin
    • It is important to note that most penicillin based antibiotics do elicit a rash on first usage, but a true allergy causes a more severe anaphylaxis reaction.
  • Cephalosporins
  • Sulfur
  • ACE Inhibitors
  • Anaesthetic reactions (especially if patient needs emergency surgery)
  • Eggs
  • Nuts

Family History

Document any significant medical problems or conditions that run in the patient's family.

The co-morbid conditions will give you a good guide on family history as well.

But remember, you are trying to get information on diseases that have caused deaths and extended treatments for immediate family.

It is important to know that a patient with a purulent breast mass has two sisters being treated for breast cancer!!

This is something that comes with practice, but if you are starting your medical journey, take time to ask as much as possible.

Social History

This includes information on the patient's occupation, living situation, and any relevant social habits such as smoking or alcohol use.

Go to this Page to see the pack years calculator for smoking.

Events preceding illness/ injury.

This is more important for patients where the mechanism of injury seems suspicious or you suspect that another illness may be precipitating current problems.

As an example, a child presents with a spiral fracture of the femur, but caregiver insists they fell from the bed. This could indicate Child abuse!

Or you could look at the example given before of the two patients with ankle injuries but completely different events led up to the injury.

This can also help you later on if cases go to court or evolve into more complex issues.

Patients Last Meal

This is especially important if the patient is going into theatre.
