History Structure

Main Complaint (Presenting Complaint)

This is a brief statement, in the patient’s own words, describing why they came to the Emergency Department.

  • Try to record it as a short phrase in quotes, e.g. “Severe chest pain since this morning.”
  • Elicit as many symptoms as possible – patients often under-report important details initially.
  • Be open to listening – patients are usually not medically trained. You may need to “read between the lines” and consider social, cultural and socio-economic factors.
  • Try to understand the circumstances around the illness or injury:
    • There is a big difference between a sprained ankle from slipping on a wet floor and an ankle injury after syncope with chest pain and collapse – your approach and urgency will be very different.

With practice, this part of the history can be done quickly without sacrificing depth or empathy.

History of Present Illness (HPI)

This is a detailed description of the current problem from onset until now.

  • Onset
    • Is this the first episode (index presentation) or a recurrence?
    • If recurrent, is this episode similar to previous ones? If not, what is different?
  • Duration and progression
    • When did the symptoms start, and how have they evolved over time?
    • If symptoms started more than 24 hours ago, ask: “What made you decide to come in today?”
    • Common reasons for finally coming in:
      • Change or worsening in severity.
      • Home or over-the-counter remedies no longer helping.
      • New associated symptoms (e.g. shortness of breath, fever, weakness).
      • Social issues (e.g. an abusive partner, lack of support at home).
  • Severity (and impact)
    • Use a pain scale if needed, e.g. 0–10 (Pain Scale).
    • Has the severity changed? Over what time-frame?
    • Does anything make it better or worse (position, exertion, meals, movement)?
    • How is it affecting function – can they walk, work, sleep, eat?
  • Character and associated symptoms
    • Character of the main symptom (sharp, dull, burning, pleuritic, colicky etc.).
    • Location and radiation (e.g. chest pain radiating to jaw or left arm).
    • Associated symptoms – fever, weight loss, nausea/vomiting, dyspnoea, palpitations, neurological symptoms, urinary or bowel changes, etc.
  • Precipitating factors and relieving factors
    • What was the patient doing when the problem started?
    • Has anything helped (rest, medication, position, ice/heat)?
  • Ideas, Concerns and Expectations (ICE)
    • Ideas: What does the patient think might be wrong?
    • Concerns: What are they most worried about (heart attack, cancer, work, family)?
    • Expectations: What were they hoping for from this visit (relief, admission, a scan, a note)?

Review of Systems (Screen for Other Problems)

A brief, focused review of other systems can reveal important associated or alternative diagnoses.

  • Cardiorespiratory: chest pain, dyspnoea, palpitations, orthopnoea, cough, haemoptysis.
  • Abdominal: pain, nausea/vomiting, diarrhoea, constipation, bleeding per rectum, jaundice.
  • Neurological: headaches, seizures, weakness, sensory changes, speech or vision changes.
  • GU: dysuria, haematuria, frequency, retention, incontinence, vaginal or urethral discharge.
  • General: fever, weight loss, night sweats, fatigue.

Past Medical and Co-Morbid History

Summarise major past illnesses, surgeries, hospitalisations and chronic conditions.

  • Ask about chronic diseases commonly seen in ED: hypertension, diabetes, HIV, TB, asthma/COPD, heart disease, epilepsy, psychiatric illness.
  • Include any previous similar episodes or admissions for the same issue.

Medications

Document all current medications, including over-the-counter and traditional remedies.

  • Note chronic medications (names if possible), dose and frequency.
  • Ask about medication changes, recent new drugs and reasons for stopping any chronic medications.
  • Try to clarify what each medication is for – it helps confirm co-morbid conditions.
  • If the patient has brought medication boxes, prescriptions or a list, thank them – it builds rapport and improves accuracy.

Allergies and Adverse Reactions

Document any known allergies and serious adverse drug reactions.

  • Common important allergies in the ED:
    • Penicillins and other beta-lactams (note difference between mild rash and anaphylaxis).
    • Cephalosporins.
    • Sulfonamides.
    • Anaesthetic reactions (especially relevant if theatre is likely).
    • ACE inhibitors (angio-oedema, cough).
    • Food and latex allergies (e.g. eggs, nuts, shellfish if relevant).
  • Clarify what happened – rash, swelling, anaphylaxis, GI upset, etc.

Family History

Ask about significant illnesses in immediate family members, especially conditions that start young or cause disability/death.

  • Focus on first-degree relatives with heart disease, stroke, diabetes, hypertension, cancers, clotting disorders or sudden deaths.
  • Relate it to the current problem – for example, a patient with a breast lump and two sisters treated for breast cancer.

Social History

Social context often explains why the patient presents in a particular way and influences your management plan.

  • Occupation and daily activities (e.g. heavy lifting, night shifts, high stress).
  • Living situation and support – who is at home, caring responsibilities, safety concerns.
  • Substance use: smoking, alcohol, recreational drugs.
    • For smoking, see the Pack Years calculator.
  • Financial and transport issues that may affect follow-up or medication adherence.

Events Preceding Illness or Injury

This is particularly important where the mechanism of injury is unclear or suspicious, or when you suspect another illness triggered the current problem.

  • Clarify exactly what happened before and during the event (collapse, loss of consciousness, seizure activity, chest pain, headache, etc.).
  • Look for red flags for non-accidental injury:
    • Injuries inconsistent with the history (e.g. spiral femur fracture in a child said to have “fallen off the bed”).
    • Multiple injuries in different stages of healing.
    • Delays in presentation or changing stories.
  • Document these details clearly – they may be vital if the case goes to court or to child-protection services.

Last Meal (Time of Last Oral Intake)

Especially important if the patient may need anaesthesia or emergency surgery.

  • Ask when they last ate or drank anything (including water, chewing gum, milk, alcohol).
  • Record the approximate time and type of intake in the notes.

Summarising and Closing the History

At the end of the history:

  • Briefly summarise the main points back to the patient to check you have understood correctly.
  • Ask if there is anything else worrying them that you have not yet discussed.
  • Explain your next steps (examination, tests, possible treatments) and obtain consent.

It is impossible to cover every nuance of history taking on a single page. Use this as a basic structure, and continue to learn from your texts, seniors and your own experience. With practice, a good ED history becomes focused, efficient and patient-centred.
