Cardiac Exam

A cardiac examination is a focused assessment of the heart and circulation. In the Emergency Department it is used to rapidly identify life-threatening conditions (e.g. acute coronary syndrome, heart failure, arrhythmias, tamponade) and to risk-stratify patients with chest pain, dyspnoea, syncope or palpitations.

Key Cardiac Symptoms

  • Chest pain: onset, character, radiation, duration, associated symptoms (sweating, nausea, dyspnoea).
  • Breathlessness: exertional, orthopnoea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnoea.
  • Palpitations: fast/slow, regular/irregular, sudden onset/offset, associated syncope or presyncope.
  • Oedema and weight gain: ankle swelling, abdominal distension, rapid weight change.
  • Exercise tolerance: change in walking distance, fatigue on exertion.
  • Red flag features: syncope with exertion, chest pain with diaphoresis, severe hypotension, acute pulmonary oedema.

Focused Cardiac History

Important points in the patient’s history include:

  • Previous ischaemic heart disease, heart failure, cardiomyopathy, valvular disease, arrhythmias, rheumatic fever.
  • History of myocardial infarction, stroke or transient ischaemic attack.
  • Hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidaemia, chronic kidney disease, known structural heart disease.
  • Family history of premature IHD, sudden cardiac death, cardiomyopathy or inherited arrhythmias.
  • Current medications (especially beta-blockers, anticoagulants, anti-platelets, anti-arrhythmics, diuretics).
  • Allergies and recent changes in medication including over-the-counter and herbal preparations.
  • Smoking, alcohol and recreational drug history (e.g. cocaine, methamphetamines).
  • Exercise habits and functional capacity.
  • Other relevant conditions: thyroid disease, autoimmune disease, pregnancy/post-partum status, HIV and treatment.

Vital Signs

The following vital signs should be measured and documented clearly:

  • Blood pressure (ideally in both arms if aortic dissection or vascular disease is suspected).
  • Heart rate and rhythm (regular/irregular, fast/slow).
  • Respiratory rate.
  • Oxygen saturation (SpO2) on room air and with supplemental oxygen if given.
  • Temperature (infection, endocarditis, myocarditis).
  • Capillary refill time and peripheral perfusion (cool vs warm peripheries).

Abnormal vital signs (hypotension, tachycardia, tachypnoea, hypoxia, fever) are early markers of haemodynamic compromise and must be acted on promptly.

Physical Examination

Examination is usually performed with the patient at 30–45° where possible.

1. General Inspection

  • Overall appearance: distress, agitation, diaphoresis, cachexia or obesity.
  • Skin and mucosa: pallor, cyanosis, jaundice, xanthelasma, peripheral oedema.
  • Hands: clubbing, splinter haemorrhages, Janeway lesions, Osler nodes, peripheral perfusion.
  • Face and eyes: conjunctival pallor, corneal arcus, malar flush (mitral stenosis), JVP visible at rest.
  • Chest wall: scars (sternotomy, pacemaker/ICD), deformities, visible apex beat, heaves or pulsations.

2. Palpation

Palpation helps assess the mechanical activity of the heart and the circulation.

  • Carotid arteries: palpate one side at a time to assess volume and character. Avoid in suspected carotid disease or dissection.
  • Jugular venous pressure (JVP): assessed with the patient at 30–45°. Elevated JVP suggests right heart failure, fluid overload or pericardial disease.
  • Precordium: feel for heaves (sustained lifts suggesting ventricular hypertrophy) and thrills (palpable murmurs due to turbulent flow).
  • Point of maximal impulse (PMI): normally at 5th intercostal space, mid-clavicular line. A displaced or diffuse PMI may indicate cardiomegaly or LV dysfunction.
  • Peripheral oedema: assess ankles, sacrum and abdomen (ascites) in suspected heart failure.
  • Peripheral pulses: radial, femoral, dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial – compare sides and assess for radio-femoral delay (coarctation, aortic disease).

3. Auscultation

Auscultate with the diaphragm and bell of the stethoscope in a quiet environment. Listen both in normal respiration and during manoeuvres (expiration, inspiration, leaning forward, left lateral position).

  • S1: closure of mitral and tricuspid valves, best at the apex.
  • S2: closure of aortic and pulmonic valves, best at the base. Splitting patterns can be physiologic or pathologic.
  • S3: “ventricular gallop”; may be normal in young patients but in older adults usually suggests volume overload or heart failure.
  • S4: “atrial gallop”; often due to a stiff ventricle (hypertension, ischaemia, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy).
  • Murmurs: character, timing (systolic/diastolic/continuous), grade, radiation and response to manoeuvres (e.g. inspiration, Valsalva, squatting).
  • Pericardial rub: scratchy, triphasic sound, best heard with the diaphragm at the left sternal border in pericarditis.
  • Gallops: S3/S4 or summation gallops in severe heart failure or cardiomyopathy.

4. Percussion

Percussion is rarely used routinely in modern cardiac examination but may be used in resource-limited or teaching settings to roughly estimate cardiac size or detect large pleural effusions. Imaging is more accurate.

Bedside and Diagnostic Tests

Based on symptoms and examination findings, common ED investigations include:

  • 12-lead ECG: rhythm, rate, conduction, ischaemia, infarction, chamber enlargement, electrolyte and drug effects.
  • Continuous cardiac monitoring: for unstable patients or those with significant arrhythmia risk.
  • Point-of-care ultrasound (POCUS): quick assessment of LV function, pericardial effusion, gross valve abnormality and IVC size where available.
  • Chest X-ray: cardiac size, pulmonary congestion, effusions, alternative causes of chest symptoms.
  • Blood tests: high-sensitivity troponin, full blood count, U&E/creatinine, glucose, lipids (non-urgent), thyroid function, BNP/NT-proBNP where indicated.
  • Echocardiogram: formal assessment of chamber size, systolic and diastolic function, valves, pericardium; urgent in suspected acute valve lesions or tamponade.
  • Cardiac stress testing: usually arranged electively or in observation units for stable patients with low–intermediate risk chest pain.
  • Coronary imaging: CT coronary angiography or invasive coronary angiography, according to local protocols and cardiology input.

Summary

A structured cardiac examination combines a focused history, careful observation, vital signs, targeted physical examination and appropriate investigations. In the Emergency Department, the priorities are to rapidly identify patients who are unstable or at high risk of acute coronary syndrome, heart failure, arrhythmias or structural cardiac disease, and to initiate timely treatment and referral.
