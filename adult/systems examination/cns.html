CNS Examination

Introduction

The central nervous system (CNS) examination is a core part of the Emergency Department assessment. It helps to rapidly identify time-critical conditions such as stroke, raised intracranial pressure, meningitis/encephalitis, spinal cord lesions and seizures, and to decide on imaging, referral and level of care.

A structured exam usually includes mental status, cranial nerves, motor system, sensory system and reflexes.

Mental Status Examination

The mental status exam assesses level of consciousness and higher cortical function:

  • Level of consciousness: AVPU or Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) – eye opening, verbal response, motor response.
  • Appearance and behaviour: grooming, agitation or drowsiness, psychomotor slowing, abnormal movements.
  • Speech and language: fluency, comprehension, repetition, naming; presence of dysarthria or aphasia.
  • Mood and affect: observed emotional state and reactivity, congruence with situation.
  • Thought content and perception: delusions, hallucinations, suicidal ideas, disorganised thinking.
  • Attention and concentration: digit span, serial 7s, spelling a word backwards.
  • Memory and orientation: person, place, time, situation; short-term recall (3 objects), long-term events.

Cranial Nerves Examination

The cranial nerve exam helps localise lesions within the brainstem and supratentorial regions, and is essential in suspected stroke, raised ICP, head injury and infections.

  • I – Olfactory: test smell in each nostril with familiar odours (if relevant and safe).
  • II – Optic: visual acuity (Snellen if available), visual fields to confrontation, funduscopy if possible.
  • III, IV, VI – Oculomotor, Trochlear, Abducens: pupil size and reactivity, eye movements in all directions, ptosis, diplopia, nystagmus.
  • V – Trigeminal: facial sensation (light touch, pinprick) in all three divisions; masseter strength, jaw opening.
  • VII – Facial: upper and lower facial movements – raise eyebrows, close eyes tightly, smile, puff out cheeks (UMN vs LMN pattern).
  • VIII – Vestibulocochlear: hearing to finger rub or whisper; consider Rinne/Weber; assess for nystagmus and balance problems.
  • IX, X – Glossopharyngeal, Vagus: palate elevation (“ah”), gag reflex (if needed), voice quality, swallowing.
  • XI – Accessory: shoulder shrug and head rotation against resistance.
  • XII – Hypoglossal: tongue position at rest and on protrusion, atrophy or fasciculations, tongue strength in cheek.

Motor System Examination

Assess bulk, tone, power, coordination and gait in a systematic way.

  • Inspection: muscle bulk (atrophy, fasciculations), abnormal posturing, tremor or involuntary movements.
  • Tone: assessed by passive movement of limbs:
    • Normal, decreased (flaccid) or increased (spasticity/rigidity).
    • Spasticity suggests an upper motor neuron (UMN) lesion; flaccid tone suggests a lower motor neuron (LMN) or acute UMN lesion.
  • Power: test key muscle groups in upper and lower limbs using MRC 0–5 scale:
    • 0 – no contraction
    • 1 – flicker
    • 2 – movement with gravity eliminated
    • 3 – movement against gravity
    • 4 – movement against some resistance
    • 5 – normal power
  • Coordination: finger-to-nose, heel-to-shin, rapid alternating movements (dysdiadochokinesia), rebound phenomenon.
  • Gait and balance: normal gait, heel and toe walking, tandem gait; Romberg’s test for proprioceptive/vestibular dysfunction.

Sensory System Examination

Sensory testing helps distinguish peripheral nerve, root, spinal cord and cortical lesions. Compare side-to-side and proximal vs distal.

  • Light touch and pinprick: test upper and lower limbs; look for dermatomal, “glove and stocking”, unilateral or patchy loss.
  • Temperature: if needed, use a cool or warm object to test abnormal areas found on pinprick.
  • Vibration sense: tuning fork over bony prominences (e.g. great toe, ankle, fingers) – often affected early in peripheral neuropathy and dorsal column lesions.
  • Proprioception: move distal phalanx up/down with eyes closed; patient identifies direction.
  • Cortical sensory functions: two-point discrimination, graphesthesia (drawn numbers), stereognosis (identify objects by touch) where relevant.

Reflexes Examination

Deep tendon reflexes help to differentiate UMN from LMN lesions and assess segmental function.

  • Deep tendon reflexes (DTRs): biceps, triceps, brachioradialis, patellar, Achilles.
    Grade:
    • 0 – absent
    • 1+ – diminished
    • 2+ – normal
    • 3+ – brisk
    • 4+ – very brisk, often with clonus
  • Plantar response: stroke lateral sole from heel to ball of foot.
    Normal – flexion of toes; extensor response (Babinski) suggests UMN lesion in adults.
  • Clonus: sustained clonus at the ankle or patella is usually pathologic and supports an UMN lesion.

Conclusion

A structured CNS examination in the Emergency Department supports rapid identification of stroke, spinal cord lesions, raised intracranial pressure, CNS infection, metabolic encephalopathy and functional disorders. Combining a focused history, mental status assessment, cranial nerve exam, motor and sensory testing, and reflexes provides powerful localisation and guides imaging, consultation and disposition decisions.
