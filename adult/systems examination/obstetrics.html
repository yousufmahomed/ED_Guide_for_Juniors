Obstetric Examination

Obstetric Assessment

During obstetric assessment in the emergency department, providers will typically perform the following:

  • Take a thorough medical history, including information about the patient's pregnancy, prenatal care, and any complications. This may include asking about the patient's due date, the number of previous pregnancies, any prior complications, and any medications or supplements the patient is taking.   • Perform a physical examination, including a pelvic exam and abdominal exam. The pelvic exam may assess cervical dilation and effacement, as well as the position of the fetus. The abdominal exam may assess fundal height and the presence of fetal movements.
  • Monitor fetal heart rate and uterine contractions. Fetal heart rate monitoring can help assess the health of the fetus, while uterine contractions can help determine if the patient is in labor.
  • Assess for signs of preterm labor, including cervical dilation and effacement. Preterm labor can be a serious complication that may require prompt intervention to prevent preterm birth and potential complications for the baby.
  • Check the patient's blood pressure, heart rate, and oxygen saturation levels. Vital sign monitoring can help assess the patient's overall health and identify any potential complications.
  • Order diagnostic tests, such as blood work, urine tests, and ultrasound imaging, as needed. These tests can help assess the health of the mother and fetus and identify any potential complications.
  • Determine the patient's gestational age and estimate the fetal weight. This information can help guide management decisions and assess the health of the fetus.
  • Assess for signs of fetal distress or other complications, such as placental abruption or uterine rupture. Fetal distress can be a serious complication that may require immediate intervention, while placental abruption and uterine rupture are rare but potentially life-threatening emergencies.

Differential Diagnosis

Conditions that may present with obstetric symptoms in the emergency department include:

Condition Common Symptoms Diagnostic Tests Treatment Danger Signs Grading
Preterm labor Contractions, pelvic pressure, vaginal discharge, back pain Fetal fibronectin test, cervical length measurement, ultrasound Tocolytic medication, corticosteroids, bed rest Increasing frequency and intensity of contractions, vaginal bleeding, ruptured membranes Based on gestational age and cervical length measurement
Placental abruption Vaginal bleeding, abdominal pain, contractions, fetal distress Ultrasound, fetal heart monitoring, blood tests Immediate delivery, blood transfusions, fluid replacement, medications to stop contractions Severe vaginal bleeding, rapid fetal heart rate, abdominal pain, signs of shock Based on severity of placental separation and fetal distress
Preeclampsia High blood pressure, proteinuria, swelling, headaches Blood tests, urine tests, fetal monitoring, ultrasound Bed rest, medication to lower blood pressure, delivery of baby if severe Severe headache, visual changes, chest pain, shortness of breath Mild, moderate, severe (based on blood pressure and laboratory results)
Gestational diabetes Increased thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, blurred vision Glucose tolerance test, blood sugar monitoring, fetal ultrasound Dietary changes, medication to control blood sugar, fetal monitoring High blood pressure, excessive fetal growth, premature birth
Placenta praevia Painless vaginal bleeding, uterine contractions Monitoring, bed rest, blood transfusion, cesarean delivery
Breech presentation Fetal buttocks or feet in the pelvis, decreased fetal movements External cephalic version, vaginal breech delivery, or cesarean delivery

Management of Eclampsia

Management Description Dose
Stabilize the patient Ensure airway, breathing, and circulation are adequate. Administer oxygen, monitor vital signs, and maintain intravenous access. N/A
Control seizures Administer magnesium sulfate to control seizures and prevent recurrence. Diazepam or lorazepam may be given as an alternative or adjunctive therapy. Magnesium sulfate: 4-6 g IV bolus, followed by 2-3 g/hour continuous infusion; Diazepam: 5-10 mg IV, may repeat once after 10 minutes; Lorazepam: 2-4 mg IV, may repeat once after 10 minutes.
Lower blood pressure Administer antihypertensive medications, such as hydralazine or labetalol, to lower blood pressure to a safe range. Hydralazine: 5-10 mg IV, may repeat every 20-30 minutes up to 30 mg total dose; Labetalol: 20-80 mg IV, may repeat every 10 minutes up to 220 mg total dose.
Induce delivery Delivery is the definitive treatment for eclampsia. In cases of severe eclampsia or fetal distress, immediate delivery may be necessary. In milder cases, induction of labor may be initiated after maternal stabilization. N/A
Postpartum management Continue magnesium sulfate for at least 24 hours postpartum to prevent recurrent seizures. Monitor for signs of postpartum hemorrhage, infection, and preeclampsia. Magnesium sulfate: 1-2 g/hour continuous infusion for at least 24 hours postpartum

Maternal Trauma and ED Caesarian Section

Maternal trauma can occur as a result of a variety of causes, including motor vehicle accidents, falls, and physical assault. In the emergency department, the patient's vital signs are immediately assessed, and a thorough physical examination is performed to assess for injuries. Diagnostic tests, such as X-rays and CT scans, may be ordered to evaluate for internal injuries.

In cases of severe maternal trauma, an emergency caesarian section may be necessary to deliver the baby quickly and avoid further harm to the mother and child. This procedure involves making an incision in the mother's abdomen and uterus to deliver the baby. Indications for an ED caesarian section include:

  • Maternal instability or cardiac arrest
  • Fetal distress or compromise
  • Complete placenta previa with heavy bleeding
  • Uterine rupture or abruption
  • Maternal trauma with fetal compromise

ED caesarian sections are considered a last resort and are only performed in emergent situations where waiting for a scheduled procedure is not feasible. The procedure is typically performed under general anesthesia, and the mother and baby are closely monitored for complications, such as bleeding and infection, following the delivery.
