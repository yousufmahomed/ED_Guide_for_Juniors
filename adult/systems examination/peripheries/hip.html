Hip Exam

Overview

The hip examination in the Emergency Department focuses on recognising neck of femur fractures, hip dislocations, septic arthritis and referred pain from the lumbar spine or pelvis. A structured approach helps distinguish patients who need urgent orthopaedic intervention from those who can be investigated and managed more conservatively.

Always consider the hip in the context of the whole lower limb and mechanism of injury, especially in the elderly (low-energy falls) and in high-energy trauma.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

After ABC and a brief trauma survey, look for:

  • Inability to weight-bear after a fall, especially in an older patient.
  • Shortened, externally rotated leg – classic for neck of femur fracture.
  • Severe pain with minimal movement of the hip, especially with fever or systemic toxicity – think septic arthritis.
  • Hip dislocation (often posterior after high-energy trauma) – limb shortened, adducted and internally rotated; sciatic nerve at risk.
  • High-energy mechanism (MVC, fall from height) – consider acetabular fractures and associated injuries.
  • Children with atraumatic limp, fever or inability to bear weight – consider septic arthritis or slipped upper femoral epiphysis (SUFE).

Any suspected neck of femur fracture, septic arthritis or hip dislocation requires urgent orthopaedic review.

Focused History

  • Mechanism: fall from standing, fall from height, direct blow, twisting injury, high-speed trauma, sports injury.
  • Onset and course: sudden vs gradual, acute trauma vs insidious pain, ability to walk immediately after injury.
  • Pain characteristics: groin, lateral hip, buttock or referred knee pain; constant vs movement-related.
  • Systemic features: fever, rigors, weight loss, night sweats, history of malignancy or infection.
  • Risk factors: osteoporosis, previous fragility fractures, chronic steroid use, alcohol excess, sickle cell disease, HIV, TB, inflammatory arthritis.
  • Functional status: baseline mobility and level of independence – important for disposition planning.
  • Red flag questions in children: recent infection, trauma, night pain, weight loss, systemic symptoms.

Examination Technique

Examine the patient supine if possible, with pelvis straight and both legs exposed from umbilicus to feet. Observe before moving the limb.

1. Look

  • General: posture, distress, ability to move in bed or stand.
  • Leg alignment: shortening, external or internal rotation, abduction or adduction.
  • Swelling, bruising or deformity over the hip, groin, lateral thigh or buttock.
  • Surgical scars, skin changes or previous wounds.

2. Feel

Palpate gently, watching the patient’s face:

  • Bony landmarks: anterior superior iliac spine (ASIS), iliac crest, greater trochanter, pubic symphysis, sacroiliac joints.
  • Soft tissues: trochanteric region (bursitis), groin, adductor origin, lateral thigh musculature.
  • Check for crepitus, warmth, effusion or obvious step deformity.

3. Move

If fracture or dislocation is suspected, keep movement to a minimum and wait for X-rays and orthopaedic input. Otherwise, assess range of motion:

  • Flexion and extension: flex hip with knee bent; assess any pain or restriction.
  • Internal and external rotation: with hip flexed to 90°, rotate leg in and out; pain in the groin may indicate intra-articular pathology.
  • Abduction and adduction: move the leg away from and towards midline, comparing sides.

4. Special Tests

  • Log roll test: gently roll the relaxed leg internally and externally; pain suggests intra-articular pathology or fracture.
  • Axial load / heel strike: gentle axial compression through the heel or flexed knee; pain in the hip raises suspicion of femoral neck or acetabular fracture.
  • FABER (Patrick’s) test: Flexion, ABduction, External Rotation – pain in groin suggests hip pathology; pain in SI area suggests sacroiliac disease.
  • FADIR test: Flexion, ADduction, Internal Rotation – may reproduce pain in femoroacetabular impingement or labral tears.
  • Trendelenburg test: patient stands on one leg; failure of the pelvis to stay level or rise on the contralateral side suggests hip abductor weakness.

Neurovascular Examination of the Lower Limb

A neurovascular assessment must be documented in all significant hip injuries, particularly dislocations and high-energy trauma.

1. Circulation

  • Femoral pulse: in the groin, midway between ASIS and pubic symphysis.
  • Popliteal, dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses: compare both sides for presence and symmetry.
  • Capillary refill: toes < 2 seconds; check colour and temperature of the foot.

2. Sensation

  • Femoral nerve: anterior thigh and medial leg (via saphenous branch).
  • Obturator nerve: medial thigh (often subtle, test with adductor squeeze).
  • Sciatic nerve: posterior thigh, leg and most of the foot (tibial and peroneal branches).

3. Motor Function

  • Femoral nerve: knee extension (quadriceps) – ask patient to straighten the knee against resistance.
  • Sciatic nerve / peroneal branch: ankle dorsiflexion and toe extension.
  • Sciatic nerve / tibial branch: ankle plantarflexion and toe flexion.

4. Red Flags

  • Absent or diminished distal pulses following hip dislocation or fracture.
  • New foot drop (common peroneal nerve involvement) or global sciatic deficit.
  • Progressive numbness or weakness after reduction or manipulation.

Any neurovascular deficit associated with a hip injury is an emergency – urgently involve orthopaedics and vascular surgery where available.

Common ED Hip Conditions

Condition Key Findings ED Pearls
Neck of femur fracture Elderly patient after a low-energy fall, inability to weight-bear, shortened and externally rotated leg, hip and groin tenderness. X-ray AP pelvis and lateral hip. Consider occult fracture if pain with log roll/axial load but normal X-ray – may need CT or MRI. Early surgery improves outcomes.
Hip dislocation (usually posterior) High-energy trauma, leg flexed, adducted and internally rotated; severe pain and inability to move. Possible sciatic nerve deficit. True orthopaedic emergency – reduction ideally within 6 hours. Obtain X-ray before and after reduction. Perform full neurovascular exam and document carefully.
Septic arthritis Acute onset severe hip pain, inability to move the joint, fever, systemic illness, often holds hip flexed and externally rotated. Do not delay antibiotics and orthopaedic review. Requires urgent aspiration and often surgical washout. Particularly important in children and immunocompromised patients.
Greater trochanteric pain syndrome / bursitis Lateral hip pain, point tenderness over greater trochanter, pain lying on that side, normal range of motion. Exclude fracture in older patients with trauma or inability to weight-bear. Often managed conservatively with analgesia and physiotherapy.
Labral tear / FAI Young or middle-aged adult with groin pain, clicking or catching, pain on FADIR/FABER, often worse with flexion/rotation. ED role is exclusion of fracture/infection, analgesia and referral to orthopaedics or sports medicine for further imaging and management.
Osteoarthritis of the hip Chronic groin or buttock pain, stiffness, reduced range of motion, difficulty with putting on shoes or getting in/out of car. Manage pain and mobility issues acutely; arrange follow-up for long-term management and potential joint replacement.
Osteonecrosis (avascular necrosis) Groin or buttock pain, often in younger adults with steroid use, alcohol excess or sickle cell disease; may have normal early X-rays. Maintain suspicion in at-risk patients with persistent hip pain. ED role is recognising risk and referring for further imaging and specialist review.

Investigations

  • X-ray: AP pelvis and lateral hip for suspected fracture or dislocation.
  • CT: better assessment of acetabular fractures, complex fractures or occult fractures not seen on X-ray.
  • MRI: useful for occult fractures, labral tears and osteonecrosis; usually arranged as outpatient unless urgent.
  • Blood tests: FBC, CRP, ESR, blood cultures if septic arthritis or osteomyelitis suspected.

Management & Prevention

Treatment depends on the underlying diagnosis and patient factors:

  • Analgesia: regular oral analgesics; consider regional blocks (e.g. femoral nerve block) in hip fractures if skilled.
  • Immobilisation and weight-bearing: non-weight-bearing and bed rest for suspected fractures until cleared; early mobilisation when safe.
  • Surgery: early operative management for most hip fractures and dislocations, selected labral tears and advanced osteoarthritis.
  • Physiotherapy and rehabilitation: crucial post-operatively and for chronic conditions.
  • Bone health and falls prevention: calcium/vitamin D, osteoporosis treatment, home safety assessment and vision review in the elderly.

Prevention of hip injuries includes regular weight-bearing exercise, attention to bone health, safe environments to reduce falls, and appropriate protective equipment in high-impact sports.
