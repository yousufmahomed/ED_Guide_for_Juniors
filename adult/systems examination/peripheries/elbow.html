Elbow Exam

Overview

The elbow examination in the Emergency Department focuses on identifying fractures, dislocations, ligamentous injuries, tendon ruptures and neurovascular compromise. A structured approach helps distinguish injuries needing urgent orthopaedic management from those suitable for conservative treatment and outpatient follow-up.

Always examine the elbow in the context of the entire upper limb and mechanism of injury, and remember that children may have subtle supracondylar or radial head fractures with minimal visible deformity.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

After ABC, assess the injured limb for the following:

  • Gross deformity with suspected fracture–dislocation.
  • Open fractures or skin tenting.
  • Severe pain with passive stretch of fingers/forearm and tense compartments – possible compartment syndrome.
  • Neurovascular compromise: absent radial pulse, cool hand, reduced capillary refill, new motor or sensory deficit.
  • Suspected supracondylar fracture in a child (fall onto outstretched hand with elbow pain and swelling).
  • Systemic signs (fever, toxicity) with a hot, swollen joint – consider septic arthritis.

Any red flag warrants immediate senior review and early orthopaedic involvement.

Focused History

  • Mechanism: fall onto outstretched hand (FOOSH), direct blow, twisting, crush, sporting injury, high-energy trauma.
  • Onset and severity: sudden vs gradual, ability to continue using the arm or bear weight through it.
  • Pain location: lateral (radial head), medial (MCL/epicondyle), posterior (olecranon), anterior (supracondylar region).
  • Mechanical symptoms: locking, catching, giving way, or inability to fully extend the elbow.
  • Associated symptoms: numbness or tingling in hand/fingers, weakness, shoulder or wrist pain.
  • Past history: previous fractures, surgery, inflammatory arthritis, gout.

Examination Technique

Examine the entire upper limb, comparing with the opposite side. Remove jewellery early if swelling is anticipated.

1. Look

  • Observe posture and carrying angle of both elbows.
  • Inspect for swelling, bruising, deformity, wounds or scars.
  • Note ability to actively move the elbow and forearm.

2. Feel

Palpate gently, starting away from the point of maximal tenderness:

  • Bony landmarks: medial and lateral epicondyles, olecranon, radial head and neck, supracondylar region.
  • Joint line and soft tissues: assess for effusion, warmth, crepitus or focal tenderness.
  • Check for step deformities and assess the relationship of the three bony points: medial epicondyle, lateral epicondyle and olecranon (triangle/line test).

3. Move

Assess both active and gentle passive range of motion:

  • Flexion and extension at the elbow.
  • Pronation and supination of the forearm with elbow flexed at 90°.
  • Note where in the range pain occurs and whether motion is blocked.

4. Special Tests (selected examples)

  • Valgus/varus stress tests: assess medial and lateral collateral ligament stability (performed cautiously and usually after X-ray if fracture suspected).
  • Chair or resisted extension tests: may reproduce pain in triceps tendon or olecranon processes.
  • Resisted wrist extension/flexion: pain over lateral epicondyle (tennis elbow) or medial epicondyle (golfer’s elbow) in chronic presentations.

5. Neurovascular Examination

Document neurovascular status before and after any manipulation, reduction or splinting.

  • Circulation: radial and ulnar pulses, capillary refill, hand temperature and colour.
  • Sensation:
    • Median nerve – palmar aspect of thumb, index and middle fingers.
    • Ulnar nerve – little finger and ulnar border of hand.
    • Radial nerve – dorsum of hand over first web space.
  • Motor:
    • Median nerve – thumb opposition, finger flexion.
    • Ulnar nerve – finger abduction/adduction.
    • Radial nerve – wrist and finger extension, thumb extension.

Common ED Elbow Conditions

Condition Key Findings ED Pearls
Supracondylar fracture (children) Usually FOOSH, swollen painful elbow, reluctance to move; may have subtle deformity. High risk of brachial artery and nerve injury. Always check and document pulses and nerves. Look for posterior fat pad / sail sign on X-ray. Urgent orthopaedic referral for displaced fractures.
Radial head / neck fracture Pain and tenderness over radial head, pain with forearm rotation, sometimes minimal swelling. Frequently missed. Suspect if patient cannot fully pronate/supinate. Consider elbow and forearm X-rays.
Elbow dislocation Obvious deformity, severe pain, often after fall or sports injury. May be associated with fractures (terrible triad). Check neurovascular status carefully. Reduction under sedation per local protocol, followed by X-ray. Early ortho input recommended.
Nursemaid’s elbow (pulled elbow) Young child, arm held flexed and pronated, refuses to use arm after a sudden pull. Minimal swelling. Clinical diagnosis; usually no X-ray required if classical. Reduction by gentle supination–flexion or hyperpronation. Document return of function.
Olecranon fracture Posterior elbow pain and swelling, difficulty actively extending elbow. Check triceps function. Many require ortho review; beware open injuries in high-energy trauma.
Septic arthritis Hot, swollen, very painful elbow with reduced range of motion, often with fever and systemic signs. Orthopaedic emergency. Bloods, cultures, antibiotics, and urgent orthopaedic assessment for aspiration and possible washout.

Imaging in the ED

  • Plain radiographs: AP and lateral elbow views are first line for trauma.
  • In children, look carefully for fat pad signs and alignment (anterior humeral line, radiocapitellar line).
  • CT: for complex intra-articular fractures or pre-operative planning.
  • MRI or ultrasound: usually outpatient for suspected ligamentous or tendon injuries in stable patients.

Management Principles

  • Analgesia: oral or IV; consider regional blocks where appropriate and skilled.
  • Immobilisation: sling, backslab or cast depending on injury and stability; immobilise joints above and below for fractures.
  • Reduction: for dislocations and some fractures, following local protocols and with adequate sedation/analgesia.
  • Neurovascular reassessment: always repeat after reduction or splinting and document clearly.
  • Follow-up: arrange orthopaedic clinic or fracture clinic review where indicated; provide clear written instructions and return precautions.

Early recognition of serious injury, proper immobilisation and meticulous documentation of neurovascular status are central to safe elbow management in the ED.
