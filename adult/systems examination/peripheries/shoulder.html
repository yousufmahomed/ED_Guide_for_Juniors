Shoulder Exam

Overview

The shoulder examination in the Emergency Department focuses on identifying dislocations, fractures, rotator cuff injuries, acromioclavicular (AC) joint injuries and referred pain (e.g. from the neck or chest). A structured approach helps distinguish time-critical pathology from conditions suitable for outpatient management.

Always consider the shoulder in the context of the entire upper limb and mechanism of injury, and remember that elderly patients may sustain significant fractures from low energy falls.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

After ABC, assess for shoulder-specific red flags:

  • Obvious deformity with suspected shoulder dislocation or proximal humerus fracture.
  • Neurovascular compromise: absent/weak radial pulse, cool hand, reduced capillary refill, new motor or sensory deficit (especially axillary nerve).
  • Open fractures or dislocations, or skin tenting threatening to open.
  • Suspected septic arthritis: acute hot, swollen shoulder with severe pain on movement, often febrile.
  • Referred pain: shoulder pain with chest pain, dyspnoea, or diaphoresis – consider cardiac or aortic causes.
  • Multiple injuries / high-energy trauma – consider associated thoracic, spinal and scapular injuries.

Any red flag warrants early senior review and often urgent orthopaedic and/or medical input.

Focused History

  • Mechanism: fall onto outstretched hand, direct blow, seizure, electric shock, traction injury, overhead sports, high-energy trauma.
  • Onset and character of pain: sudden vs gradual, constant vs movement-related, night pain.
  • Functional impact: ability to move the arm, dress, reach overhead, or lie on that side.
  • Previous shoulder history: recurrent dislocations, surgery, chronic rotator cuff disease.
  • Associated symptoms: neck pain, distal paraesthesiae, weakness, chest pain, dyspnoea.
  • Systemic features: fevers, weight loss, malignancy, IV drug use (for infection or metastasis).

Examination Technique

Examine both shoulders where possible, comparing with the unaffected side. Expose the shoulder and upper chest while preserving dignity.

1. Look

  • Inspect from front, side and back for asymmetry, swelling, bruising or deformity.
  • Note any loss of normal deltoid contour (suggesting anterior dislocation) or prominent acromion.
  • Look for scapular winging, muscle wasting (chronic cuff tears or nerve injury) and surgical scars.

2. Feel

  • Palpate the clavicle, AC joint, acromion, scapular spine, humeral head and bicipital groove.
  • Assess for point tenderness, step deformities and crepitus.
  • Check for warmth or effusion in suspected infection or inflammatory disease.

3. Move – Range of Motion

Assess active movement first; if limited, cautiously assess passive movement (unless fracture/dislocation suspected).

  • Forward flexion: raising arm in front.
  • Abduction: lifting arm sideways to 90° and above (glenohumeral and scapulothoracic contribution).
  • External rotation: with elbow at side, forearm forward.
  • Internal rotation: “hand behind back” and note highest vertebral level reached.

In an acute setting, limited active elevation with preserved passive motion suggests a rotator cuff tear, whereas both active and passive movements are restricted in adhesive capsulitis or significant joint pathology.

4. Special Tests (Selected ED-Relevant)

  • Rotator cuff:
    • Supraspinatus (Jobe’s “empty can”): resisted elevation in scapular plane.
    • Infraspinatus/teres minor: resisted external rotation with elbow at side.
    • Subscapularis: lift-off or belly-press tests.
  • AC joint: focal tenderness over ACJ, pain on cross-body adduction.
  • Impingement signs: painful arc (60–120° of abduction), Hawkins–Kennedy test.
  • Instability (usually for recurrent cases, not acute dislocation pre-reduction): apprehension test in abduction–external rotation.

5. Neurovascular Examination

Document neurovascular status in all significant shoulder injuries, especially dislocations.

  • Axillary nerve: sensation over “regimental badge” area (lateral deltoid), deltoid power (shoulder abduction).
  • Radial, median, ulnar nerves: motor and sensory function distally in the hand.
  • Circulation: radial pulse, capillary refill, limb temperature and colour.

Indications for Imaging

Imaging is guided by mechanism, age, examination findings and local protocols (e.g. Ottawa shoulder rules if used).

Indication Imaging Study
Suspected fracture or dislocation Plain X-rays (AP, scapular Y, axillary or modified axillary views). CT for complex proximal humerus or glenoid fractures, or pre-operative planning.
Post-reduction assessment X-rays to confirm successful relocation and exclude associated fractures (e.g. greater tuberosity).
Suspected rotator cuff tear (especially traumatic in older patients) Initial X-rays to exclude fracture; ultrasound or MRI often arranged on an outpatient basis via orthopaedics.
Suspected infection or malignancy X-ray and further imaging (MRI, CT, ultrasound) guided by orthopaedics/medical teams.

Management of Common Shoulder Injuries

Management depends on the underlying pathology, patient age, functional demands and comorbidities. Core ED principles include analgesia, reduction where indicated, immobilisation and appropriate follow-up.

  • Anterior shoulder dislocation:
    • Prompt reduction using an appropriate technique with adequate analgesia/sedation.
    • Check and document neurovascular status pre- and post-reduction.
    • Post-reduction X-rays and immobilisation in a sling; early orthopaedic follow-up (especially in young, active patients).
  • Proximal humerus fracture:
    • Analgesia, sling or collar-and-cuff for most minimally displaced fractures.
    • Urgent ortho review for displaced fractures, fracture–dislocations, open injuries or neurovascular compromise.
  • AC joint injury:
    • Sling, ice, analgesia for low-grade injuries.
    • Higher grade separations or those with major deformity may require orthopaedic assessment.
  • Suspected septic arthritis:
    • Urgent orthopaedic review, bloods and cultures, IV antibiotics and likely surgical washout.
  • Rotator cuff tears (especially acute traumatic):
    • Analgesia, sling for comfort, encourage early pendulum exercises if stable.
    • Arrange orthopaedic or shoulder clinic follow-up – early recognition in older patients is important.

Patient Advice & Follow-Up

  • Explain the likely diagnosis and expected recovery timeline.
  • Encourage gentle range of motion exercises as advised (to reduce stiffness) once safe.
  • Provide clear return precautions: increasing pain, fever, redness, new swelling, numbness or weakness.
  • Arrange appropriate fracture or orthopaedic clinic follow-up, especially for dislocations, fractures or suspected cuff tears.
