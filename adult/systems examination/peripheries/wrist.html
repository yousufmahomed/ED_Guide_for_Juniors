Wrist Exam

Introduction

A wrist examination is performed in the emergency department to assess for any injuries or abnormalities in the wrist and hand. This examination is typically done on patients who have sustained a wrist injury or are experiencing wrist pain, and is essential for identifying any potential fractures or ligament injuries.

Examination Technique

The wrist examination involves the following steps:

  1. Inspect the wrist for any obvious abnormalities or deformities, such as swelling, bruising, or lacerations. Check for any penetrating injuries or foreign objects that may have entered the wrist.
  2. Palpate the wrist, assessing for tenderness, warmth, and swelling. Check for any bony abnormalities or crepitus.
  3. Assess the range of motion of the wrist, including flexion, extension, abduction, and adduction. Ask the patient to move their wrist in each direction, observing for any limitations or pain.
  4. Perform specific tests to assess for specific injuries or conditions, such as the Finkelstein's test for De Quervain's tenosynovitis, or the Tinel's sign for carpal tunnel syndrome.
  5. Perform a neurovascular examination, assessing for any signs of nerve or blood vessel injury. Test the patient's sensation and reflexes, as well as their pulse and blood pressure in the affected hand or fingers.
  6. Assess for any signs of carpal tunnel syndrome, such as tingling or numbness in the thumb, index, and middle fingers. This may be done using the Phalen's test, Tinel's sign, or other diagnostic maneuvers.
  7. Check for any deformities or abnormalities in the wrist or hand, such as swelling, bruising, or discoloration. Assess for any signs of arthritis, tendonitis, or other inflammatory conditions.
  8. Evaluate the range of motion of the wrist and fingers, including flexion, extension, and rotation. Ask the patient to make a fist, touch their fingers to their palm, and spread their fingers apart, observing for any limitations or pain.
  9. Perform any additional tests or maneuvers as indicated by the patient's symptoms or medical history. This may include provocative testing for specific conditions, such as De Quervain's tenosynovitis or trigger finger.

Imaging studies, such as X-rays or MRI, may be ordered if the examination reveals any abnormalities or if further evaluation is needed.

Common Wrist Injuries and Conditions

Some common wrist injuries and conditions that may be detected during a wrist examination include:

Injury/Condition Signs and Symptoms Differential Diagnosis Treatment
Fracture Pain, swelling, tenderness, deformity Sprain, dislocation, tendonitis, arthritis Immobilization, surgery, pain management
Sprain Pain, swelling, tenderness, limited range of motion Fracture, dislocation, tendonitis, arthritis Immobilization, rest, ice, compression, elevation, pain management
Tendonitis Pain, swelling, tenderness, limited range of motion, clicking or popping sensation Fracture, sprain, arthritis, ganglion cyst Rest, ice, compression, elevation, physical therapy, pain management
Arthritis Pain, swelling, stiffness, limited range of motion Rheumatoid arthritis, osteoarthritis, gout, infection Medications, physical therapy, surgery
De Quervain's tenosynovitis Pain and swelling on the thumb side of the wrist, difficulty gripping or making a fist Tendonitis, ganglion cyst, arthritis Rest, ice, compression, physical therapy, corticosteroid injection
Trigger finger Finger or thumb locks in a bent position, clicking The diagnosis of trigger finger is usually made based on a physical examination by a healthcare provider. Imaging studies such as X-rays or MRI may be ordered in some cases to rule out other conditions, such as arthritis or tendonitis. The treatment of trigger finger depends on the severity of the condition and the patient's symptoms.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of wrist injuries is usually made based on a physical examination by a healthcare provider. Imaging studies such as X-rays, MRI, or CT scans may be ordered in some cases to rule out fractures, dislocations, or ligament injuries.

Treatment

The treatment of wrist injuries depends on the severity of the injury and the patient's symptoms. Treatment options may include:

  • Rest: Resting the affected wrist and avoiding activities that may aggravate the injury
  • Immobilization: Wearing a cast or splint to immobilize the wrist and allow it to rest and heal
  • Physical therapy: Performing exercises to improve wrist and hand strength and flexibility and promote healing
  • Medications: Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) or corticosteroid injections may be prescribed to reduce inflammation and pain
  • Surgery: In severe cases, surgery may be necessary to repair fractures, dislocations, or torn ligaments

Prevention

There are several measures that can be taken to prevent wrist injuries:

  • Using proper ergonomics and technique when performing activities that involve wrist and hand use
  • Taking frequent breaks during activities that involve prolonged wrist and hand use
  • Performing stretching exercises to improve wrist and hand flexibility
  • Wearing wrist guards or protective gear during activities that carry a risk of wrist injury
