Wrist Exam

Overview

The wrist examination in the Emergency Department aims to detect distal radius and ulna fractures, carpal fractures (especially scaphoid), ligament injuries, distal radioulnar joint (DRUJ) instability, tendon injuries and acute nerve compression. Many apparently “minor” wrist injuries can have important functional consequences if missed.

Mechanisms are often falls onto an outstretched hand (FOOSH), sports trauma, road traffic collisions or work-related injuries. Always examine the entire upper limb and consider associated elbow or hand injuries.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

Identify these early and escalate:

  • Neurovascular compromise: pale, cool hand, reduced capillary refill, absent or weak radial/ulnar pulses, new motor or sensory deficit.
  • Open fractures or high-energy crush injuries.
  • Compartment syndrome: severe pain out of proportion, pain on passive finger stretch, tense forearm compartments, altered sensation.
  • High-force perilunate / lunate injuries: marked swelling, severe pain, median nerve symptoms, reduced wrist motion.
  • Suspicion of scaphoid fracture (snuffbox tenderness) with normal initial X-rays.
  • Bite injuries or heavily contaminated wounds near joints or tendon sheaths.

Any red flag warrants early orthopaedic/hand surgery consultation and careful documentation of neurovascular status.

Focused History

  • Mechanism: FOOSH, direct blow, twisting injury, crush, machinery, sports, assault, bite, high-pressure injection.
  • Timing: onset, progression of pain and swelling, previous injuries or surgery to wrist/hand.
  • Symptoms: pain location (radial, ulnar, dorsal, volar), stiffness, locking, clicking, weakness or loss of grip strength.
  • Neurological symptoms: numbness, tingling, burning in fingers (median, ulnar or radial distributions).
  • Functional impact: ability to use the hand at work, drive, perform activities of daily living.
  • Medical background: diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, anticoagulation, osteopenia/osteoporosis, immunosuppression.
  • Tetanus status in open injuries.

Examination Technique

Examine both wrists and hands, comparing to the uninjured side. Remove rings early if swelling is anticipated.

1. Look

  • Inspect for swelling, bruising, deformity, scars or wounds.
  • Note wrist alignment and resting posture of the hand and fingers.
  • Look for obvious dorsal angulation of distal radius (Colles-type deformity) or volar deformity (Smith’s fracture).

2. Feel

Palpate gently, starting away from the painful area:

  • Distal radius and ulna: dorsal and volar aspects, radial styloid, ulnar styloid.
  • Carpal bones:
    • Scaphoid: anatomic snuffbox, scaphoid tubercle volarly, pain on axial thumb loading.
    • Lunate and capitate region dorsally.
    • Triquetrum, pisiform ulnarly.
  • DRUJ: palpate dorsal and volar aspects while pronating/supinating for instability or tenderness.
  • Assess for crepitus, step deformities or focal tenderness over ligaments.

3. Move – Range of Motion

  • Wrist flexion and extension.
  • Radial and ulnar deviation.
  • Forearm pronation and supination (with elbow at 90°).
  • Observe where pain occurs and whether movement is mechanically blocked.

4. Special Tests (Selected ED-Relevant)

  • Scaphoid suspicion: snuffbox tenderness, scaphoid tubercle tenderness, pain on axial loading of thumb.
  • De Quervain’s tenosynovitis: Finkelstein’s test (thumb in fist, ulnar deviation of wrist).
  • Carpal tunnel: Tinel’s sign at carpal tunnel, Phalen’s test (for teaching context – not always necessary acutely).
  • DRUJ instability: pain or “clunk” on pronation/supination with hand in neutral; compare with other side.

5. Neurovascular Examination

Document before and after any manipulation or splinting.

  • Sensation:
    • Median nerve – palmar thumb, index, middle and radial half of ring finger.
    • Ulnar nerve – little finger and ulnar half of ring finger.
    • Radial nerve – dorsum of hand over first web space.
  • Motor:
    • Median – thumb opposition, “OK” sign.
    • Ulnar – finger abduction/adduction.
    • Radial – wrist and finger extension, thumb extension.
  • Circulation: radial and ulnar pulses, capillary refill in each finger, hand temperature and colour.

Common ED Wrist Injuries and Conditions

The table below focuses on conditions most relevant to ED practice. Many require early orthopaedic/hand surgery follow-up even if apparently minor.

Condition Key Findings ED Pearls / Management Notes
Distal radius fracture (e.g. Colles) FOOSH, pain and swelling over distal radius, possible “dinner-fork” deformity, reduced wrist motion. Analgesia, immobilise in appropriate splint/backslab, check and document neurovascular status. Many stable fractures can be managed with outpatient ortho follow-up; unstable or significantly displaced fractures need urgent ortho review. Consider associated ulnar styloid fracture and DRUJ injury. Elderly patients may need falls and bone health assessment.
Suspected scaphoid fracture Snuffbox tenderness, scaphoid tubercle tenderness, pain on axial thumb loading, often normal initial X-ray. Treat as a fracture if suspicion is high: thumb spica or scaphoid cast/splint, analgesia and early fracture clinic follow-up for repeat imaging (X-ray or MRI as per local protocol). High risk of non-union and AVN if missed. State clearly in notes that X-ray may be normal and that scaphoid injury is suspected.
Perilunate / lunate dislocation High-energy trauma, marked pain and swelling, decreased range of motion, median nerve symptoms; subtle or gross carpal malalignment on X-ray. Orthopaedic emergency. Prompt recognition, splinting, analgesia, urgent ortho/hand surgery review; often requires urgent reduction and surgery. Look for “spilled teacup” sign or disruption of Gilula’s arcs on lateral X-ray. Easily missed.
Wrist sprain / ligamentous injury Pain and swelling without obvious fracture on X-ray, often following twisting or FOOSH. Exclude fractures (particularly scaphoid and carpal). Provide splint, analgesia and clear return advice. Persisting pain warrants re-evaluation and possible advanced imaging. Consider scapholunate injury if dorsal wrist pain, clicking or widening of scapholunate gap on X-ray.
De Quervain’s tenosynovitis Radial-sided wrist pain, worse with thumb movement and gripping, positive Finkelstein’s test. Analgesia, thumb spica splint, activity modification. Arrange outpatient follow-up (GP/ortho) for potential steroid injection or physio. Often related to repetitive thumb/wrist use; more chronic than acute trauma in many cases.
Carpal tunnel syndrome (acute exacerbation) Numbness/tingling in radial 3½ fingers, nocturnal symptoms, weakness of thumb abduction; in acute trauma may present after fracture or swelling. Exclude compartment syndrome. Splint wrist in neutral, analgesia and arrange follow-up. Acute severe neuropathy after fracture may need urgent orthopaedic review for decompression. Consider systemic contributors (diabetes, hypothyroidism, pregnancy, fluid overload).
Ganglion cyst Smooth, fluctuant swelling typically on dorsal wrist; may be asymptomatic or cause ache/weakness. ED role is reassurance and exclusion of more serious pathology. Analgesia if needed, and outpatient follow-up if symptomatic or functionally limiting. No emergency treatment usually required; aspiration or surgery is elective.

Diagnosis & Imaging

  • Plain X-rays: standard PA and lateral views; consider scaphoid views when scaphoid injury suspected.
  • Look carefully for subtle signs: cortical breaks, carpal alignment, scapholunate gap, lunate position on lateral film.
  • CT: useful for complex intra-articular fractures or when X-rays are equivocal.
  • MRI: best for occult fractures and soft tissue/ligament injury; usually arranged via orthopaedics.
  • Ultrasound: may assist with tendon injuries or detecting fluid collections where available.

Treatment Principles in the ED

  • Analgesia: oral or IV; consider regional blocks (e.g. haematoma block for fractures) where skilled and appropriate.
  • Immobilisation: splints or casts tailored to injury (e.g. volar forearm splint, thumb spica, sugar-tong); elevate the limb to reduce swelling.
  • Wound care: irrigate, debride, assess for foreign bodies; antibiotics for bites, open fractures and contaminated wounds.
  • Tetanus prophylaxis: update as indicated.
  • Reduction: for selected distal radius fractures and wrist dislocations as per local guidelines, with appropriate analgesia/sedation and post-reduction X-rays.
  • Documentation: carefully record neurovascular status before and after procedures or splinting.
  • Follow-up: arrange fracture or orthopaedic clinic review as appropriate; provide clear written return precautions (increasing pain, numbness, swelling, colour change or loss of function).

Prevention & Patient Education

  • Encourage use of wrist guards in high-risk sports (skateboarding, rollerblading, snowboarding).
  • Promote safe workplace practices and appropriate protective equipment.
  • Advise on ergonomics and regular breaks for repetitive wrist tasks.
  • Emphasise the need for re-assessment if pain or dysfunction persists despite normal initial X-rays.
