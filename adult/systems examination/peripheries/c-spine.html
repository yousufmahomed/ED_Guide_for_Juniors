Cervical Spine Exam

Overview

The cervical spine examination in the Emergency Department focuses on identifying clinically important cervical spine injuries and spinal cord compromise following trauma, as well as non-traumatic pathology such as infection, malignancy and degenerative disease.

A key principle is that you should not move the neck or test range of motion until it has been clinically cleared or adequately imaged. Maintain spinal precautions in any patient with suspected cervical injury.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

After primary survey (ABC), identify the following red flags:

  • High-risk mechanism: high-speed RTC, axial load (diving), fall from height, ejection from vehicle, pedestrian vs vehicle.
  • Neurological deficit: weakness, paraesthesia, sensory level, sphincter disturbance, priapism after trauma.
  • Midline cervical tenderness on palpation.
  • Altered mental status: GCS <15, confusion, agitation, intoxication.
  • Distracting injuries: major limb fractures, thoracoabdominal trauma, severe facial injuries.
  • Signs of spinal cord injury: hypotension with bradycardia (neurogenic shock), flaccid weakness, sensory loss, abnormal reflexes.
  • Non-traumatic red flags: fever, weight loss, known malignancy, severe night pain, immunosuppression, IV drug use, inflammatory arthritis.

If any red flag is present, maintain full spinal immobilisation and obtain urgent senior and orthopaedic/neurosurgical input.

Focused History

  • Mechanism of injury: exact mechanism, speed, height, direct blow vs axial load, associated head injury or loss of consciousness.
  • Onset of symptoms: immediate vs delayed neck pain, any initial paraesthesia or weakness.
  • Neurological symptoms: limb weakness, numbness, tingling, gait disturbance, bowel or bladder dysfunction.
  • Pain features: location (midline vs paraspinal), severity, radiation to shoulders or arms.
  • Associated injuries: head, facial, chest, abdominal or limb injuries that may distract from neck pain.
  • Non-traumatic context: fevers, weight loss, known cancer, RA, ankylosing spondylitis, IV drug use.
  • Medications & background: anticoagulants/antiplatelets, osteoporosis, previous cervical surgery or known cervical disease.

Cervical Spine Clearance (Clinical Decision Rules)

In alert, stable adults, validated decision rules (e.g. NEXUS, Canadian C-Spine Rule) help determine whether imaging is required. Local protocols may vary; always follow your hospital guideline.

In general, imaging is usually required if any of the following apply:

  • Midline cervical tenderness.
  • Focal neurological deficit.
  • Altered level of consciousness or intoxication.
  • Painful distracting injury.
  • High-risk mechanism or age ≥65 years (depending on the rule used).

Only when the cervical spine is clinically cleared using a validated rule and the patient demonstrates full, painless active range of motion is it safe to remove spinal precautions without imaging (per local policy).

Examination Technique

Important: In any patient with suspected cervical spine injury, maintain in-line stabilisation and do not assess active range of motion until the neck is cleared clinically or radiologically.

1. Look

  • Inspect for deformity, step-off, bruising, wounds, or swelling along the neck.
  • Note position of the head and neck, use of cervical collar, and presence of any torticollis.
  • Look for associated head/facial injuries and signs of trauma to the shoulders or thorax.

2. Feel

  • With in-line stabilisation, palpate the spinous processes from the occiput to T1 for midline tenderness or step deformities.
  • Palpate paraspinal muscles for spasm or tenderness.
  • Do not force movement; keep the neck in neutral alignment.

3. Neurological Examination

Perform and document a focused neurological exam of all four limbs:

  • Motor: test power in key myotomes (shoulder abduction, elbow flexion/extension, wrist extension, finger abduction, hip and knee movements, ankle dorsiflexion/plantarflexion).
  • Sensation: assess light touch and pinprick in dermatomal distribution from C2–S1.
  • Reflexes: biceps, brachioradialis, triceps, knee and ankle jerks; note hyperreflexia or clonus.
  • Upper motor neuron signs: Babinski, Hoffman’s sign, tone and coordination if spinal cord injury suspected.
  • Sphincter function: ask about urinary retention, incontinence, or loss of anal tone in severe or high lesions (usually with senior involvement).

4. Range of Motion (Only When Safe)

Assess active range of motion only if the cervical spine has been cleared by decision rules or imaging, and the patient has:

  • No midline tenderness.
  • No neurological deficits.
  • No intoxication or distracting injuries.

Then ask the patient to:

  • Flex and extend the neck (“chin to chest”, “look at the ceiling”).
  • Rotate to left and right (usually ≥45° to each side if using Canadian C-Spine criteria).
  • Laterally tilt ears towards shoulders.

Stop if any movement causes severe pain, dizziness, or new neurological symptoms.

Indications for Imaging Studies

CT has largely replaced plain X-rays for cervical spine assessment in many centres, particularly for moderate-to-high risk trauma. Follow your local imaging protocol.

Indication Imaging Study
High-risk mechanism of injury or age ≥65 years CT cervical spine (preferred in most trauma protocols). Plain X-rays may be used where CT is not available, but must adequately visualise C1–T1.
Midline cervical tenderness CT cervical spine or plain X-rays (per local guideline).
Any focal neurological deficit or suspected spinal cord injury CT cervical spine urgently; MRI for cord, ligamentous injury or epidural lesion as advised by neurosurgery/orthopaedics.
Altered mental status, intoxication, or distracting injuries CT cervical spine if the neck cannot be clinically cleared.
Non-traumatic red flags (infection, malignancy, inflammatory disease) MRI cervical spine (often after initial plain films or CT), guided by specialist advice.

Management of Cervical Spine Injuries

Management depends on the nature and severity of injury, but several ED principles apply:

  • Immobilisation: maintain in-line stabilisation and appropriate spinal precautions (collar, blocks, tape or vacuum mattress) until the cervical spine is cleared.
  • Pain management: multimodal analgesia (paracetamol, NSAIDs if appropriate, opioids), muscle relaxants when safe and after serious injury is excluded.
  • Neuroprotection: maintain adequate blood pressure and oxygenation; avoid hypotension and hypoxia, which worsen spinal cord outcomes.
  • Surgical management: unstable fractures, dislocations, cord compression or progressive neurology require urgent orthopaedic/neurosurgical review and often surgical stabilisation or decompression.
  • Conservative management: stable fractures or soft tissue injuries may be managed with collars, analgesia and outpatient follow-up, as guided by specialists.
  • Rehabilitation: early involvement of physiotherapy and rehabilitation teams is crucial once the injury is stabilised.

Patient Advice & Follow-Up

  • Explain the diagnosis (or lack of structural injury if cleared) and expected recovery.
  • Provide advice on collar use where appropriate, and gentle mobilisation exercises if safe.
  • Give clear return precautions: increasing neck pain, new weakness, numbness, gait disturbance, or bladder/bowel symptoms.
  • Arrange follow-up with orthopaedics, neurosurgery or primary care as indicated by imaging and clinical findings.
