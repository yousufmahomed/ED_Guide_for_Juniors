Lumbar Spine Exam

Overview

The lumbar spine examination in the Emergency Department aims to differentiate benign mechanical back pain from serious pathology such as cauda equina syndrome, spinal fractures, infection, malignancy and significant radiculopathy.

In trauma, treat the lumbar spine as part of the whole spine and maintain spinal precautions until serious injury is excluded. In non-traumatic back pain, a focused history and examination help identify red flags that warrant urgent imaging or specialist input.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

After ABC stabilisation, screen for serious pathology:

  • Trauma with high-risk mechanism: fall from height, high-speed RTC, axial load, direct blow to back.
  • Cauda equina features: urinary retention or incontinence, faecal incontinence, saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg weakness or numbness.
  • Severe or progressive neurological deficit: motor weakness, foot drop, widespread sensory loss, gait disturbance.
  • Infection red flags: fever, rigors, recent systemic infection, IV drug use, immunosuppression, spinal tenderness.
  • Malignancy red flags: known cancer, unexplained weight loss, night pain, age >50 with new severe back pain.
  • Inflammatory or structural disease: known ankylosing spondylitis or other spondyloarthropathy, minor trauma with disproportionate pain.
  • Systemic illness: malaise, night sweats, generalised weakness.

Any red flag should prompt early senior review and consideration of urgent MRI or CT, according to local pathways.

Focused History

  • Onset & mechanism: sudden vs gradual; traumatic vs non-traumatic; heavy lifting, twisting, direct blow, fall.
  • Pain characteristics: location (midline vs paraspinal), radiation (buttock, posterior thigh, below knee), severity and pattern (night pain, constant vs mechanical).
  • Neurological symptoms: numbness, tingling, weakness, falls, gait disturbance, difficulty climbing stairs.
  • Bowel and bladder function: retention, incontinence, change in urinary stream, loss of urge, constipation or faecal incontinence.
  • Saddle region symptoms: altered sensation around perineum, genitalia, inner thighs.
  • Red flag context: history of cancer, TB, HIV, recent infection, weight loss, IV drug use, immunosuppression.
  • Past history: previous back problems, surgery, fractures, osteoporosis, steroid use.
  • Function: ability to mobilise, work, perform activities of daily living.

Examination Technique

Examine the spine in the context of the whole patient. In trauma, maintain spinal precautions until serious injury is excluded. In non-traumatic back pain, encourage patient-led movement as tolerated.

1. Look

  • Inspect the lumbar region for deformity, swelling, bruising, scars or step-offs.
  • Observe posture and gait (if safe): antalgic gait, stooping, difficulty standing upright.
  • Look for muscle wasting or asymmetry in buttocks and lower limbs.

2. Feel

  • Palpate spinous processes from lower thoracic to sacrum for midline tenderness or deformity.
  • Palpate paraspinal muscles for spasm or trigger points.
  • Assess sacroiliac joint region for tenderness if inflammatory or sacroiliac pathology suspected.

3. Range of Motion (non-traumatic cases)

In non-traumatic back pain without red flags, assess active movement:

  • Flexion: ask patient to bend forward, noting range and pain.
  • Extension: lean back gently, noting restriction or pain.
  • Lateral flexion: side-bending left and right.
  • Rotation: rotated trunk with pelvis stabilised.

Stop if movement triggers severe pain, weakness, or neurological symptoms.

4. Neurological Examination of Lower Limbs

Assess both legs and compare sides:

  • Motor strength:
    • Hip flexion/extension.
    • Knee extension (L3–L4) and flexion.
    • Ankle dorsiflexion (L4–L5) and plantarflexion (S1).
    • Great toe dorsiflexion (L5).
  • Sensation: light touch and pinprick across dermatomes L1–S2 (anterior thigh, medial leg, lateral leg, dorsum of foot, sole, heel, perineum).
  • Reflexes: patellar (L3–L4) and Achilles (S1); note reduced or brisk responses and clonus.
  • Upper motor neuron signs: Babinski, tone and coordination if cord involvement suspected.
  • Perineal sensation & anal tone: assess gently if cauda equina suspected (usually with chaperone and senior help).

5. Nerve Root / Radicular Tests (where appropriate)

  • Straight Leg Raise (SLR): with patient supine, raise the straight leg; radicular pain radiating below the knee between ~30–70° suggests nerve root irritation (commonly L5/S1).
  • Crossed SLR: radicular pain in the symptomatic leg when the opposite leg is raised is highly specific for disc prolapse.
  • Femoral stretch test: prone or side-lying, flex the knee and extend the hip; anterior thigh pain suggests higher lumbar root involvement (L2–L4).

Perform these tests only if safe, and stop if they cause severe pain or neurological deterioration.

Indications for Imaging Studies

Most acute, non-specific low back pain without red flags does not require immediate imaging. Imaging is reserved for trauma, red flags or persistent/progressive symptoms.

Indication Imaging Study
Suspected cauda equina syndrome Urgent MRI of the lumbar spine (or CT myelogram if MRI unavailable), with immediate neurosurgical/orthopaedic consultation.
Progressive or severe neurological deficit MRI lumbar spine to assess for disc herniation, stenosis, tumour, or abscess.
Suspected infection (e.g. discitis, epidural abscess) MRI lumbar spine; consider blood cultures and inflammatory markers.
Known malignancy or strong suspicion of spinal metastases MRI lumbar spine (and further imaging as guided by oncology/surgery).
Trauma with suspected lumbar fracture CT lumbar spine (often as part of CT trauma series); plain X-rays where CT is not available.
Persistent severe back pain without red flags, not improving with conservative care Usually arranged as outpatient imaging (X-ray or MRI) according to local guidelines rather than in the ED.

Management of Lumbar Spine Conditions in the ED

Management depends on the underlying cause, but general principles include:

  • Analgesia: stepwise approach with paracetamol, NSAIDs (if not contraindicated), short-term opioids for severe pain, and muscle relaxants where appropriate.
  • Activity guidance: encourage early gentle mobilisation in simple mechanical back pain; avoid prolonged bed rest.
  • Spinal precautions: in trauma or suspected serious pathology, maintain spinal precautions until imaging and specialist review.
  • Specific emergencies:
    • Cauda equina syndrome – urgent MRI and emergency surgical decompression.
    • Spinal infection – early antibiotics after appropriate cultures and urgent specialist input.
    • Malignant cord compression – urgent imaging, steroids as per local protocol and oncology/neurosurgical involvement.
    • Unstable fractures – immobilisation and operative or bracing management guided by specialists.
  • Non-specific mechanical back pain: analgesia, advice to remain active within limits, short period off heavy duties if needed, and GP/physio follow-up.

Patient Advice & Follow-Up

  • Explain the likely diagnosis (mechanical vs serious cause) and expected course.
  • Encourage gradual return to normal activity, avoidance of prolonged bed rest, and use of simple analgesia.
  • Provide clear return precautions: new or worsening leg weakness, numbness, saddle anaesthesia, difficulty passing urine, loss of urinary or faecal control, fever, night sweats, or rapidly escalating pain.
  • Arrange follow-up with primary care, physiotherapy, orthopaedics, neurosurgery or pain clinic as indicated.
