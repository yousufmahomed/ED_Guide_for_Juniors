Pelvic Exam

Overview

In the Emergency Department, a pelvic examination primarily aims to identify pelvic fractures, haemorrhage and associated urogenital or abdominal injury. The pelvis is a major potential source of life-threatening bleeding after trauma, and examination must be systematic but gentle, integrated into the primary and secondary survey.

A separate, focused genital or gynaecological examination may be required depending on the presentation (e.g. vaginal bleeding, suspected sexual assault, lower abdominal pain), and should follow local protocols with appropriate chaperoning and consent.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

Consider the pelvis early in any significant trauma. Red flags include:

  • Haemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension) with suspected pelvic injury.
  • Pelvic pain or deformity after high-energy mechanism (MVC, pedestrian struck, fall from height, crush injury).
  • Shortened or externally rotated limb without obvious hip fracture.
  • Perineal, scrotal, labial or flank bruising, or expanding haematoma.
  • Blood at urethral meatus, inability to pass urine, high-riding or non-palpable prostate on DRE.
  • Vaginal or rectal bleeding following trauma.
  • Open pelvic fractures or visible bone in perineum, vagina or rectum.

Any haemodynamically unstable patient with suspected pelvic fracture should have a pelvic binder applied early and be managed according to massive haemorrhage protocols.

Focused History

  • Mechanism of injury: high-speed collision, fall from height, crush, motorcycle or pedestrian vs vehicle, blast injury.
  • Pain: location (groin, buttock, lower back, suprapubic, hip), onset and severity.
  • Associated symptoms: inability to stand or walk, haematuria, vaginal/rectal bleeding, abdominal pain.
  • Past history: previous pelvic or spinal surgery, anticoagulation, bleeding disorders, pregnancy.

Examination Technique

The pelvic examination forms part of the secondary survey. Handle the pelvis gently to avoid dislodging clots and worsening bleeding.

1. Look

  • Inspect the abdomen, pelvis, perineum and proximal thighs for bruising, deformity, wounds, swelling or step deformity.
  • Look for perineal, scrotal or labial haematoma, blood at the urethral meatus, and any vaginal or rectal bleeding.
  • Check limb position: external rotation, apparent shortening, or asymmetry may suggest pelvic ring injury.

2. Feel (Pelvic Stability)

Assess pelvic stability only once if it has not yet been done and only in relatively stable patients. Avoid repeated “rocking” of the pelvis.

  • Place hands over the anterior superior iliac spines (ASIS).
  • Apply gentle medial–lateral compression and, if needed, gentle antero-posterior compression over the iliac crests.
  • Stop immediately if this reproduces pain or obvious movement, and apply a pelvic binder if not already in place.

3. Perineal, Genital and Rectal Examination

These components are performed where indicated by mechanism and findings, with consent and a chaperone:

  • Perineum and external genitalia: look for lacerations, haematoma, bleeding or urethral injury.
  • Digital rectal examination (DRE): check for high-riding or non-palpable prostate, gross blood, abnormal tone or bony fragments.
  • Vaginal examination (where indicated): assess for lacerations, bleeding or open fracture communication; usually performed with senior supervision.

4. Neurovascular Assessment

  • Check femoral, popliteal and distal pulses, limb temperature and capillary refill.
  • Assess lower limb motor and sensory function (femoral, sciatic and peroneal distributions).

Pelvic Binder & Early Management

In haemodynamically unstable trauma patients with suspected pelvic fractures:

  • Apply a pelvic binder at the level of the greater trochanters (not the iliac crests).
  • Initiate massive transfusion protocol where appropriate (balanced blood products rather than crystalloids).
  • Avoid unnecessary log-rolling or repeated manipulation of the pelvis.
  • Consider REBOA, angio-embolisation or pelvic packing in centres where available, in conjunction with surgical teams.

Early mechanical stabilisation, haemorrhage control and rapid imaging are key to improving survival in major pelvic trauma.

Investigations

  • X-ray pelvis (AP view): rapid screening in trauma; may show pelvic ring disruption, sacral fractures or hip dislocation.
  • CT scan with contrast: gold standard for evaluating pelvic fractures and associated injuries in stable or stabilised patients.
  • FAST / eFAST: identify intraperitoneal bleeding; remember major pelvic bleeding may be retroperitoneal and not visible on FAST.
  • Retrograde urethrogram: indicated if urethral injury is suspected (blood at meatus, high-riding prostate, perineal haematoma).
  • Laboratory tests: full blood count, coagulation profile, cross-match, lactate, arterial blood gas in major trauma.

Prevention & Longer-Term Considerations

Many pelvic injuries are not preventable at individual level, but risk can be reduced through:

  • Seatbelt use, appropriate child restraints and road safety measures.
  • Occupational safety in high-risk environments (mining, construction, industry).
  • Falls-prevention strategies in older adults (bone health, home safety, vision care).

Survivors of significant pelvic trauma often require multidisciplinary follow-up including orthopaedics, physiotherapy, urology, gynaecology and sometimes pelvic floor physiotherapy for long-term pain, mobility and continence issues.
