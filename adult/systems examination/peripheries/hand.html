Hand Exam

Overview

The hand examination in the Emergency Department aims to detect fractures, dislocations, tendon and nerve injuries, vascular compromise and infection. Small wounds can hide major damage, so a systematic approach and meticulous documentation are essential.

Always examine the hand in the context of the entire upper limb and mechanism of injury, and remember that missed tendon or nerve injuries can lead to lifelong disability.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

Identify and act urgently if any of the following are present:

  • Ischaemia: pale, cold hand with delayed capillary refill, absent pulses.
  • Compartment syndrome: severe pain out of proportion, pain on passive stretch, tense compartments, altered sensation.
  • Open fractures or gross contamination (farm injuries, machinery).
  • Fight bites / clenched fist injuries: lacerations over MCP joints after punching a mouth.
  • Flexor tendon sheath infection (flexor tenosynovitis): Kanavel’s signs – fusiform swelling, pain on passive extension, finger held in flexion, tenderness along flexor sheath.
  • High-pressure injection injuries (paint, grease, etc.).
  • Suspected major nerve injury with loss of motor function or dense anaesthesia.

Any red flag should prompt early hand surgery / orthopaedic consultation and urgent antibiotics if infection is suspected.

Focused History

  • Mechanism: crush, laceration (glass, knife), saw injury, bite, fall, sports, industrial machinery, injection injury.
  • Timing: time of injury, delay to presentation, any first aid given.
  • Dominant hand and occupation/hobbies (instrumental for function and medico-legal implications).
  • Symptoms: pain, weakness, numbness or tingling, loss of function, locking or triggering.
  • Wound specifics: depth, contamination (soil, saliva), foreign body, glove use.
  • Past history: diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, immunosuppression, previous hand surgery.
  • Tetanus status and allergies.

Examination Technique

Examine both hands where possible, comparing with the uninjured side. Remove rings early if swelling is anticipated.

1. Look

  • Inspect the skin, nails and alignment of fingers and thumb.
  • Look for swelling, bruising, deformity, lacerations and nail bed injuries.
  • Assess finger posture at rest; a flexed or dropped digit may indicate tendon rupture.

2. Feel

  • Palpate along the bones (phalanges, metacarpals, carpal region) for tenderness and crepitus.
  • Palpate over joints and along tendons, especially flexor and extensor surfaces.
  • Assess temperature, localised tenderness and fluctuance in suspected infection.

3. Move – Joints and Tendons

Test each joint in isolation where possible, checking both active and passive motion.

  • Wrist: flexion, extension, radial and ulnar deviation.
  • MCP, PIP, DIP joints: flexion and extension of each finger; compare with the opposite hand.
  • Thumb: flexion/extension, abduction/adduction, opposition to little finger.
  • Note any lag, inability to fully extend/flex, or painful restriction.

4. Specific Functional Tests

  • Flexor tendons:
    • FDP – hold PIP in extension, ask patient to flex DIP.
    • FDS – hold adjacent fingers in extension, ask patient to flex PIP of the finger being tested.
  • Extensor tendons: ask patient to extend each joint against resistance; look for extensor lag or mallet deformity.
  • De Quervain’s tenosynovitis: Finkelstein’s test (thumb in fist, ulnar deviation of wrist).
  • Trigger finger: palpable nodule and catching/locking during flexion–extension.
  • Carpal tunnel (median nerve): Tinel’s sign at carpal tunnel, Phalen’s test (optional for ED teaching context).

5. Neurovascular Examination

Document sensory and motor function before and after any procedure.

  • Sensation:
    • Median nerve – palmar aspect of thumb, index, middle and radial half of ring finger.
    • Ulnar nerve – little finger and ulnar half of ring finger, dorsal ulnar hand.
    • Radial nerve – dorsum of hand over first web space.
  • Motor:
    • Median – “OK” sign (FPL and FDP to index), thumb opposition.
    • Ulnar – finger abduction/adduction (interossei), Froment’s sign (paper between thumb and index).
    • Radial – wrist and MCP extension (“thumbs up”).
  • Circulation: radial and ulnar pulses, capillary refill in each finger, hand temperature and colour.

Common ED Hand Injuries and Conditions

The table below highlights key ED-relevant conditions. Many require urgent or early hand surgery review.

Condition Key Findings ED Pearls / Management
Phalangeal / metacarpal fractures Pain, swelling, deformity, rotational malalignment (fingers cross when making a fist), tenderness over bone. Check rotation carefully. Immobilise in safe hand position (MCP flexed, IP joints straight) and refer to orthopaedics/hand surgery. Open or intra-articular fractures need urgent review.
Dislocations (e.g. PIP, MCP) Obvious deformity, inability to move joint, usually after hyperextension or axial load. Neurovascular assessment before and after reduction. Post-reduction X-ray to exclude fracture. Splint in functional position and arrange follow-up.
Flexor tendon laceration Deep palmar wound, inability to flex DIP/PIP of affected finger, painful or weak flexion. High risk of missed injury. Do not ignore small wounds in flexor zones. Irrigate, splint, give antibiotics and tetanus as indicated, and refer urgently to hand surgery.
Extensor tendon injury Inability to actively extend finger or thumb (e.g. mallet finger, boutonnière), dorsal laceration. Splint in appropriate position (e.g. DIP in extension for mallet). Many require hand clinic follow-up; some need surgical repair.
Flexor tenosynovitis Kanavel’s signs: fusiform swelling, finger held in flexion, tenderness along flexor sheath, pain on passive extension; often systemic features. Orthopaedic emergency. Start IV antibiotics, elevate limb and obtain urgent hand surgery review for possible surgical washout.
Human bite / “fight bite” Small laceration over MCP joint (often 3rd/4th), usually after punching someone in the mouth; may look innocuous. High infection risk with joint involvement. Irrigate thoroughly, give broad-spectrum antibiotics and tetanus, avoid primary closure, and refer to hand surgery.
De Quervain’s tenosynovitis Pain and swelling over radial styloid, worse with thumb movement, positive Finkelstein’s test. ED role is exclusion of other pathology, basic analgesia, splinting and outpatient follow-up for steroid injection / physio if needed.
Carpal tunnel syndrome (acute flare) Numbness/tingling in radial 3½ fingers, nocturnal symptoms, positive Tinel’s/Phalen’s; in acute setting may follow trauma or swelling. Exclude compartment syndrome. Splint wrist in neutral, analgesia and arrange follow-up. Acute severe cases with weakness or trauma may need urgent surgical review.
Trigger finger Finger catches or locks during flexion–extension, palpable nodule at A1 pulley, often painful. ED role mainly reassurance, analgesia and splinting if needed. Arrange outpatient follow-up for steroid injection or surgical release.

Diagnosis & Imaging

  • Plain X-rays: essential for suspected fractures or dislocations; obtain at least two views and consider special views for scaphoid or specific injuries.
  • Ultrasound: may help with tendon injuries or superficial foreign bodies where available.
  • MRI / CT: usually not ED tests; arranged via orthopaedics/hand surgery for complex or occult injuries.
  • Blood tests: FBC, CRP, cultures if significant infection suspected (e.g. tenosynovitis, deep-space infection, bite wounds with systemic features).

Treatment Principles in the ED

  • Analgesia: oral or IV; consider regional blocks or digital nerve blocks for painful procedures.
  • Irrigation and debridement: especially for contaminated wounds and bites.
  • Immobilisation: splints or casts in a safe hand position; elevate the hand to reduce swelling.
  • Antibiotics: for bites, open fractures, flexor sheath infections, contaminated wounds and high-risk patients.
  • Tetanus prophylaxis: update as indicated.
  • Referral and follow-up: low threshold for hand surgery review in tendon, nerve, complex fracture or infection cases; provide clear written instructions and safety-net advice.

Prevention & Patient Education

  • Encourage use of appropriate protective equipment at work and during sports.
  • Promote safe tool and machinery use, with adherence to occupational safety standards.
  • Explain the importance of early review if pain, swelling, numbness or loss of function worsens.
  • In chronic overuse conditions, discuss basic ergonomics, rest and gradual return to activity.
