Hand Exam

Introduction

A hand examination is performed in the medical setting to assess for any injuries or abnormalities in the hand, including the palm, dorsum of hand, fingers, and thumb. This examination is typically done on patients who have sustained a hand injury or are experiencing hand pain or dysfunction, and is essential for identifying any potential fractures, ligament injuries, or nerve damage.

Examination Technique

The hand examination involves the following steps:

  1. Inspect the hand for any obvious abnormalities or deformities, such as swelling, bruising, or lacerations. Check for any penetrating injuries or foreign objects that may have entered the hand.
  2. Palpate the hand, assessing for tenderness, warmth, and swelling. Check for any bony abnormalities or crepitus.
  3. Assess the range of motion of the hand, including flexion, extension, abduction, and adduction of the wrist, fingers, and thumb. Ask the patient to move their hand in each direction, observing for any limitations or pain.
  4. Perform specific tests to assess for specific injuries or conditions, such as the Finkelstein's test for De Quervain's tenosynovitis, or the Tinel's sign for carpal tunnel syndrome.
  5. Perform a neurovascular examination, assessing for any signs of nerve or blood vessel injury. Test the patient's sensation and reflexes, as well as their pulse and blood pressure in the affected hand or fingers.
  6. Assess for any deformities or abnormalities in the hand and fingers, such as swelling, bruising, or discoloration. Check for any penetrating injuries or foreign objects that may have entered the hand.
  7. Assess the range of motion of the fingers and thumb, including flexion, extension, abduction, and adduction. Ask the patient to move their fingers and thumb in each direction, observing for any limitations or pain.
  8. Perform specific tests to assess for specific injuries or conditions, such as the Allen's test for arterial insufficiency or the Froment's sign for ulnar nerve palsy.
  9. Assess for any signs of tendonitis, such as pain, swelling, tenderness, and limited range of motion. Check for any clicking or popping sensations that may be present.
  10. Check for any signs of arthritis, such as pain, swelling, stiffness, and limited range of motion. Ask the patient about any history of autoimmune disease or family history of arthritis.
  11. Evaluate the grip strength of the hand, asking the patient to squeeze your hand as tightly as possible. This can help assess for any weakness or motor deficits.
  12. Perform any additional tests or maneuvers as indicated by the patient's symptoms or medical history. This may include provocative testing for specific conditions, such as De Quervain's tenosynovitis or trigger finger.

Imaging studies, such as X-rays or MRI, may be ordered if the examination reveals any abnormalities or if further evaluation is needed.

Common Hand and Finger Injuries and Conditions

Some common hand and finger injuries and conditions that may be detected during an examination include:

Injury/Condition Signs and Symptoms Differential Diagnosis Treatment
Fracture Pain, swelling, tenderness, deformity Sprain, dislocation, tendonitis, arthritis Immobilization, surgery, pain management
Sprain Pain, swelling, tenderness, limited range of motion Fracture, dislocation, tendonitis, arthritis Immobilization, rest, ice, compression, elevation, pain management

Imaging studies such as X-rays or MRI may be ordered in some cases to rule out other conditions, such as arthritis or tendonitis. The treatment of trigger finger depends on the severity of the condition and the patient's symptoms.

Diagnosis

The diagnosis of hand injuries and conditions is usually made based on a physical examination by a healthcare provider. Imaging studies such as X-rays, MRI, or CT scans may be ordered in some cases to rule out fractures, dislocations, or ligament injuries.

Treatment

The treatment of hand injuries and conditions depends on the severity of the injury or condition and the patient's symptoms. Treatment options may include:

  • Rest: Resting the affected hand and avoiding activities that may aggravate the injury or condition
  • Immobilization: Wearing a cast or splint to immobilize the hand and allow it to rest and heal
  • Physical therapy: Performing exercises to improve hand and finger strength and flexibility and promote healing
  • Medications: Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) or corticosteroid injections may be prescribed to reduce inflammation and pain
  • Surgery: In severe cases, surgery may be necessary to repair fractures, dislocations, or torn ligaments

Prevention

There are several measures that can be taken to prevent hand injuries and conditions:

  • Using proper ergonomics and technique when performing activities that involve hand and finger use
  • Taking frequent breaks during activities that involve prolonged hand and finger use
  • Performing stretching exercises to improve hand and finger flexibility
  • Wearing hand and finger guards or protective gear during activities that carry a risk of hand or finger injury
