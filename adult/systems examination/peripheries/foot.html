Foot Exam

Overview

A careful foot examination in the Emergency Department is essential to detect fractures, soft-tissue injuries, neurovascular compromise and diabetic or infective pathology that can threaten mobility and limb viability. Always examine both the plantar and dorsal aspects of the foot and toes, and consider the foot in the context of the entire lower limb and mechanism of injury.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

Address ABC first, then focus on the limb. For the foot, be alert for:

  • Open fractures or obvious deformity of the forefoot, midfoot or hindfoot.
  • A pale, cool, pulseless or mottled foot – acute limb ischaemia until proven otherwise.
  • Severe pain out of proportion to findings, especially with tense compartments or pain on passive toe stretch – consider compartment syndrome.
  • Diabetic foot with deep ulcers, necrosis, crepitus or systemic features – possible necrotising infection or osteomyelitis.
  • Hot, swollen, very painful joint with reduced movement and fever – septic arthritis until proven otherwise.
  • High-energy mechanism (fall from height, crush injury, MVC) – think of calcaneal, talar and Lisfranc injuries.

Any red flag requires urgent senior review and early orthopaedic +/- vascular involvement.

Focused History

  • Mechanism: twisting, inversion/eversion, direct blow, crush, fall from height, repetitive overuse, foreign body, burn, puncture wound.
  • Onset and course: sudden vs gradual, acute trauma vs insidious pain, ability to continue walking or weight-bearing.
  • Site of pain: heel, arch, midfoot, forefoot, toes, plantar vs dorsal surface.
  • Systemic features: fever, rigors, malaise (infection); night pain, weight loss (malignancy or inflammatory disease).
  • Risk factors: diabetes, peripheral vascular disease, neuropathy, immunosuppression, gout, inflammatory arthritis, anticoagulation.
  • Functional impact: distance able to walk, ability to stand, work, wear shoes.
  • Previous problems: recurrent ulcers, previous fractures or surgery, prior foot deformities.

Examination Technique

Expose both feet fully. Examine the patient standing (if able) and then seated or supine with the foot supported at a comfortable height.

1. Look

  • Observe gait, ability to weight-bear and stance (antalgic gait, toe-walking, avoiding heel strike).
  • Inspect skin for ulcers, callosities, blisters, colour change, scars, deformity and nail changes.
  • Check for swelling, bruising or deformity of toes, forefoot, midfoot and hindfoot.
  • Assess alignment: flatfoot, pes cavus, hallux valgus, claw toes, hammertoes.
  • Inspect the plantar surface carefully in diabetics and neuropathic patients.

2. Feel

Palpate systematically, from proximal to distal, comparing both feet:

  • Bony landmarks: calcaneus, plantar fascia origin, talus, navicular, cuboid, cuneiforms, metatarsal shafts and heads, bases of metatarsals, phalanges.
  • Special attention to:
    • Base of the 5th metatarsal and navicular (Ottawa foot rule sites).
    • Lisfranc joint region (tarsometatarsal joints) – tenderness suggests Lisfranc injury.
    • Metatarsal heads and interdigital spaces (Morton’s neuroma).
  • Soft tissues: plantar fascia, Achilles insertion, peroneal tendons, posterior tibial tendon, interdigital spaces for neuroma.
  • Assess temperature and compare sides – increased warmth may suggest infection, gout or inflammatory arthritis.

3. Move

Assess both active and passive range of motion:

  • Ankle movements: dorsiflexion and plantarflexion (also covered on the ankle page).
  • Subtalar joint: inversion and eversion.
  • Midfoot and forefoot: flexion/extension of toes, movement at metatarsophalangeal joints.
  • Note pain, restriction or crepitus with any movement.

4. Special Tests

  • Mulder’s sign: squeeze forefoot while pressing the web space (usually 3rd–4th); a click and pain suggests Morton’s neuroma.
  • Calcaneal squeeze: squeeze calcaneus from both sides; pain suggests calcaneal fracture or severe plantar fasciitis.
  • Plantar fascia tenderness: localised pain at the medial calcaneal tubercle and along the plantar fascia, often worse with first steps in the morning.
  • Toe stress and axial loading: pain with axial load may suggest phalangeal or metatarsal fracture.
  • Ankle and Achilles tests: Thomas/Thompson test for Achilles rupture (see ankle page for detail).

Neurovascular Examination of the Lower Limb

A focused neurovascular assessment must be performed and documented for all significant foot and ankle injuries, and before and after any manipulation, reduction or splinting.

1. Circulation

  • Inspection: colour (pink vs pale, mottled or cyanosed), swelling, visible wounds or haematoma.
  • Temperature: compare both limbs with the back of your hand – a cool foot is concerning.
  • Pulses:
    • Dorsalis pedis: dorsum of the foot between the 1st and 2nd metatarsals.
    • Posterior tibial: behind the medial malleolus.
    Comment on presence, symmetry and quality (strong/weak, absent).
  • Capillary refill: press a toe nail bed – normal refill is < 2 seconds.

2. Sensation

Test light touch or pin-prick, comparing with the uninjured side:

  • Superficial peroneal nerve: dorsum of the foot.
  • Deep peroneal nerve: first web space between the 1st and 2nd toes.
  • Tibial nerve: sole of the foot.
  • Sural nerve: lateral border of the foot.
  • Saphenous nerve: medial ankle and medial leg.

Note any numbness, tingling or altered sensation and whether it is new since the injury or any procedure.

3. Motor Function

  • Deep peroneal nerve: ankle dorsiflexion and big toe extension (“point toes up, lift the big toe”).
  • Tibial nerve: plantarflexion (“press down like a car pedal”) and toe flexion.
  • Check proximal function (knee and hip movements) if you suspect more proximal injury or nerve root involvement.

4. Red Flags

  • Pale, cool, pulseless or mottled foot.
  • Rapidly increasing pain, especially with passive toe stretch (possible compartment syndrome).
  • New or progressive motor weakness or loss of sensation.

Any neurovascular abnormality after trauma or reduction is an emergency – urgently involve orthopaedics and vascular surgery where available.

5. Documentation

Document clearly:

  • Pulses (present/absent, Doppler if used) and capillary refill.
  • Sensory findings in named nerve distributions.
  • Motor power (e.g. ankle dorsiflexion/plantarflexion, big toe extension).
  • Status before and after any procedure (manipulation, plaster, traction, etc.).

Common ED Foot Conditions

Condition Key Findings ED Pearls
Plantar fasciitis Heel and arch pain, often worse with first steps in the morning, tenderness at the medial calcaneal tubercle and along plantar fascia, normal neurovascular exam. Usually an outpatient diagnosis; exclude fracture or infection if atypical. Provide analgesia, stretching advice and appropriate follow-up rather than extensive ED imaging.
Metatarsal / phalangeal fractures Localised bony tenderness, swelling, bruising, pain on axial loading, sometimes deformity. Obtain X-rays when indicated. Assess alignment and joint involvement. Buddy strapping or stiff-soled shoe for stable fractures; refer displaced or intra-articular fractures to ortho.
Lisfranc (tarsometatarsal) injury Midfoot pain and swelling, plantar bruising, pain with forefoot stress, difficulty weight-bearing; may have subtle X-ray changes. High risk of chronic disability if missed. Get weight-bearing foot X-rays if possible. Low threshold for CT and early orthopaedic referral.
Morton’s neuroma Burning or shooting pain in forefoot, often between 3rd and 4th toes; positive Mulder’s sign, may report “pebble in the shoe” sensation. Usually managed electively. ED role is exclusion of acute injury and providing analgesia, footwear advice and outpatient referral.
Diabetic foot infection Ulcers, surrounding cellulitis, callus, foul discharge, reduced sensation, may have systemic signs of sepsis. Treat as limb- and life-threatening if deep or spreading. Early antibiotics, blood tests, X-ray to exclude osteomyelitis, vascular assessment and multidisciplinary input.
Gout / inflammatory arthritis Acute onset red, hot, very tender joint (often 1st MTP), reduced range of motion, may have history of similar attacks. Exclude septic arthritis in high-risk patients (fever, immunosuppression, marked systemic features). Treat pain and arrange follow-up for long-term management.
Septic arthritis Intensely painful, swollen joint with severe restriction of movement, often febrile and systemically unwell. Orthopaedic emergency. Urgent blood tests, cultures, antibiotics and ortho referral for possible surgical washout.

Prevention & Patient Education

Many foot problems are preventable or can be minimised with simple measures. Reinforce the following, especially in diabetic or high-risk patients:

  • Wear well-fitting shoes with adequate support and cushioning; avoid tight or high-heeled shoes for long periods.
  • Use appropriate footwear for sport or work, and avoid walking barefoot in unsafe environments.
  • Gradually increase activity levels and training intensity to reduce overuse injuries.
  • Stretch calf muscles and plantar fascia regularly, particularly in runners and people on their feet all day.
  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce load on feet and lower limbs.
  • For diabetics: daily self-inspection of feet, careful nail care, avoid barefoot walking, and early presentation for any blister, ulcer or break in the skin.
  • Avoid smoking, which worsens peripheral vascular disease and delays healing.

Provide written discharge instructions and clear safety-net advice on when to return (e.g. worsening pain, spreading redness, fever, new numbness or difficulty weight-bearing).
