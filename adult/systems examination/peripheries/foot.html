Foot Exam

Introduction

A thorough foot examination is important in the emergency department to evaluate any injury or pathology that can affect a patient's mobility and quality of life. The exam should include an assessment of the plantar and dorsal aspects of the foot and toes, as well as the surrounding soft tissue and neurovascular structures. In this guide, we will discuss the key components of a foot examination and the common conditions that can be identified through this assessment.

Components of a Foot Examination

The following components should be included in a comprehensive foot examination:

  • History: Start by asking the patient about their current symptoms, as well as any relevant medical history and recent changes in their activity level. This information can provide valuable clues to guide your examination.
  • Visual inspection: Observe the patient's gait and posture, and inspect the foot for any swelling, deformities, or discoloration. Pay particular attention to the plantar and dorsal aspects of the foot, as well as the toes.
  • Palpation: Use your hands to feel for any areas of tenderness, swelling, or bony prominences. Check for any masses, such as ganglion cysts, and assess the temperature of the foot to rule out any signs of infection or neurological issues.
  • Check for any deformities or abnormalities in the foot, including bunions, hammertoes, or flat feet.
  • Assess the patient's gait and balance, looking for any abnormalities or difficulties in walking.
  • Perform range of motion exercises on the foot and toes to assess flexibility and detect any limitations in movement.
  • Palpate the foot and toes, checking for any areas of tenderness, swelling, or nodules, which may indicate the presence of a mass or tumor.
  • Perform a neurological exam of the foot and toes, assessing for any signs of neuropathy, such as loss of sensation or reflexes.
  • Check the patient's vascular status by evaluating the pulses in the foot and checking for any signs of poor circulation, such as coolness, discoloration, or slow healing wounds.
  • Perform specific tests for certain conditions, such as the Mulder's sign for Morton's neuroma or the Thompson test for Achilles tendon rupture.

Common Foot Conditions

There are many different foot conditions that can affect patients. Some of the most common conditions that are encountered during a foot examination in the emergency department include:

Condition Symptoms Treatment
Plantar fasciitis Pain on the bottom of the foot, particularly in the heel; stiffness or tenderness in the arch of the foot Rest, ice, stretching exercises, physical therapy, orthotics, corticosteroid injections, surgery (in severe cases)
Morton's neuroma Sharp, burning pain in the ball of the foot; tingling or numbness in the toes Rest, ice, padding or arch supports, corticosteroid injections, surgery (in severe cases)
Plantar warts Small, fleshy growths on the bottom of the foot; tenderness or pain when walking or standing Topical medications, cryotherapy, laser therapy, surgery (in severe cases)
Ingrown toenails Tenderness, swelling, or redness in the toe; pain or discomfort when pressure is applied Soaking the foot, wearing comfortable shoes, trimming toenails properly, antibiotics (if infected), surgery (in severe cases)
Plantar fascia rupture Sudden onset of pain and swelling in the bottom of the foot; difficulty walking or standing Rest, ice, immobilization, physical therapy, surgery (in severe cases)
Achilles tendonitis Pain and stiffness in the Achilles tendon; swelling, redness, or warmth in the area Rest, ice, compression, elevation, physical therapy, orthotics, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, corticosteroid injections, surgery (rare)

Prevention

There are several measures that can be taken to prevent foot injuries:

  • Wear properly fitting shoes that provide adequate support and cushioning.
  • Wear appropriate footwear for the activity being performed.
  • Gradually increase the intensity and duration of physical activity to avoid overuse injuries.
  • Stretch and warm up properly before exercise or physical activity.
  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce the risk of stress on the feet and lower extremities.
  • Avoid walking barefoot in public areas to prevent cuts, scrapes, and infections.
  • Inspect your feet regularly for any signs of injury or infection.
  • Keep toenails trimmed properly to avoid ingrown toenails.
  • Avoid smoking, as it can reduce blood flow to the feet and increase the risk of peripheral artery disease.
