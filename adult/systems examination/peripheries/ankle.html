Ankle Exam

Overview

The ankle examination in the Emergency Department focuses on rapidly identifying fractures, ligament injuries, tendon ruptures and neurovascular compromise. A structured approach helps you decide who needs imaging, who can be safely discharged and who requires urgent orthopaedic or vascular referral.

Always examine the ankle in the context of the entire limb and mechanism of injury. Missed ankle and midfoot injuries (e.g. syndesmosis, Lisfranc, calcaneal fractures) can cause lifelong disability.

Initial Priorities & Red Flags

Begin with ABC and a quick global trauma assessment. For the limb, look for:

  • Open fractures or gross deformity of the ankle or foot.
  • Pale, cold, pulseless or mottled foot – limb ischaemia until proven otherwise.
  • Severe pain out of proportion, especially with tense compartments – consider compartment syndrome.
  • Inability to move toes or loss of sensation – possible nerve injury or spinal lesion.
  • Suspected septic arthritis or cellulitis – hot, swollen joint with fever or systemic toxicity.
  • High-energy mechanism (fall from height, MVC) – think of associated tibial, calcaneal and midfoot injuries.

Any red flag should prompt immediate senior review and early involvement of orthopaedics/vascular surgery.

Focused History

  • Mechanism: inversion, eversion, axial load, fall from height, twisting, direct blow, sporting injury, road traffic collision.
  • Onset: immediate vs delayed swelling, ability to continue activity or weight-bearing.
  • Location of pain: lateral malleolus, medial malleolus, Achilles region, heel, midfoot, base of 5th metatarsal.
  • Previous ankle injuries/surgery: chronic instability, previous fractures, tendon pathology.
  • Systemic symptoms: fever, night pain, weight loss, inflammatory arthropathy, diabetes, immunosuppression.
  • Functional impact: ability to weight-bear immediately and on presentation, stairs, distance walked.
  • Red flag questions: paraesthesia, weakness, severe unrelenting pain, history of anticoagulation.

Examination Technique

Examine the patient standing if able, then seated or lying with the ankle exposed from knee to toes, comparing both sides.

1. Look

  • General: gait, antalgic posture, reluctance to weight-bear.
  • Swelling, bruising and deformity around malleoli, Achilles tendon, heel and midfoot.
  • Skin integrity: wounds, blisters, abrasions, open fractures.
  • Alignment: hindfoot varus/valgus, flatfoot deformity, high arch (pes cavus).

2. Feel

Palpate systematically, watching the patient’s face:

  • Bone points: distal tibia and fibula, medial and lateral malleolus, posterior edge of malleoli, base of 5th metatarsal, navicular, calcaneus, talus and midfoot joints.
  • Ligaments: ATFL and CFL (lateral), deltoid ligament (medial), syndesmosis (anterior tibiofibular region).
  • Tendons: Achilles tendon (gap, tenderness), peroneal tendons, posterior tibial tendon, plantar fascia origin at calcaneus.
  • Temperature and tenderness: compare both sides; note focal warmth or exquisite point tenderness.

3. Move

Assess both active and passive range of motion:

  • Dorsiflexion and plantarflexion at the ankle joint.
  • Inversion and eversion (subtalar joint).
  • Note pain, crepitus, restriction or apprehension.

4. Special Tests

  • Anterior drawer test: tests ATFL integrity (lateral instability).
  • Talar tilt test: lateral or medial ligament instability.
  • Squeeze test: compress tibia and fibula proximally – pain at syndesmosis suggests high ankle sprain.
  • External rotation stress test: pain at distal tibiofibular joint suggests syndesmotic injury.
  • Thompson test (Simmonds): squeeze calf; absent plantarflexion suggests Achilles tendon rupture.
  • Calcaneal squeeze: heel pain and tenderness may suggest calcaneal fracture or stress injury following axial load.

5. Neurovascular Examination

Always document neurovascular status before and after any manipulation, reduction or splinting.

  • Pulses: dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses; capillary refill in toes (<2 seconds).
  • Colour and temperature: pink, warm foot vs pale, mottled or cold.
  • Sensation:
    • Superficial peroneal nerve – dorsum of foot.
    • Deep peroneal nerve – web space between 1st and 2nd toes.
    • Tibial nerve – sole of foot.
    • Sural and saphenous nerves – lateral and medial borders of foot.
  • Motor: ankle dorsiflexion and plantarflexion, big toe extension and flexion.

When to X-ray: Ottawa Ankle & Foot Rules

Use the Ottawa rules to decide who needs plain radiographs – they are highly sensitive for clinically significant fractures.

  • Ankle X-ray is indicated if:
    • Pain in the malleolar zone and
    • Bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the lateral malleolus or medial malleolus, or
    • Inability to weight-bear both immediately and in the ED (four steps).
  • Foot X-ray is indicated if:
    • Pain in the midfoot zone and
    • Bone tenderness at the base of the 5th metatarsal or navicular, or
    • Inability to weight-bear both immediately and in the ED.

These rules are for patients with acute blunt trauma and a normal level of consciousness. Use clinical judgement in children, the elderly, high-energy trauma and patients with multiple injuries or intoxication.

Common ED Ankle & Foot Conditions

Condition Key Findings ED Pearls
Lateral ankle sprain Inversion mechanism, tenderness over ATFL/CFL, swelling and bruising laterally, stable joint, normal X-ray. Exclude fracture using Ottawa rules. Early protected mobilisation, RICE, analgesia. Recurrent sprains need physio and possible ortho follow-up.
Medial or bimalleolar fracture Medial pain or tenderness, often with deformity; may be associated with fibular fracture higher up. Check syndesmosis and proximal fibula (Maisonneuve fracture). Unstable fractures or talar shift require urgent ortho review and likely operative management.
Syndesmosis (high ankle) sprain Pain above ankle joint, positive squeeze or external rotation stress test, difficulty with push-off. Often missed. Low threshold for X-ray and ortho referral if suspected, especially in athletes or high-demand patients.
Achilles tendon rupture Sudden pain in posterior ankle, sometimes a “pop”, difficulty with plantarflexion, positive Thompson test, palpable gap in tendon. Can be subtle and misdiagnosed as “sprain”. Urgent ortho referral. Place in equinus splint and keep non-weight-bearing.
Calcaneal fracture Heel pain after axial load (fall from height), difficulty weight-bearing, heel swelling and bruising. Often associated with spinal or other lower limb fractures. Obtain appropriate X-ray views; CT may be required. Early ortho involvement is essential.
Lisfranc (midfoot) injury Midfoot pain and swelling, plantar ecchymosis, pain with forefoot stress, difficulty weight-bearing. High risk of chronic disability if missed. Obtain weight-bearing foot X-rays if possible and refer ortho early.
Septic arthritis / severe cellulitis Hot, very painful ankle, reduced movement, systemic features (fever, tachycardia), surrounding cellulitis. Treat as a limb-threatening emergency. Blood tests, cultures, early antibiotics and urgent ortho review for possible washout.

General ED Management Principles

Management depends on the diagnosis, stability of the joint and presence of red flags.

  • Analgesia: oral or IV; consider regional block in severe injuries if skilled and appropriate.
  • Immobilisation: functional brace or bandage for simple sprains; backslab or cast for fractures or significant instability.
  • Weight-bearing status: from full weight-bearing with support to non-weight-bearing with crutches, depending on injury severity.
  • RICE principles: rest, ice, compression and elevation for most soft-tissue injuries.
  • Thromboprophylaxis: consider in high-risk patients with prolonged immobilisation, according to local guidelines.
  • Physiotherapy and rehab: essential for ankle sprains, Achilles injuries and post-fracture recovery.

Urgent orthopaedic referral is required for:

  • Open fractures or fracture-dislocations.
  • Neurovascular compromise or suspected compartment syndrome.
  • Unstable ankle fractures (bimalleolar, trimalleolar, talar shift on X-ray).
  • Achilles tendon rupture, suspected Lisfranc or complex midfoot fractures.
  • Septic arthritis and rapidly spreading infection.

Documentation & Summary

Carefully document:

  • Mechanism of injury and weight-bearing ability.
  • Exact location of tenderness and swelling.
  • Special tests performed (e.g. Thompson, anterior drawer) and results.
  • Neurovascular status before and after any manipulation or splinting.
  • Imaging findings and discussions with orthopaedics.
  • Weight-bearing instructions, follow-up plan and safety-net advice.

A structured ankle examination helps you safely distinguish simple sprains from occult fractures, tendon ruptures and limb-threatening pathology. Always think beyond “just a sprain” in the Emergency Department.
