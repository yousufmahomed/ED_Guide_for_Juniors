Ankle Exam

Introduction

An ankle examination is a physical examination performed by a healthcare provider to evaluate the function and health of the ankle joint. The ankle joint is a complex joint that connects the foot and leg bones and allows for a wide range of motion, so it is essential to perform a thorough examination to identify any injuries or abnormalities that may be present.

Examination Technique

The ankle examination involves the following steps:

  1. Inspect the ankle and foot for any visible signs of injury or swelling, such as bruising, redness, or deformities.
  2. Palpate the ankle joint, checking for tenderness or swelling in the bones, ligaments, and tendons surrounding the joint.
  3. Assess the range of motion of the ankle joint, including dorsiflexion, plantarflexion, inversion, and eversion. Ask the patient to move their ankle in each direction, observing for any limitations or pain.
  4. Perform specific tests to assess for specific injuries or conditions, such as the anterior drawer test for anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) injuries, the talar tilt test for lateral ankle sprains, or the squeeze test for high ankle sprains.
  5. Perform a neurovascular examination, assessing for any signs of nerve or blood vessel injury. Test the patient's sensation and reflexes in the affected foot and toes, and check for any abnormalities in pulse or blood pressure.
  6. Check for any swelling, redness, or discoloration around the ankle joint. Palpate the area for any tenderness or pain.
  7. Assess the range of motion of the ankle, including dorsiflexion, plantarflexion, inversion, and eversion. Ask the patient to move their foot in each direction, observing for any limitations or pain.
  8. Perform specific tests to assess for specific injuries or conditions, such as the Thompson test for Achilles tendon rupture, or the anterior drawer test for ankle instability.
  9. Assess for any signs of ankle instability or ligament injuries, such as pain or weakness when standing or walking on uneven surfaces.
  10. Evaluate the patient's gait and walking pattern, checking for any limping or abnormal movements.
  11. Check for any signs of arthritis, tendonitis, or other inflammatory conditions in the ankle joint. Assess for any signs of swelling, stiffness, or pain in the joint.
  12. Perform any additional tests or maneuvers as indicated by the patient's symptoms or medical history. This may include provocative testing for specific conditions, such as peroneal tendonitis or tarsal tunnel syndrome.

Imaging studies, such as X-rays or MRI, may be ordered if the examination reveals any abnormalities or if further evaluation is needed.

Common Ankle Injuries and Conditions

Some common ankle injuries and conditions that may be detected during an ankle examination include:

Injury/Condition Signs and Symptoms Differential Diagnosis Treatment
Ankle sprain Pain, swelling, tenderness, limited range of motion Fracture, ligament tear, tendonitis, arthritis Rest, ice, compression, elevation, physical therapy, pain management
Achilles tendonitis Pain and stiffness in the back of the ankle, swelling, tenderness Achilles tendon rupture, plantar fasciitis, bursitis Rest, ice, compression, physical therapy, pain management
Ankle impingement Pain and tenderness in the ankle joint, limited range of motion Arthritis, bone spur, ligament injury Rest, physical therapy, pain management, surgery
Peroneal tendonitis Pain, swelling, tenderness, and warmth in the affected area, weakness or instability, snapping or popping sensation, limited range of motion Rest, ice, compression, and elevation (RICE), non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs), physical therapy, bracing, corticosteroid injections
Achilles tendinopathy Pain and stiffness in the Achilles tendon, swelling, tenderness, limited range of motion, thickening of the tendon, difficulty walking or running Rest, ice, compression, and elevation (RICE), physical therapy, orthotics, stretching and strengthening exercises, NSAIDs, corticosteroid injections, shockwave therapy, surgery in severe cases
Plantar fasciitis Heel pain, especially in the morning or after long periods of sitting or standing, tenderness, swelling, limited range of motion, stiffness Rest, ice, compression, and elevation (RICE), physical therapy, orthotics, stretching and strengthening exercises, NSAIDs, corticosteroid injections, shockwave therapy, surgery in severe cases
Tarsal tunnel syndrome Pain, burning, tingling, and numbness in the ankle and foot, weakness or atrophy of the muscles in the foot, difficulty walking or standing for long periods of time Rest, physical therapy, orthotics, stretching exercises, NSAIDs, corticosteroid injections, surgery in severe cases
Posterior tibial tendonitis Pain, swelling, and tenderness in the inner ankle, difficulty walking or standing, flatfoot, heel pain, arch pain Rest, physical therapy, orthotics, bracing, stretching and strengthening exercises, NSAIDs, corticosteroid injections, surgery in severe cases

Treatment for ankle conditions may also involve immobilization with a brace or cast, depending on the severity of the injury or condition. Surgery may be necessary in severe cases, such as fractures or severe ligament tears. In any case, early diagnosis and treatment can help improve outcomes and prevent long-term complications.

Preventing Ankle Injuries

While not all ankle injuries can be prevented, there are some steps you can take to reduce your risk:

  • Wear proper footwear with good support, especially during physical activity
  • Stretch and warm up before exercise or physical activity
  • Avoid uneven surfaces and obstacles when possible
  • Avoid high-impact activities or sports that involve a lot of jumping or sudden changes in direction
  • Gradually increase the intensity and duration of physical activity
  • Take breaks when needed to avoid overuse injuries
  • Maintain a healthy weight to reduce stress on the ankles and feet
  • Seek medical attention for any ankle pain or injury, and follow a rehabilitation program as directed by a healthcare professional performing a comprehensive ankle examination and identifying the underlying cause of symptoms, healthcare providers can develop an appropriate treatment plan for their patients.

Treatment options for ankle conditions may include:

  • Rest and immobilization: Reducing or avoiding weight-bearing activities and immobilizing the ankle with a brace or cast may be necessary for some conditions.
  • Pain management: Over-the-counter or prescription pain medications, as well as icing and elevation, may help reduce pain and swelling.
  • Physical therapy: Strengthening and stretching exercises can improve ankle function and reduce the risk of future injury.
  • Injections: In some cases, corticosteroid injections may be used to reduce inflammation and pain in the ankle joint.
  • Surgery: In severe cases or when other treatments have been unsuccessful, surgical procedures such as ankle arthroscopy, ankle fusion, or ankle replacement may be recommended.

Prevention is also an important aspect of ankle care. Simple steps that individuals can take to reduce their risk of ankle injury include:

  • Wearing appropriate footwear for activities, such as shoes with good support and cushioning for running or hiking.
  • Performing stretching and strengthening exercises for the ankle and surrounding muscles.
  • Avoiding uneven or unstable surfaces that may increase the risk of ankle sprains or twists.
  • Using appropriate protective gear, such as ankle braces or guards, during sports or activities that may increase the risk of ankle injury.
