Genitourinary Exam

Overview

The genitourinary (GU) examination in the Emergency Department focuses on identifying time-critical conditions such as testicular torsion, Fournier’s gangrene, urinary retention, renal colic, pyelonephritis and acute STI presentations. It combines a sensitive, respectful examination with appropriate privacy, chaperoning and clear documentation.

The GU system includes the kidneys, ureters, bladder, urethra, prostate (in males) and reproductive organs. Always consider associated abdominal, back and systemic symptoms.

Key Symptoms & Red Flags

  • Dysuria, frequency, urgency, nocturia, incontinence.
  • Haematuria (visible or dipstick), clots, dark or foul-smelling urine.
  • Flank or loin pain, groin pain, suprapubic pain.
  • Scrotal pain or swelling, penile pain, discharge or lesions.
  • Vaginal discharge, bleeding, pelvic pain, dyspareunia.
  • Systemic features: fever, rigors, nausea/vomiting, hypotension.

Red flags: suspected testicular torsion, Fournier’s gangrene, acute urinary retention, severe sepsis from urinary source, scrotal trauma with possible rupture, heavy vaginal bleeding, pregnancy with abdominal pain or PV bleeding, and suspected sexual assault.

History

Take a focused but respectful history, ensuring privacy and using a chaperone where appropriate.

  • Urinary symptoms: onset, duration, dysuria, frequency, urgency, incontinence, hesitancy, weak stream, nocturia, retention, haematuria.
  • Pain: site (flank, groin, suprapubic, scrotal, pelvic), character, radiation, severity, timing, aggravating/relieving factors (e.g. movement, voiding).
  • Systemic features: fever, nausea, vomiting, rigors, weight loss, night sweats.
  • Sexual history (where relevant): partners, protection, new partner, discharge, genital ulcers or lesions, dyspareunia, prior STIs.
  • Past medical history: kidney disease, recurrent UTIs, stones, prostate disease, diabetes, immunosuppression, prior genital surgery or trauma.
  • Medication history: anticoagulants, antiplatelets, recent antibiotics, nephrotoxic drugs.
  • Gynaecological/obstetric history (females): LMP, contraception, pregnancy status, PV bleeding, previous ectopic pregnancy or PID.

Physical Examination

Ensure a chaperone is present for intimate examinations, explain each step, and provide draping. Start with general and abdominal examination, then focus on genital and rectal examinations as indicated.

General & Abdominal

  • General appearance: distress, sepsis, dehydration, pallor, jaundice.
  • Vital signs: fever, tachycardia, hypotension, tachypnoea.
  • Abdominal examination: inspection, palpation (tenderness, guarding, masses), percussion, auscultation.
  • Flank and costovertebral angle tenderness in suspected pyelonephritis or renal colic.
  • Bladder palpation and percussion for distension/retention.

Male Examination

For male patients, with appropriate privacy and chaperone:

  • Inspection of external genitalia: penile shaft and glans for lesions, ulcers, discharge, phimosis/paraphimosis, trauma.
  • Scrotum: size, asymmetry, skin changes, erythema, oedema, ulcers, crepitus (suspicious for Fournier’s gangrene).
  • Testes and epididymis: palpate each testis for size, consistency, tenderness and lumps; compare sides. Note high-riding or horizontally lying testis in torsion.
  • Spermatic cord: tenderness, thickening or “bag of worms” (varicocele).
  • Inguinal region: hernias, lymphadenopathy.
  • Digital rectal examination (DRE): if indicated – prostate size, consistency, tenderness (prostatitis), nodules; rectal masses or tenderness.

Female Examination

For female patients, with appropriate privacy and chaperone:

  • External genitalia: vulva, labia and perineum for lesions, ulcers, warts, discharge, skin changes or trauma.
  • Speculum examination: where indicated – assess cervix (erythema, friability, discharge, ulcers, ectropion, lesions), presence of blood or pus in the vaginal vault.
  • Bimanual examination: assess uterine size, position and mobility; adnexal tenderness or masses; cervical motion tenderness (suggestive of PID).
  • Pelvic tenderness: localised vs generalised, rebound or guarding (consider ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, ruptured cyst, PID, appendicitis, etc.).

Diagnostic Tests

Investigations should be guided by history and examination findings:

  • Urinalysis (dipstick and microscopy): leucocytes, nitrites, blood, protein, glucose, ketones.
  • Urine culture: in suspected UTI, pyelonephritis, recurrent infections or complicated cases.
  • Blood tests: FBC, U&E/creatinine, CRP, LFTs, blood cultures in sepsis; β-hCG in women of reproductive age.
  • Imaging: ultrasound (kidneys, bladder, scrotum, pelvis), CT KUB for stones, CT scan for suspected Fournier’s or abscess, as per local protocols.
  • Bladder scan: for suspected urinary retention.
  • Cystoscopy: usually not ED but urology-led for haematuria work-up, strictures, recurrent infections.

Table 1: Testicular Torsion

Clinical Features Associated Factors / Notes
Sudden onset of severe unilateral testicular pain (often in adolescents/young men). Twisting of spermatic cord → arterial occlusion → ischaemia.
Scrotal swelling and possible erythema; high-riding or horizontally lying testis. Reduced or absent blood flow on Doppler ultrasound (if available – do not delay theatre).
Lower abdominal pain, nausea or vomiting. Often misdiagnosed as abdominal pathology; maintain high index of suspicion.
Very tender testis; often absent cremasteric reflex on the affected side. Time critical – salvage best within 6 hours; urgent surgical review.

Table 2: Fournier's Gangrene

Clinical Features Risk Factors / Causes
Pain, swelling and erythema of scrotum, perineum or lower abdominal wall. Polymicrobial necrotising soft tissue infection.
Rapidly progressive tissue necrosis, skin discolouration, crepitus, bullae. Often arises from perianal, perineal or genitourinary infection or trauma.
Systemic toxicity: fever, tachycardia, hypotension, confusion. Common in diabetes, obesity, immunosuppression, chronic alcohol use.
Foul-smelling discharge or gas in tissues. Delay in diagnosis or treatment increases mortality; requires urgent surgery + broad-spectrum IV antibiotics.

Table 3: Testicular Cancer

Clinical Features Risk Factors / Associations
Painless lump or swelling in the testicle. Abnormal germ cell growth within the testis.
Heaviness or dragging sensation in the scrotum. Previous history of testicular cancer.
Occasional dull ache or discomfort in testicle or lower abdomen. Family history of testicular cancer.
Enlarged or firm testis compared with the other side. Cryptorchidism (undescended testis), particularly if not corrected early.
Back or abdominal pain with advanced disease (from nodal or metastatic spread). Previous testicular atrophy, certain genetic conditions or prior chemotherapy/radiation.

Common Sexually Transmitted Infections (STIs)

Sexually Transmitted Infection Typical Clinical Features Example Treatments*
Chlamydia Often asymptomatic; urethral or vaginal discharge, dysuria, intermenstrual bleeding, pelvic pain. Azithromycin or doxycycline as per guideline.
Gonorrhoea Purulent urethral/cervical discharge, dysuria, pelvic pain; often asymptomatic in women. Ceftriaxone-based regimen plus coverage for Chlamydia as per guideline.
Syphilis Primary: painless chancre; Secondary: rash, mucous patches, lymphadenopathy; Late: neurological and cardiac complications. Penicillin G (benzathine or parenteral preparation depending on stage).
Genital Herpes (HSV) Recurrent painful vesicles/ulcers, burning or itching, may have systemic symptoms at first episode. Oral antivirals (e.g. aciclovir, valaciclovir, famciclovir).
HIV Acute retroviral syndrome (fever, rash, sore throat) or asymptomatic; late disease with opportunistic infections. Antiretroviral therapy (ART) and linkage to HIV care.
HPV Genital warts; abnormal Pap smear; many infections asymptomatic. HPV vaccination, topical treatments, cryotherapy or surgical removal of warts as indicated.

*Always follow the latest national and local STI treatment guidelines.

Conclusion

The genitourinary examination is an important part of the Emergency Department assessment when patients present with urinary, scrotal, genital or pelvic symptoms. A respectful, chaperoned examination combined with focused history, bedside tests and appropriate imaging can identify life-threatening conditions such as torsion and Fournier’s gangrene, guide management of common problems like UTIs and renal colic, and ensure timely diagnosis and treatment of STIs in line with national guidelines.
