Ophthalmology Exam

Overview

The eye examination in the Emergency Department focuses on identifying vision-threatening emergencies (e.g. acute angle-closure glaucoma, retinal detachment, central retinal artery occlusion, orbital cellulitis, open globe injuries, chemical burns) and common ED presentations such as conjunctivitis, corneal abrasions and minor trauma.

A structured approach includes visual acuity, external inspection, pupils, ocular movements, anterior segment assessment, fundus examination and intraocular pressure where appropriate.

Red Flags in Eye Complaints

  • Sudden, severe loss of vision in one or both eyes.
  • Severe eye pain, especially with headache, nausea and halos around lights.
  • Marked photophobia, ciliary flush or corneal haze.
  • Trauma with suspected open globe, penetrating injury or foreign body at high velocity.
  • Chemical exposure to the eye.
  • Proptosis, painful/restricted eye movements, fever (suspected orbital cellulitis).
  • New afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) or markedly abnormal pupils.
  • Acute diplopia with neurological signs.

Visual Acuity

Visual acuity is the “vital sign” of the eye and should be documented in each eye separately (with and without pinhole / correction if possible).

  • Use a Snellen chart at 6 m (or 3 m/near chart as available).
  • Test with the patient’s usual glasses/contact lenses where applicable, then with pinhole if reduced.
  • Record as, for example, “VA: OD 6/9, OS 6/18 (improves to 6/9 with pinhole)”.
  • If unable to read the top line, progress to counting fingers, hand movements, perception of light.

Sudden unilateral loss or significant asymmetry between eyes warrants urgent evaluation and often specialist input.

External Eye Examination

Inspect both eyes and peri-orbital structures:

  • General: symmetry, ptosis, proptosis, lid swelling, bruising or lacerations.
  • Lids and lashes: entropion, ectropion, styes, chalazia, crusting, blepharitis.
  • Conjunctiva and sclera: injection (pattern), chemosis, subconjunctival haemorrhage, discharge (watery, mucous, purulent).
  • Cornea: clarity, foreign bodies, obvious abrasions, opacities.
  • Foreign bodies: evert upper lid to exclude subtarsal foreign bodies in abrasion/foreign body history.

Pupils, Eye Movements and Visual Fields

  • Pupils: size, shape and equality in normal light and in the dark.
  • Light reaction: direct and consensual response to a bright light.
  • Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD): swinging flashlight test – suggests optic nerve or severe retinal disease.
  • Eye movements: assess ductions in the six cardinal positions of gaze; note diplopia, pain, or restriction.
  • Visual fields: confrontational testing – gross defects such as hemianopia or quadrantanopia.

Anterior Segment and Internal Eye Exam

Where available, use a slit lamp. Otherwise use a bright light and magnification to evaluate the anterior segment.

  • Conjunctiva and cornea: pattern of redness (conjunctival vs ciliary injection), corneal clarity, presence of ulcer, infiltrate or scar.
  • Anterior chamber: depth (shallow in angle-closure), cells/flare (uveitis), blood (hyphaema), hypopyon.
  • Lens: obvious cataract or lens dislocation if visible.
  • Fundus/optic nerve: using direct ophthalmoscope – optic disc (colour, margins, cupping), vessels, macula, haemorrhages, exudates, retinal detachment where visible.

Fluorescein Staining and Corneal Assessment

In patients with pain, foreign body sensation or trauma, apply topical anaesthetic and fluorescein, then examine with cobalt blue light where available.

  • Identify corneal abrasions, ulcers and dendritic lesions (herpetic keratitis).
  • Look for linear vertical staining suggesting subtarsal foreign body.
  • Perform a Seidel test if you suspect globe perforation (streaming of dye indicates aqueous leak) – if positive, shield and do not press the eye, immediate ophthalmology referral.

Intraocular Pressure (IOP)

Check IOP using a tonometer where trained and when it is safe to do so (avoid in suspected open globe). Elevated pressure suggests glaucoma or other causes of raised IOP; very low pressure can occur after globe rupture.

  • Normal IOP: approximately 10–21 mmHg.
  • Marked elevation with pain, redness, mid-dilated pupil and cloudy cornea suggests acute angle-closure glaucoma.
  • Document the value and which eye was tested.

Types of Glaucoma

Type of Glaucoma Common Clinical Features
Open-angle glaucoma
  • Chronic, usually asymptomatic early on.
  • Gradual optic nerve damage and visual field loss (peripheral first).
  • Intraocular pressure often elevated but can be normal (“normal-tension” glaucoma).
  • No acute redness or severe pain in most cases.
  • Often detected on routine screening or progressive field loss.
Acute angle-closure glaucoma
  • Sudden onset of severe eye pain and headache.
  • Blurred vision with halos around lights.
  • Nausea and vomiting, systemic unwell feeling.
  • Red eye with a mid-dilated, sluggish pupil.
  • Corneal oedema giving a “hazy” appearance.
  • Markedly raised intraocular pressure – ophthalmic emergency.

Types of Orbital/Periorbital Cellulitis

Type of Cellulitis Common Clinical Features
Pre-septal cellulitis (periorbital)
  • Swelling, redness and warmth of eyelids and periorbital skin.
  • Tenderness around the eye.
  • May have low-grade fever.
  • Vision normal, no proptosis, full painless eye movements.
Orbital cellulitis
  • Swelling and redness around the eye plus systemic illness (fever, malaise).
  • Proptosis (bulging eye) and chemosis.
  • Painful and/or restricted eye movements.
  • Decreased or blurred vision, possible RAPD.
  • Diplopia.
  • Tenderness over orbit and often sinus tenderness.
  • Potential intracranial and cavernous sinus complications – urgent imaging and IV antibiotics.

Summary

A structured ED eye exam begins with visual acuity and red flag screening, followed by systematic assessment of external structures, pupils, movements, anterior segment, cornea and fundus, with IOP measurement where appropriate. Early recognition of emergencies such as acute angle-closure glaucoma, orbital cellulitis, open globe injuries and acute retinal or optic nerve pathology is critical to preserving vision and preventing serious complications.
