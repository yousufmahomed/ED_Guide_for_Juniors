Respiratory Exam

Overview

The respiratory examination is a key component of Emergency Department assessment for patients with shortness of breath, cough, chest pain, wheeze, trauma, sepsis or altered consciousness. The aim is to rapidly identify life-threatening conditions such as severe asthma, COPD exacerbations, pneumonia with sepsis, pulmonary oedema, pulmonary embolism, pneumothorax and chest trauma.

A structured approach includes inspection, palpation, percussion and auscultation, integrated with vital signs, pulse oximetry and bedside investigations (e.g. ABG, chest X-ray).

Initial Assessment & Red Flags

  • Assess airway, breathing and circulation immediately.
  • Record vital signs: respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, heart rate, blood pressure, temperature.
  • Note ability to speak in full sentences vs single words, use of accessory muscles, agitation or drowsiness.
  • Look for cyanosis, diaphoresis, altered mental status or signs of shock.

Red flags: silent chest, exhaustion, confusion, severe hypoxia, rapidly worsening respiratory distress, tracheal deviation, unilateral absent breath sounds, haemodynamic instability or chest trauma with obvious deformity.

Inspection

Begin with inspection, observing the patient’s overall appearance, breathing pattern and chest movements:

  • Assess respiratory rate, rhythm and depth.
  • Observe for increased work of breathing: nasal flaring, intercostal/subcostal retractions, use of accessory muscles, pursed-lip breathing.
  • Inspect the chest wall for deformities, asymmetry, scars, bruising, surgical wounds or paradoxical movement (flail chest).
  • Look for cyanosis of lips, tongue or nail beds and digital clubbing or peripheral oedema.

Palpation

Palpation assesses chest wall tenderness, expansion and underlying air in soft tissues:

  • Palpate the chest wall for areas of tenderness, deformity, crepitus (subcutaneous emphysema) or step deformities (rib fractures).
  • Assess chest expansion by placing hands on the lower chest posteriorly or anteriorly and observing symmetry of movement.
  • Check tactile vocal fremitus (if practiced): increased in consolidation, decreased in effusion or pneumothorax.
  • Palpate the trachea for deviation (may occur in tension pneumothorax or large effusion).
  • Assess for tenderness or crepitus in the neck, suggesting subcutaneous emphysema or associated trauma.

Percussion

Percussion helps distinguish between air, fluid and solid tissue in the thorax:

  • Percuss over the anterior, lateral and posterior chest walls in a systematic fashion, comparing side to side.
  • Dullness may suggest consolidation (e.g. pneumonia), pleural effusion or collapse.
  • Hyperresonance may suggest pneumothorax or markedly hyperinflated lungs (e.g. severe emphysema/asthma).

Auscultation

Auscultation is used to assess breath sounds and identify added sounds that indicate pathology:

  • Auscultate the anterior, lateral and posterior chest systematically, comparing left with right at each level.
  • Note the intensity and quality of breath sounds (vesicular vs bronchial, or decreased/absent).

Common abnormal breath sounds:

  • Wheezing: High-pitched, musical sounds due to narrowing of airways; seen in asthma, COPD or bronchitis.
  • Crackles (rales): Discontinuous, popping sounds from fluid or collapsed airways; seen in pneumonia, pulmonary oedema, fibrosis.
  • Rhonchi: Low-pitched, snoring sounds from secretions in larger airways; often in bronchitis or COPD.
  • Stridor: High-pitched, harsh sound from upper airway obstruction; usually inspiratory (e.g. foreign body, croup, epiglottitis, laryngeal oedema).
  • Pleural friction rub: Creaking/grating sound from inflamed pleural surfaces rubbing; may occur in pleurisy, pneumonia or pulmonary embolism.
  • Auscultate over the trachea and main bronchi to assess for stridor and compare central vs peripheral breath sounds.

Additional Tests

Depending on symptoms and examination findings, additional ED investigations may include:

  • Chest X-ray: to assess for consolidation, effusion, pneumothorax, pulmonary oedema, cardiomegaly, fractures.
  • Pulse oximetry: continuous SpO2 monitoring to assess oxygenation and response to therapy.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG): to evaluate oxygenation, ventilation and acid–base status, especially in severe COPD/asthma, respiratory distress or shock.
  • Point-of-care ultrasound (where available): for pneumothorax, effusion, B-lines (oedema) and volume status.
  • Pulmonary function tests: typically outpatient; limited role in acute ED setting, but peak flow may be useful in asthma.

Common Findings and Abnormalities

Typical abnormal findings during a respiratory examination include:

  • Increased work of breathing: retractions, nasal flaring, tachypnoea, inability to speak full sentences.
  • Decreased or absent breath sounds (effusion, pneumothorax, severe obstruction).
  • Focal bronchial breath sounds and dullness (lobar consolidation).
  • Diffuse wheeze (asthma/COPD exacerbation).
  • Fine or coarse crackles (pulmonary oedema, pneumonia, fibrosis).
  • Dullness or hyperresonance on percussion corresponding to X-ray abnormalities.
  • Abnormal oxygen saturation or ABG results indicating hypoxaemia, hypercapnia or acid–base disturbance.

Summary

A structured respiratory examination in the Emergency Department—integrating inspection, palpation, percussion and auscultation with vital signs, SpO2, ABG and imaging—helps rapidly identify life-threatening respiratory conditions and guide urgent treatment decisions such as oxygen therapy, bronchodilators, antibiotics, diuretics, non-invasive ventilation or intubation.
