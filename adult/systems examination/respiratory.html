Respiratory Examination in the Emergency Department

A respiratory examination is a key component of the assessment of patients presenting to the emergency department with respiratory symptoms or complaints. The examination aims to identify the presence and nature of any respiratory abnormalities, including those affecting the lungs, airways, and chest wall.

Inspection

The respiratory examination typically begins with inspection, during which healthcare providers observe the patient's breathing and chest movements. They may also look for any signs of respiratory distress or cyanosis, including bluish discoloration of the lips, tongue, or fingers.

  • Assess the patient's breathing rate, rhythm, and depth.
  • Observe for any signs of increased work of breathing, such as retractions or nasal flaring.
  • Inspect the chest wall for any deformities or asymmetry.
  • Look for any signs of skin or nail bed changes that may indicate hypoxia or cyanosis.

Palpation

Palpation during a respiratory examination involves feeling different areas of the chest and neck for any abnormalities or indications of respiratory function. Here are the key areas that healthcare providers typically palpate during a respiratory examination:

  • Palpate the chest wall for any areas of tenderness, crepitus, or masses.
  • Assess for any areas of decreased or absent breath sounds, which may indicate obstruction or consolidation.
  • Assess for any areas of increased or loud breath sounds, which may indicate bronchial breath sounds or wheezing.
  • Palpate the trachea for any deviation, which may indicate airway obstruction.
  • Assess for any areas of tenderness or crepitus in the neck, which may indicate subcutaneous emphysema.

Percussion

Percussion is a technique used during a respiratory examination to assess the density of the underlying lung tissue. Here are the key areas that healthcare providers typically percuss during a respiratory examination:

  • Percuss the chest wall to assess for areas of dullness, which may indicate consolidation or pleural effusion.
  • Percuss the chest wall to assess for areas of hyperresonance, which may indicate pneumothorax or emphysema.

Auscultation

Auscultation is an important component of the respiratory examination that involves listening to the patient's breath sounds with a stethoscope. Healthcare providers listen for any abnormalities or indications of respiratory function, including breath sounds, wheezing, and crackles. Here are the key areas that healthcare providers typically auscultate during a respiratory examination:

  • Auscultate the anterior and posterior chest walls, listening for breath sounds and any adventitious sounds such as wheezing, crackles, or rhonchi.

    • During auscultation of the respiratory system, healthcare providers listen for any abnormalities in breath sounds that may indicate respiratory distress or disease. Here are some of the most common abnormal breath sounds and their descriptions:

    • Wheezing: A high-pitched, musical sound that occurs when air flows through narrow airways. Wheezing can be heard during both inspiration and expiration and is often associated with conditions such as asthma, bronchitis, or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD).
    • Crackles: Also known as rales, crackles are a series of short, nonmusical sounds that occur when air moves through fluid-filled airways or when collapsed airways suddenly open. Crackles can be heard during both inspiration and expiration and are often associated with conditions such as pneumonia, heart failure, or pulmonary fibrosis.
    • Rhonchi: A low-pitched, rumbling sound that occurs when air flows through obstructed airways. Rhonchi are often described as snoring or rattling and can be heard during both inspiration and expiration. Rhonchi are often associated with conditions such as chronic bronchitis, bronchiectasis, or COPD.
    • Stridor: A high-pitched, harsh sound that occurs when there is obstruction of the upper airway. Stridor is typically heard during inspiration and is often associated with conditions such as croup, epiglottitis, or foreign body aspiration.
    • Pleural friction rub: A creaking or grating sound that occurs when inflamed pleural surfaces rub against each other during respiration. Pleural friction rubs are often heard during both inspiration and expiration and are often associated with conditions such as pleurisy, pneumonia, or pulmonary embolism.
  • Auscultate over the trachea and mainstem bronchi to assess for any abnormalities in breath sounds or the presence of stridor.

Additional Tests

Depending on the patient's symptoms and physical exam findings, diagnostic tests may be ordered to further evaluate respiratory function. Here are some common tests that may be ordered:

  • Chest X-ray to assess for any abnormalities in lung tissue or the chest wall.
  • Pulse oximetry to assess for oxygen saturation levels in the blood.
  • Arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis to assess for acid-base imbalances and oxygenation levels in the blood.
  • Pulmonary function tests to assess lung function and capacity.

Common Findings and Abnormalities

Common findings and abnormalities detected during a respiratory examination include:

  • Increased work of breathing, such as retractions or nasal flaring.
  • Decreased or absent breath sounds, which may indicate obstruction or consolidation.
  • Increased or loud breath sounds, which may indicate bronchial breath sounds or wheezing.
  • Signs of respiratory distress or cyanosis.
  • Dullness or hyperresonance on percussion.
  • Abnormal breath sounds, such as crackles or rhonchi.
  • Abnormal oxygen saturation levels or arterial blood gas results.
  • Abnormal chest X-ray or pulmonary function test results.

Conclusion

A respiratory examination is a critical component of the assessment of patients presenting to the emergency department with respiratory symptoms or complaints. By carefully inspecting, palpating, percussing, and auscultating the patient's respiratory system, healthcare providers can identify any abnormalities or indications of respiratory function and develop an appropriate treatment plan to ensure the best possible outcomes for their patients.
