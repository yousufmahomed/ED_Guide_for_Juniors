Abdominal Examination

Introduction

An abdominal examination is a key part of assessing patients with abdominal pain, vomiting, diarrhoea, trauma or non-specific unwellness in the Emergency Department. A structured approach helps you detect time-critical pathology, support safe decision-making, and communicate clearly with seniors and surgeons.

The classic sequence is: Inspection → Auscultation → Percussion → Palpation, but in the ED, your assessment is always guided by airway, breathing, circulation and haemodynamic stability.

Preparation

Before you start:

  • Ensure the patient is haemodynamically stable — if shocked, prioritise resus first.
  • Explain the examination and obtain consent where possible.
  • Position: patient supine, arms by the side, abdomen exposed from xiphisternum to pubis, with privacy maintained.
  • Provide a pillow or blanket for comfort and warmth.
  • Ask about pain and examine the painful area last.
  • Wash your hands, use gloves when appropriate, and ensure good lighting.

Inspection

Stand at the end of the bed and look for:

  • Distension, asymmetry or visible masses.
  • Scars (previous surgery), stomas or drains.
  • Visible peristalsis or pulsations.
  • Skin changes — bruising (e.g. Cullen’s or Grey-Turner’s signs), erythema, rash, striae.
  • Visible hernias (inguinal, umbilical, incisional).
  • Respiratory pattern (pain-related splinting or shallow breathing).

Auscultation

Auscultate before you palpate or percuss:

  • Listen in all four quadrants for bowel sounds (frequency, character).
  • Normal sounds are low-pitched and intermittent. Absent or very faint sounds may suggest ileus or obstruction.
  • High-pitched “tinkling” sounds may also be seen in obstruction.
  • Over the epigastrium or aorta, listen for bruits if vascular pathology is suspected.

Palpation

Start away from the pain and build up gradually:

  • Light palpation for tenderness, superficial masses and guarding.
  • Deep palpation for organomegaly (liver, spleen) and deeper masses.
  • Assess for guarding, rigidity and rebound tenderness — concerning for peritonitis.
  • Check for hernias (inguinal, femoral, umbilical, incisional) — cough impulse, reducibility, tenderness.

Always re-assess your patient’s pain, vitals and overall appearance during palpation. Worsening pain, tachycardia, hypotension or collapse requires urgent escalation.

Percussion

Percuss the abdomen to assess:

  • Tympany over gas-filled loops of bowel.
  • Dullness suggesting fluid (ascites), enlarged organs or masses.
  • Shifting dullness if ascites is suspected.
  • Dullness over the liver and splenic areas to help define organ size.

Differential Diagnosis by Pain Location

Pain location, associated symptoms and risk factors help narrow your differential. Always remember extra-abdominal causes such as pneumonia or MI in atypical presentations.

Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ)
  • Cholecystitis / biliary colic (gallstones).
  • Hepatitis or other liver disease.
  • Pancreatitis.
  • Duodenal ulcer.
  • Right lower lobe pneumonia or pleurisy.
Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ)
  • Splenic rupture or infarction.
  • Gastritis or peptic ulcer disease.
  • Pancreatitis.
  • Left lower lobe pneumonia or pleurisy.
Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ)
  • Appendicitis.
  • Mesenteric adenitis.
  • Diverticulitis (right-sided in some populations).
  • Salpingitis, ovarian cyst, ovarian torsion, ectopic pregnancy.
  • Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis.
Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ)
  • Diverticulitis.
  • Salpingitis, ovarian cyst, ovarian torsion, ectopic pregnancy.
  • Crohn’s disease or ulcerative colitis.
  • Constipation.

Additional Tests

Based on history and examination, consider:

  • Point-of-care tests: urine dipstick, pregnancy test (in all females of childbearing age).
  • Bloods: FBC, U&E, LFTs, CRP, amylase/lipase, coagulation profile, lactate.
  • Urinalysis and culture.
  • Abdominal ultrasound (RUQ pain, biliary disease, AAA, ascites, gynaecological pathology).
  • CT abdomen and pelvis (especially in undifferentiated acute abdomen, trauma, suspected obstruction).
  • Endoscopy as indicated for upper or lower GI bleeding.

Common Findings & Abnormalities

  • Localised or generalised abdominal tenderness or pain.
  • Palpable masses or organ enlargement.
  • Distended abdomen.
  • Guarding, rigidity or rebound tenderness.
  • Hernias (reducible vs tender / incarcerated / strangulated).
  • Absent, reduced or hyperactive bowel sounds.
  • Areas of dullness or tympany on percussion.

Appendicitis – Key Features

Clinical Feature Description
Abdominal pain Classically begins peri-umbilically, then localises to the right iliac fossa. Atypical in pregnancy or retrocaecal appendix.
Nausea and vomiting Common, particularly as inflammation progresses.
Loss of appetite Very frequent in appendicitis; anorexia is a key supporting feature.
Fever Typically low-grade; higher fever may suggest perforation or abscess.
Tenderness and rigidity RIF tenderness with voluntary or involuntary guarding.
Rebound tenderness Pain on release of pressure over RIF — peritoneal irritation.
Guarding Involuntary tensing of abdominal muscles due to peritoneal inflammation.
Altered bowel habit Diarrhoea or constipation can occur but are non-specific.
Malaise and fatigue Non-specific sense of being unwell, often present.

Cholecystitis

Feature Description
Abdominal pain RUQ pain, may radiate to back or right shoulder, often post-prandial (fatty meals).
Nausea and vomiting Common with biliary colic and cholecystitis.
Fever Low-grade fever is common in acute cholecystitis.
Murphy’s sign Arrest of inspiration with deep palpation under right costal margin.
Jaundice May occur if there is associated obstruction of the common bile duct.
Gallstones Often visible on ultrasound or other imaging.

Ascending Cholangitis

Feature Description
Abdominal pain RUQ or epigastric pain, may radiate to back or shoulder.
Fever High fever is typical — consider sepsis.
Jaundice Due to biliary obstruction.
Hypotension May occur in septic shock.
Mental status changes Confusion, agitation or reduced GCS in severe sepsis.
Charcot’s triad Fever, jaundice and RUQ pain.
Reynolds’ pentad Charcot’s triad plus hypotension and altered mental status.

Upper GIT Bleed – Common Causes

Cause Clinical Features Diagnostic Tests Treatment
Peptic ulcer disease Epigastric pain, nausea, vomiting, melaena, haematemeis. Endoscopy, FBC, coagulation profile, group & cross-match. PPIs, H. pylori eradication, resuscitation, endoscopic haemostasis, surgery if refractory.
Gastritis Epigastric discomfort, nausea, vomiting, possible melaena or haematemeis. Endoscopy, FBC, coagulation profile. PPIs, remove offending agents (NSAIDs, alcohol), transfusion as needed.
Mallory–Weiss tear Haematemesis after forceful vomiting; epigastric pain or discomfort. Endoscopy, FBC, coagulation profile. Often supportive; endoscopic therapy if ongoing bleeding.
Oesophageal varices Massive haematemesis, melaena, stigmata of chronic liver disease. Endoscopy, FBC, LFTs, coagulation profile, group & cross-match. Resuscitation, vasoactive drugs, banding/sclerotherapy, antibiotics, consider TIPS, manage portal hypertension.
Malignancy Epigastric pain, weight loss, anorexia, anaemia, occult or overt GI bleeding. Endoscopy with biopsy, imaging as needed. Oncologic management (surgery, chemo, radiotherapy), palliative care as appropriate.

Lower GIT Bleed – Common Causes

Cause Clinical Features Diagnostic Tests Treatment
Diverticulosis / Diverticulitis Lower abdominal pain, rectal bleeding, constipation or diarrhoea, fever (if inflamed). CT abdomen, colonoscopy (once stable), FBC, CRP. Bowel rest, antibiotics if diverticulitis, surgery if perforation or severe disease.
Colorectal cancer Rectal bleeding, change in bowel habit, abdominal pain, weight loss, anaemia. Colonoscopy with biopsy, imaging, FBC. Surgery, chemotherapy, radiotherapy, palliative care as needed.
Inflammatory bowel disease Bloody diarrhoea, abdominal pain, weight loss, extra-intestinal features. Colonoscopy with biopsy, faecal calprotectin, stool cultures, bloods. Aminosalicylates, steroids, immunomodulators, biologics, surgery in complications.
Ischaemic colitis Acute abdominal pain (often left sided), rectal bleeding, diarrhoea, cardiovascular risk factors. CT angiography or CT abdomen, colonoscopy, bloods. Supportive care, bowel rest, treat precipitating cause, surgery if necrosis/perforation.
Haemorrhoids Bright red rectal bleeding on paper or in bowl, anal discomfort or pruritus. Clinical exam, proctoscopy / sigmoidoscopy. Dietary modification, topical therapy, banding, surgery in refractory cases.

Red Flags & ED Pearls

  • Severe, sudden onset “tearing” pain — consider AAA, perforation, mesenteric ischaemia.
  • Persistent tachycardia, hypotension or rising lactate despite fluids — escalate early.
  • Peritonism (rigidity, rebound) — surgical review urgently.
  • Unexplained abdominal pain in pregnancy — always consider ectopic pregnancy.
  • Elderly, diabetic or immunocompromised patients may have blunted signs — have a low threshold for imaging and senior input.

Conclusion

Abdominal examination in the ED should be systematic, focused and repeatable. Combine your findings with a careful history, vital signs, bedside tests and appropriate imaging. When in doubt, escalate early, consider serial examinations, and involve surgical, gastroenterology or ICU teams as needed.
