Abdominal Exam

Introduction

An abdominal examination is an important part of the physical exam for patients presenting with abdominal pain or other gastrointestinal symptoms in the emergency department. This exam involves a systematic approach to inspecting, auscultating, palpating, and percussing the patient's abdomen to assess for any abnormalities or signs of underlying disease.

Preparation

Before beginning the examination, ensure that the patient is comfortable and properly positioned. The patient should be lying flat on their back with their arms at their sides and their legs extended. It may be necessary to provide a pillow or blanket for support or warmth. Wash your hands thoroughly and put on gloves before beginning the examination.

Inspection

Begin the examination by visually inspecting the patient's abdomen. Look for any visible abnormalities such as scars, hernias, or distention. Observe the patient's respiratory rate and pattern as well, as this can provide clues to underlying abdominal pathology.

Auscultation

Use a stethoscope to listen to the patient's bowel sounds. The bowel sounds should be audible in all four quadrants of the abdomen and should be regular and normal in tone. Absent or hyperactive bowel sounds may indicate intestinal obstruction or ileus.

Palpation

Palpate the patient's abdomen for any areas of tenderness, masses, or abnormal organ enlargement. Use a light touch to assess for surface tenderness, then gradually increase pressure to assess for deeper tenderness. It may be necessary to perform a deep palpation to assess for any masses or organ enlargement.

During palpation, also assess for any signs of guarding, rigidity, or rebound tenderness, as these may indicate peritonitis or other intra-abdominal pathology. Additionally, assess for the presence of a hernia by performing a cough test or asking the patient to perform a Valsalva maneuver.

Percussion

Percuss the patient's abdomen to assess for areas of dullness or tympany. Dullness may indicate organ enlargement, fluid accumulation, or a mass, while tympany may indicate the presence of gas in the intestines.

Differential Diagnosis for Abdominal Pain

The location of abdominal pain can provide clues to the underlying cause of the pain. Here are some common differential diagnoses for pain in each quadrant of the abdomen:

Right Upper Quadrant
  • Gallstones or cholecystitis
  • Hepatitis or other liver disease
  • Pancreatitis
  • Duodenal ulcer
  • Right lower lobe pneumonia
Left Upper Quadrant
  • Splenic rupture or infarction
  • Gastritis or peptic ulcer disease
  • Pancreatitis
  • Left lower lobe pneumonia
Right Lower Quadrant
  • Appendicitis
  • Diverticulitis
  • Salpingitis or ovarian cyst
  • Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis
  • Right lower lobe pneumonia
Left Lower Quadrant
  • Diverticulitis
  • Salpingitis or ovarian cyst
  • Crohn's disease or ulcerative colitis
  • Left lower lobe pneumonia

Additional Tests

Depending on the patient's symptoms and physical exam findings, additional tests may be ordered to further assess the abdominal area. These tests may include imaging studies such as an abdominal X-ray, ultrasound, or CT scan, as well as laboratory tests to assess for signs of infection or inflammation.

Common Findings and Abnormalities

  • Abdominal tenderness or pain
  • Masses or organ enlargement
  • Distended abdomen
  • Guarding or rigidity
  • Rebound tenderness
  • Hernias
  • Absent or hyperactive bowel sounds
  • Areas of dullness or tympany on percussion

Conclusion

An abdominal examination is an important component of the physical exam for patients presenting with abdominal symptoms in the emergency department. By using a systematic approach to inspecting, auscultating, palpating, and percussing the abdomen, healthcare providers can assess for any signs of underlying disease or pathology and provide appropriate care and treatment.
