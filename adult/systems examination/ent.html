ENT Exam

Overview

The ENT examination in the Emergency Department focuses on rapid identification of airway compromise, deep neck space infection, severe epistaxis, complications of otitis, foreign bodies and trauma. A structured approach includes history, general inspection and targeted examination of the ears, nose and throat.

Focused History

Key symptom clusters and red flags:

  • Ear symptoms: otalgia, discharge, hearing loss (sudden vs gradual), fullness, tinnitus, vertigo, history of trauma, inserting objects or cotton buds, recent water exposure.
  • Nasal symptoms: obstruction, rhinorrhoea (clear vs purulent), epistaxis, facial pain/pressure, anosmia, history of allergy, prior sinus disease or surgery.
  • Throat symptoms: sore throat, odynophagia, dysphagia, voice change, muffled “hot potato” voice, drooling, trismus, neck pain or swelling.
  • Vertigo/dizziness: true spinning vs light-headedness, positional triggers, associated hearing loss or neurological symptoms.
  • Systemic features: fever, weight loss, night sweats, recent upper respiratory infection, immunosuppression, diabetes, HIV, anticoagulation use.

Red flags requiring urgent attention: stridor, drooling, severe trismus, rapidly progressive neck swelling, respiratory distress, severe unilateral otalgia in diabetics (malignant otitis externa), severe unilateral facial swelling, or posterior epistaxis with haemodynamic compromise.

Physical Examination

Always begin with a general assessment of airway, breathing and circulation before focusing on ENT.

1. Ears

Inspect externally, then use an otoscope to examine the canal and tympanic membrane.

  • External ear and canal: deformity, trauma, lacerations, mastoid tenderness/swelling, erythema, oedema, discharge, foreign bodies.
  • Tympanic membrane: colour, position, translucency, light reflex, perforation, scarring, presence of fluid or bubbles, bullae.
  • Signs of infection:
    • Otitis externa – painful, swollen canal, debris, tragal/pinna tenderness.
    • Acute otitis media – bulging, opaque drum, reduced mobility (if pneumatic otoscopy), possible perforation with discharge.
  • Screening hearing tests: whisper test, finger rub; tuning fork tests (Rinne, Weber) if indicated.

2. Nose

Inspect externally then examine the nasal cavity with a light source and, if available, a nasal speculum.

  • External nose and midface: deformity, tenderness, crepitus, septal deviation from trauma.
  • Nasal cavities: mucosal colour, oedema, septum, turbinates, discharge (purulent, serous, bloody), crusting, foreign bodies, polyps or masses.
  • Epistaxis: identify anterior source (e.g. Kiesselbach’s area) vs suspected posterior bleed (blood in oropharynx, difficult visualisation, haemodynamic instability).
  • Septal haematoma: soft, fluctuant swelling on one or both sides of the septum after nasal trauma – urgent ENT referral.

3. Throat and Oral Cavity

Use a light source and tongue depressor; assess airway closely in distressed patients.

  • Oral cavity: lips, buccal mucosa, tongue (colour, coating, lesions, mobility), floor of mouth, dentition and gingivae.
  • Oropharynx: tonsils, pillars, posterior pharyngeal wall; look for erythema, exudates, swelling, uvular deviation, asymmetry.
  • Tonsillitis/pharyngitis: erythema, exudate, enlarged tonsils, tender cervical nodes, fever.
  • Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy): unilateral swelling, uvular deviation away from lesion, trismus, muffled “hot potato” voice, drooling.
  • Deep neck infection suspicion: neck swelling, severe pain, stiffness, toxic appearance, stridor or respiratory distress – low threshold for imaging and senior review.
  • Lymph nodes: palpate cervical nodes for tenderness, size, fixation and consistency.

Investigations in the ED

Further tests are guided by symptoms, examination and the patient’s stability. Common options include:

  • Audiometry: formal hearing assessment (often outpatient) for persistent or unexplained hearing loss or tinnitus.
  • Laboratory tests: FBC, CRP, blood cultures in suspected sepsis; coagulation profile in significant epistaxis, especially on anticoagulants.
  • CT scan: CT sinuses, temporal bone or neck for complicated sinusitis, deep neck infection, orbital involvement, mastoiditis, trauma or suspected fractures.
  • MRI: selected cases with suspected tumours, cranial nerve pathology or intracranial complications.
  • Endoscopy (flexible or rigid): ENT-performed assessment of nasal cavity, nasopharynx, larynx and hypopharynx in persistent hoarseness, suspected malignancy, airway obstruction or unexplained dysphagia.
  • Microbiology: throat or ear swabs where results will change management (e.g. persistent otorrhoea, immunocompromised host, suspected MRSA).

Summary

A structured ENT examination in the Emergency Department begins with airway assessment and red flag screening, followed by targeted examination of ears, nose and throat. Careful history, focused examination and judicious use of imaging and microbiology help to identify conditions that require urgent intervention, ENT referral or safe conservative management and follow-up.
