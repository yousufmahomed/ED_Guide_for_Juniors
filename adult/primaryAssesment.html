Primary Assessment – JACCOL(D)

JACCOL(D) is a quick primary assessment screen for systemic signs: Jaundice, Anaemia, Cyanosis, Clubbing, Oedema, Lymphadenopathy, and Dehydration. It is useful at the bedside to identify serious underlying disease early.

J – Jaundice

Clinical features: Yellowing of the skin and sclera, dark urine, pale stools.

Symptoms: Fatigue, weakness, abdominal pain, pruritus (itching), nausea.

Causes: Hepatocellular disease (e.g. viral hepatitis, cirrhosis), hemolytic anaemia, biliary obstruction (stones, strictures, malignancy), drug-induced liver injury.

Relevant investigations: Liver function tests (ALT, AST, ALP, GGT), bilirubin (total and conjugated), full blood count, ultrasound abdomen ± CT scan, viral hepatitis screen as indicated.

A – Anaemia

Clinical features: Pallor (conjunctiva, palms, nail beds), tachycardia, postural hypotension.

Symptoms: Fatigue, exertional dyspnoea, dizziness, headache, chest pain or angina in high-risk patients.

Causes: Iron deficiency (chronic blood loss, poor intake), vitamin B12/folate deficiency, chronic disease, haemolysis, acute blood loss.

Relevant investigations: Full blood count and indices, reticulocyte count, iron studies, B12/folate, stool occult blood test or endoscopy where indicated.

C – Cyanosis

Clinical features: Bluish discolouration of lips, tongue, nail beds or extremities.

Symptoms: Shortness of breath, chest pain, confusion, headache.

Causes: Hypoxia from respiratory disease (e.g. pneumonia, PE, COPD, asthma), congenital or acquired heart disease, shock, cold exposure, abnormal haemoglobin (e.g. methaemoglobinaemia).

Relevant investigations: Pulse oximetry, arterial blood gas, chest X-ray, ECG, echocardiogram, D-dimer / CT pulmonary angiogram if PE suspected.

C – Clubbing

Clinical features: Loss of nail-bed angle, spongy nail beds, bulbous enlargement of distal phalanges; may be unilateral or bilateral.

Symptoms: Often asymptomatic; may coexist with chronic cough, dyspnoea, weight loss or gastrointestinal symptoms.

Causes: Chronic lung disease (bronchiectasis, lung abscess, lung cancer, cystic fibrosis), cyanotic heart disease, inflammatory bowel disease, liver disease (e.g. cirrhosis).

Relevant investigations: Chest X-ray, CT chest, pulmonary function tests, echocardiogram, liver function tests, abdominal imaging as indicated.

O – Oedema

Clinical features: Pitting swelling of feet, ankles, legs or sacrum; may involve abdomen (ascites) or generalised anasarca.

Symptoms: Dyspnoea, orthopnoea, fatigue, rapid weight gain, reduced exercise tolerance.

Causes: Heart failure, nephrotic syndrome or renal failure, chronic liver disease/portal hypertension, lymphatic obstruction, venous insufficiency, some medications.

Relevant investigations: Urea/creatinine and electrolytes, liver function tests, urinalysis (protein), BNP where available, chest X-ray, echocardiogram, Doppler studies if DVT or venous disease suspected.

L – Lymphadenopathy

Clinical features: Enlarged lymph nodes (cervical, axillary, inguinal or generalised); may be tender or non-tender, discrete or matted.

Symptoms: Fever, night sweats, weight loss, local infective symptoms (sore throat, dental sepsis, skin lesions).

Causes: Local or systemic infection (bacterial, viral, TB), inflammatory or autoimmune disease, haematological malignancy (lymphoma, leukaemia), metastatic cancer.

Relevant investigations: Full blood count, inflammatory markers, HIV/TB serology where indicated, ultrasound, CT/MRI; fine needle aspiration or lymph node biopsy if malignancy is suspected.

D – Dehydration

Clinical features: Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, sunken eyes (especially in children), reduced urine output, tachycardia, hypotension, cool peripheries, delayed capillary refill.

Symptoms: Thirst, fatigue, dizziness or presyncope, confusion, headache.

Causes: Vomiting, diarrhoea, polyuria (e.g. uncontrolled diabetes, diuretics), fever, excessive sweating, poor oral intake, sepsis.

Relevant investigations: Electrolytes and renal function, capillary or venous glucose, lactate if sepsis suspected, urine dipstick and specific gravity, bedside weight in paediatrics if available.
