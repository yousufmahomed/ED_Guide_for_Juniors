Lumbar Puncture Checklist

Indications for Lumbar Puncture

  • Diagnostic evaluation of meningitis, encephalitis, or other central nervous system infections
  • Diagnostic evaluation of subarachnoid hemorrhage
  • Diagnostic evaluation of demyelinating or inflammatory central nervous system disorders (e.g., multiple sclerosis, neurosarcoidosis)
  • Diagnostic evaluation of idiopathic intracranial hypertension (pseudotumor cerebri)
  • Therapeutic drainage of cerebrospinal fluid in cases of idiopathic intracranial hypertension or other disorders
  • Administration of intrathecal medications (e.g., chemotherapy, antibiotics, spinal anesthesia)

Contraindications for Lumbar Puncture

  • Increased intracranial pressure (risk of brain herniation)
  • Suspected spinal cord compression or spinal epidural abscess
  • Coagulopathy or ongoing anticoagulation therapy (increased risk of spinal hematoma)
  • Infection at the puncture site
  • Uncorrected hypovolemia or shock

Equipment and Supplies

  1. Personal protective equipment (gloves, mask, gown)
  2. Sterile drape
  3. Local anesthetic (e.g., lidocaine)
  4. Skin disinfectant (e.g., chlorhexidine or povidone-iodine)
  5. Syringe and needle for administering local anesthetic
  6. Spinal needle (22-25 gauge) with stylet
  7. Manometer for measuring cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) opening pressure
  8. Sterile specimen tubes for collecting CSF
  9. Bandage or sterile dressing to cover the puncture site

Lumbar Puncture Procedure Steps

  1. Verify the indications and review contraindications for lumbar puncture
  2. Obtain informed consent from the patient or their legal guardian
  3. Position the patient correctly (usually sitting or lying on their side with knees drawn up to the chest and chin tucked in)
  4. Identify the appropriate intervertebral space (usually L3-L4 or L4-L5)
  5. Prepare the puncture site using a skin disinfectant and sterile draping
  6. Administer local anesthetic to the puncture site
  7. Insert the spinal needle with the bevel parallel to the longitudinal axis of the spine, and advance it through the skin, subcutaneous tissue, and ligaments until the subarachnoid space is entered
  8. Remove the stylet and attach the manometer to measure the CSF opening pressure (if indicated)
  9. Collect CSF samples in sterile specimen tubes
  10. Measure the CSF closing pressure (if indicated) and remove the spinal needle
  11. Apply a bandage or sterile dressing to the puncture site
  12. Monitor the patient for any immediate complications (e.g., headache, infection, bleeding)
  13. Send the CSF samples to the laboratory for appropriate analysis
