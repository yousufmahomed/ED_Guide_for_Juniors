Psychiatric History

Psychiatric History in the Emergency Department

Presenting Complaint / Main Problem

Begin with the patient’s own words: “What brought you here today?” If the patient is incoherent, severely disorganised or psychotic, obtain collateral information from family, EMS or other staff.

  • Clarify the main symptom clusters: mood, anxiety, psychosis, behaviour, substance use, cognitive change.
  • Ask about the onset, time course, and precipitating events (loss, conflict, trauma, substances, medical illness).
  • Explore context: social, cultural and economic factors that may be driving the current crisis.
  • Identify any **immediate safety concerns**: suicidality, aggression, inability to care for self, vulnerability.

Presenting Symptoms – Key Domains

Screen systematically across the main symptom areas:

  • Depression: low mood, anhedonia, low energy, sleep and appetite change, guilt, poor concentration, suicidal thoughts or plans.
  • Anxiety: excessive worry, panic attacks, restlessness, muscle tension, avoidance, physical symptoms (palpitations, SOB, tremor).
  • Agitation: pacing, motor restlessness, irritability, aggression, inability to sit still.
  • Psychosis: hallucinations (especially auditory), delusions (paranoid, grandiose, guilt, reference), disorganised behaviour, thought disorder.
  • Mania / Hypomania: elevated or irritable mood, decreased need for sleep, increased energy, pressured speech, racing thoughts, risk-taking behaviour, grandiosity.
  • Substance use: type, amount, route and frequency; recent binges or withdrawals; impact on functioning.
  • Risk symptoms: suicidal ideation, plans, previous attempts; homicidal or violent thoughts; self-harm behaviour.

Past Psychiatric History

Obtain a concise but clear psychiatric history:

  • Previous diagnoses, episodes and typical pattern of illness.
  • Previous admissions (dates, duration, voluntary/involuntary).
  • Past self-harm or suicide attempts: method, severity, triggers, intent.
  • Previous episodes of aggression or violence and what helped.
  • Past medications, doses, response and side-effects; ECT or other treatments.

Medical, Family & Social History

Medical history:

  • Chronic illnesses (epilepsy, HIV, endocrine, neurological disease, head injury).
  • Current medications, including over-the-counter and traditional medicines.
  • Alcohol and substance history (if not already detailed above).

Family history:

  • Psychiatric disorders in first-degree relatives (mood, psychosis, substance use).
  • History of suicide, self-harm or violence in family.

Social history:

  • Living circumstances, support system, finances, dependants, caregiving roles.
  • Work or school functioning, recent losses or stressors.
  • History of trauma, abuse or neglect (as far as safely explored in ED context).

Mental Status Examination (MSE)

The MSE is a structured, “snapshot” assessment of current mental state. In the ED, focus on high-yield features that affect risk, capacity and diagnosis.

1. Appearance & Behaviour

  • Grooming, hygiene, dress, posture, eye contact.
  • Level of cooperation, agitation or retardation, bizarre or disinhibited behaviour.

2. Speech

  • Rate, volume, tone and fluency (e.g. normal, pressured, slow, whispered).
  • Abnormal patterns: flight of ideas, tangentiality, neologisms, mutism, thought blocking.

3. Mood & Affect

  • Mood: the patient’s subjective emotional state (e.g. depressed, anxious, euphoric, irritable).
  • Affect: observed emotional expression – range (restricted/flat/labile), appropriateness to content, reactivity.

4. Thought Form (Process)

  • Logical, coherent and goal-directed vs disorganised, circumstantial, tangential, loose associations, flight of ideas.
  • Any thought-blocking or perseveration.

5. Thought Content

  • Delusions (persecutory, grandiose, guilt, nihilistic, referential, somatic).
  • Obsessions, compulsions, ruminations, phobias, preoccupations.
  • Suicidal or homicidal ideation: frequency, intent, plan, access to means.

6. Perception

  • Hallucinations (auditory, visual, tactile, olfactory, gustatory).
  • Illusions, depersonalisation, derealisation if present.

7. Cognition

  • Level of consciousness, orientation (person, place, time, situation).
  • Attention and concentration (e.g. serial 7s, WORLD backwards).
  • Memory and obvious language or visuospatial deficits.

8. Insight & Judgement

  • Insight into illness (does the patient see a problem, accept need for treatment?).
  • Judgement and impulse control: recent risky choices, aggression, disinhibition.

Document key MSE findings clearly – they are crucial for medico-legal decisions and future reviews.

Risk Assessment & Managing Aggression

Every ED psychiatric assessment must include a clear risk assessment (self-harm, harm to others, vulnerability, absconding, neglect).

Managing a violent, highly aggressive or armed patient:

  1. Safety first: call for help, alert security; remove unnecessary staff and patients. If weapons are involved, involve security and law enforcement immediately.
  2. Environmental control: ensure an open exit for staff, minimise stimuli, remove potential weapons from the room.
  3. Rapid assessment: try to identify likely drivers (substances, psychosis, delirium, severe agitation, pain).
  4. Verbal de-escalation: calm, non-threatening stance, simple language, active listening, offer choices where possible. Avoid arguing, shouting or sudden movements.
  5. Medication: if verbal de-escalation fails and risk remains high, use appropriate rapid-tranquilisation as per local protocols and with monitoring.
  6. Restraint: physical restraints are a last resort; use only if the patient is an immediate danger to self or others, with appropriate orders, documentation, monitoring and regular review.
  7. Ongoing review: once calm, reassess mental state, diagnosis and ongoing risk; plan disposition (psychiatric admission, medical stay, safe discharge with follow-up).

All staff should receive regular training in de-escalation and safe restraint techniques. Follow your hospital’s mental health and restraint policies.

Treatment Plan

Develop a plan tailored to the patient’s diagnosis, risk and social context:

  • Immediate ED management (e.g. sedation, antipsychotics, antidepressants, mood stabilisers, anxiolytics, management of medical comorbidity).
  • Need for admission (medical vs psychiatric; voluntary vs involuntary according to local legislation).
  • Psychosocial interventions: psychoeducation, safety planning, family involvement, social work support.
  • Follow-up: outpatient psychiatry, counselling, substance use services, community resources.

Document clearly: working diagnosis, risk level, capacity, legal status, and agreed plan.

Assessment Aids & Tools

The following tools can help quantify symptom severity and support your clinical judgement. In the ED they are adjuncts – they do not replace a full clinical assessment.

Any child or adolescent younger than 16 years with significant psychiatric symptoms should be referred for specialist psychiatric assessment, in line with local child and adolescent mental health policies.

Disorder-Specific Severity Measures – Adults

  • LEVEL 2—Depression—Adult (PROMIS Emotional Distress—Depression—Short Form)
  • Severity Measure for Separation Anxiety Disorder—Adult
  • Severity Measure for Specific Phobia—Adult
  • Severity Measure for Social Anxiety Disorder—Adult
  • Severity Measure for Panic Disorder—Adult
  • Severity Measure for Agoraphobia—Adult
  • Severity Measure for Generalized Anxiety Disorder—Adult
  • Severity of Posttraumatic Stress Symptoms—Adult (NSESS)
  • Severity of Acute Stress Symptoms—Adult (NSESS)
  • Severity of Dissociative Symptoms—Adult (DES-B)

Disorder-Specific Severity Measures – Children (11–17)

  • Severity Measure for Depression—Child 11–17 (PHQ-A Adapted)
  • Severity Measure for Separation Anxiety Disorder—Child 11–17
  • Severity Measure for Specific Phobia—Child 11–17
  • Severity Measure for Social Anxiety Disorder—Child 11–17
  • Severity Measure for Panic Disorder—Child 11–17
  • Severity Measure for Agoraphobia—Child 11–17
  • Severity Measure for Generalized Anxiety Disorder—Child 11–17
  • Severity of Posttraumatic Stress Symptoms—Child (NSESS)
  • Severity of Acute Stress Symptoms—Child (NSESS)
  • Severity of Dissociative Symptoms—Child (DES-B)

While you are still learning or working in a busy ED, you can use these tools, calculate a score, and then discuss the interpretation with a senior. The scoring and clinical nuance can become complex.
