Basic ECG Guide for Junior Doctors

Introduction

An electrocardiogram (ECG) is a test that records the electrical activity of the heart. ECGs are commonly used to diagnose heart problems, such as arrhythmias, heart attacks, and other cardiac conditions. As a junior doctor, it is important to have a basic understanding of how to interpret ECGs.

ECG Components

There are several key components of an ECG that you should be familiar with:

  • P wave: Represents atrial depolarization (contraction).
  • QRS complex: Represents ventricular depolarization (contraction).
  • T wave: Represents ventricular repolarization (relaxation).
  • ST segment: Represents the time between ventricular depolarization and repolarization.

By understanding these components, you can begin to interpret ECG tracings.

Interpreting ECGs

When interpreting ECGs, there are several key steps to follow:

  1. Check the patient's details, such as name, age, and sex, to ensure that the correct patient is being tested.
  2. Check the ECG tracing to ensure that it is of good quality and free from artifacts, such as muscle tremors or electrical interference.
  3. Identify the heart rate by counting the number of QRS complexes in a six-second strip and multiplying by 10.
  4. Assess the rhythm by examining the P waves and the regularity of the QRS complexes. Determine whether the rhythm is sinus (normal) or abnormal, such as atrial fibrillation.
  5. Examine the ST segment to identify any abnormalities, such as ST elevation or depression, which may indicate ischemia or injury to the heart.

Conclusion

ECG interpretation is an important skill for junior doctors to develop. By understanding the key components of an ECG and following a systematic approach to interpretation, you can diagnose and manage a range of cardiac conditions.
