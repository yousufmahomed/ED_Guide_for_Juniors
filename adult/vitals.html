Vitals

Body Temperature

Normal adult oral temperature is usually between 36.1 °C and 37.2 °C. Individual values vary with time of day, age, activity and baseline health.

A temperature outside this range may indicate fever or hypothermia and must be interpreted in context.

Temperature grading:

  • Normal: around 37 °C (roughly 36.1–37.2 °C).
  • Low-grade fever: 38–39 °C – often viral infection, early infection or mild inflammatory process.
  • Moderate fever: 39–40 °C – common with bacterial infections, some inflammatory or autoimmune conditions.
  • High fever: >40 °C – red flag; think serious infection (sepsis, meningitis, malaria), drug reactions or heat stroke. Needs urgent evaluation.
  • Mild hypothermia: 32–35 °C – shivering, numbness, confusion, tachycardia.
  • Moderate hypothermia: 28–32 °C – slurred speech, reduced GCS, bradycardia, slow respirations.
  • Severe hypothermia: <28 °C – high risk of arrhythmias, very slow pulse and respirations, decreased or absent consciousness. Handle gently.

Blood Pressure

For most adults, a normal BP is roughly <120/80 mmHg. Always interpret in the clinical context (pain, anxiety, pregnancy, sepsis, etc.).

Hypertension categories:

  • Stage 1 hypertension: SBP 130–139 mmHg or DBP 80–89 mmHg.
  • Stage 2 hypertension: SBP ≥140 mmHg or DBP ≥90 mmHg.

Severe hypertension in the ED:

  • Hypertensive urgency: BP usually ≥180 systolic and/or ≥110 diastolic, without acute target organ damage.
    – Needs BP reduction over hours–days and close follow-up, not rapid IV lowering.
  • Hypertensive emergency: BP ≥180/120 mmHg with acute organ damage (neurological, cardiac, renal, aortic, etc.).
    – Treat as an emergency with IV antihypertensives and monitored setting.

Pulse / Heart Rate

Normal resting adult heart rate is typically 60–100 bpm. Check rate, rhythm, volume and compare with monitor/ECG if available.

  • Tachycardia (>100 bpm): pain, fever, anxiety, hypovolaemia, sepsis, PE, arrhythmias, drugs, thyroid disease, anaemia, etc.
    • Sinus tachycardia: regular rhythm, usually appropriate to a trigger (pain, fever, hypoxia).
    • Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT): fast, often regular, narrow-complex; may need vagal manoeuvres / adenosine.
    • Ventricular tachycardia: wide-complex, potentially unstable or pulseless – treat per ACLS, can deteriorate into VF.
  • Bradycardia (<60 bpm): can be normal (fit young adult) or due to drugs, conduction disease, raised ICP, hypothermia, etc.
    • Sinus bradycardia: regular, often benign if patient well perfused.
    • AV block: 1°, 2°, 3° block – risk of syncope / low output; manage according to degree and symptoms.
  • Arrhythmias: irregular or abnormal rhythm; think AF, atrial flutter, SVT, VT, PVCs, etc.
    • PVCs: occasional isolated beats often benign; frequent, multifocal, or “runs” need evaluation.
    • Ventricular fibrillation: immediately life-threatening – requires defibrillation and ALS.

Respiratory Rate

Count breaths for a full minute wherever possible. Normal adult resting RR is about 12–20 breaths/min.

RR is one of the earliest markers of deterioration and should never be ignored.

Respiratory distress grading (bedside):

  • Mild: RR raised but can speak full sentences; may have mild accessory muscle use.
  • Moderate: RR clearly increased, speaking only phrases, using accessory muscles, may have wheeze, recession or nasal flaring.
  • Severe: unable to speak full words, gasping, marked recession, cyanosis or agitation/“air hunger”.
    – Treat as high-priority; prepare for escalation (NIV/intubation) as indicated.

Oxygen Saturation (SpO₂)

Normal SpO₂ in most adults is about 95–100 % on room air at sea level. Always interpret together with RR, work of breathing and GCS.

Pulse oximetry can be unreliable in poor perfusion, movement, dark nail polish, high ambient light, CO poisoning, anaemia, some haemoglobinopathies and severe hypotension.

Hypoxia grading (approximate):

  • Normal: 95–100 %.
    – No specific treatment if asymptomatic and clinically well.
  • Mild hypoxia: 91–94 %.
    – Assess cause; often give low-flow oxygen and investigate.
  • Moderate hypoxia: 86–90 %.
    – Usually requires supplemental oxygen, close monitoring and investigation (CXR, ABG, etc.).
  • Severe hypoxia: <85 %.
    – Medical emergency; high-flow oxygen / advanced airway, consider NIV/intubation; treat underlying cause urgently.

COPD note: Many COPD patients are chronically hypoxic and may sit safely around 88–92 %. In known CO₂ retainers, aim for a target range (often 88–92 %) per local protocol and monitor CO₂ (VBG/ABG). Over-oxygenation can worsen CO₂ retention and mental status.

Blood Glucose (Capillary or Venous)

Blood glucose grading (fasting, mmol/L):

  • Hypoglycaemia: <3.9 mmol/L (symptoms often <3.2 mmol/L).
    – Treat promptly with oral / IV glucose depending on GCS; search for cause.
  • Normal fasting: 3.9–5.5 mmol/L.
  • Pre-diabetes: 5.6–6.9 mmol/L (impaired fasting glucose).
  • Diabetes: ≥7.0 mmol/L (fasting) or ≥11.1 mmol/L with classic symptoms, or ≥11.1 mmol/L at 2 h on OGTT.
    – In the ED, think of hyperglycaemic emergencies (DKA/HHS) when high glucose + illness / acidosis / dehydration are present.
