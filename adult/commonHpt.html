Commonly Used Antihypertensive Drugs

Note: The doses below are typical adult maintenance doses. Always individualise therapy and follow local / national hypertension and emergency guidelines. Acute BP management in the ED should be discussed with a senior, especially in suspected hypertensive emergency.

Class Drug Dosage (Adult, Typical) Route Indication (Summary)
Diuretics Hydrochlorothiazide 12.5–25 mg once daily, titrate according to response Oral Hypertension, mild–moderate oedema

Spironolactone 12.5–100 mg once daily, titrate according to response Oral Resistant hypertension, heart failure, hyperaldosteronism
Beta Blockers Metoprolol 50–200 mg once or twice daily, titrate according to response Oral Hypertension, angina, rate control in AF, heart failure (selected patients)
Propranolol 20–320 mg/day in divided doses, titrate according to response Oral, IV Hypertension, angina, arrhythmias, migraine prophylaxis, thyrotoxicosis symptoms

Enalapril 2.5–40 mg once or twice daily, titrate according to response Oral Hypertension, heart failure
ARBs Losartan 25–100 mg once daily, titrate according to response Oral Hypertension, diabetic nephropathy, ACEi-intolerant patients
Valsartan 80–320 mg once daily, titrate according to response Oral Hypertension, heart failure, post-MI

Emergency Department Tips

  • Hypertensive emergency vs urgency: Emergency = markedly elevated BP with acute target-organ damage (e.g. encephalopathy, stroke, ACS, pulmonary oedema, aortic dissection, AKI). These patients need IV agents and controlled BP reduction with senior input.
  • Do not aggressively drop BP in asymptomatic chronic hypertension: Rapid reductions can worsen cerebral, coronary and renal perfusion.
  • Know your go-to IV agents: In many hospitals, IV labetalol, GTN and furosemide are commonly available; exact choice should follow your local protocol.
  • Check ECG, creatinine, troponin, urine and fundi in significantly high BPs to screen for organ damage where feasible.
  • Restarting chronic meds: Where non-adherence is suspected (and no contraindication), it is usually safe to restart the patient’s usual oral regimen and arrange early follow-up.
  • Pregnancy and postpartum: Use pregnancy-safe options (e.g. labetalol, nifedipine, methyldopa) and follow obstetric / hypertensive disorders of pregnancy guidelines.

Common Side Effects & Cautions (Quick Reference)

Class / Drug Common Side Effects Key Cautions
Thiazide diuretics (e.g. HCTZ) Hyponatraemia, hypokalaemia, ↑ uric acid, mild ↑ glucose Monitor U&E; caution in gout, severe renal impairment, elderly / frail patients.
Loop diuretics (Furosemide) Hypovolaemia, electrolyte disturbance, ototoxicity at high doses Avoid excessive diuresis; monitor renal function, K⁺, Mg²⁺.
Spironolactone Hyperkalaemia, gynaecomastia, menstrual irregularities Caution in renal impairment, ACEi/ARB combination, and baseline high K⁺.
ACE Inhibitors (Lisinopril, Enalapril) Dry cough, hyperkalaemia, ↑ creatinine, angioedema (rare) Avoid in pregnancy, bilateral renal artery stenosis; monitor renal function & K⁺.
ARBs (Losartan, Valsartan) Hyperkalaemia, dizziness, ↑ creatinine Similar cautions as ACEi; usually used if ACEi cough or angioedema history.
Beta Blockers Bradycardia, fatigue, bronchospasm, masking of hypoglycaemia Avoid in acute asthma/COPD exacerbation, severe bradycardia, acute decomp HF.
Dihydropyridine CCBs (Amlodipine) Ankle oedema, flushing, headache Can worsen peripheral oedema; generally safe in asthma/COPD.
Non-DHP CCBs (Diltiazem) Bradycardia, heart block, constipation, hypotension Avoid in significant LV dysfunction, pre-existing heart block, with beta-blockers in some patients.
Central agents (Clonidine, Methyldopa) Sedation, dry mouth, rebound hypertension if abruptly stopped Do not stop suddenly; methyldopa can cause hepatitis / haemolysis – monitor if on long term.

Reminder: For hypertensive emergencies and complex patients, stabilise the patient and follow your local protocol or discuss with a senior / specialist physician.
