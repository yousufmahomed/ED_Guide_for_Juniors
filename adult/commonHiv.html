Commonly Used HIV Drugs

Note: Doses below are typical adult maintenance doses. Initiation, switching or interruption of ART should follow your hospital / HIV clinic guidelines and be discussed with a senior or HIV specialist.

Drug Typical Adult Dose Route Indication (Summary)
Tenofovir disoproxil fumarate (TDF) 300 mg once daily Oral Backbone NRTI in ART; used in HIV-1 treatment and some PrEP regimens
Emtricitabine (FTC) 200 mg once daily Oral NRTI; combined with TDF in HIV-1 treatment and PrEP regimens
Dolutegravir (DTG) 50 mg once daily Oral Integrase inhibitor; core agent in many first-line regimens
Raltegravir (RAL) 400 mg twice daily Oral Integrase inhibitor; used in ART and sometimes PEP
Atazanavir (ATV) 300–400 mg once daily with ritonavir 100 mg once daily (or 200 mg if given with TDF) Oral Protease inhibitor (usually boosted); component of some ART regimens
Ritonavir (RTV) 100 mg once or twice daily (boosting dose) Oral Pharmacokinetic booster for protease inhibitors
Darunavir (DRV) 800 mg once daily + RTV 100 mg once daily, or 600 mg BD + RTV 100 mg BD Oral Boosted protease inhibitor for ART, especially in treatment-experienced patients
Lopinavir/ritonavir (LPV/r) 800/200 mg once or twice daily Oral Boosted protease inhibitor combination for ART

Emergency Department Tips

  • Do not start or stop chronic ART lightly: Avoid changing long-term regimens from the ED unless clear toxicity or life-threatening reaction is suspected and a senior / HIV specialist is involved.
  • Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP): Use your local PEP guideline for occupational and sexual exposures. Start as soon as possible (ideally within hours, and usually within 72 hours) and ensure good follow-up.
  • Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP): Initial counselling and prescriptions should usually be done via HIV / primary care clinics; the ED role is often identifying candidates and referring appropriately.
  • Drug interactions: Always consider interactions with TB drugs, anticonvulsants, azoles, anticoagulants and anti-arrhythmics – especially with boosted protease inhibitors (RTV / LPV/r / DRV).
  • Acute presentations: In suspected IRIS, severe opportunistic infection, liver failure or lactic acidosis, urgently discuss with an HIV / ID specialist before stopping or changing ART.
  • Adherence questions: Ask about missed doses, stock-outs and traditional / over-the-counter medicines.

Common Side Effects & Cautions (Quick Reference)

Drug Common Side Effects Key Cautions
TDF Renal dysfunction, ↓ bone mineral density, mild GI upset Avoid or reduce dose in significant renal impairment; monitor creatinine and eGFR.
FTC Generally well tolerated; headache, nausea Adjust dose in renal impairment; usually given in fixed-dose combination with TDF.
DTG Insomnia, headache, GI upset, weight gain Watch for interactions with cation-containing antacids; discuss with specialist in early pregnancy or women of child-bearing potential as per local guidance.
RAL Headache, nausea, rash, rare myopathy CK elevation possible; separate from polyvalent cation antacids.
ATV Hyperbilirubinaemia (jaundice), GI upset Avoid with proton pump inhibitors in some regimens; check for drug interactions; use with RTV as booster.
DRV + RTV GI upset, rash, ↑ lipids, liver enzyme elevation Many drug interactions (CYP3A4); caution in hepatic impairment; always confirm doses with guideline / senior.
LPV/r GI upset, hyperlipidaemia, hyperglycaemia Risk of QT / PR prolongation and metabolic complications; check interactions with other QT-prolonging drugs.

Reminder: Management of ART, PEP and PrEP must follow national / provincial HIV guidelines. When unsure, stabilise the patient and contact your local HIV / ID team or senior doctor for advice.
