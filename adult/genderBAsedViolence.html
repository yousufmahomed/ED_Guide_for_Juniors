Gender-Based Violence and Sexual Assault

Purpose of this Guide

This guide is for medical officers working in the Emergency Department to help identify, manage and appropriately refer patients affected by gender-based violence (GBV) and sexual assault. It complements the full hospital protocol and does not replace local policies, national legislation or specialist forensic guidance.

General Principles

  • Provide trauma-informed, non-judgmental care at all times.
  • Introduce yourself, explain your role and what will happen next.
  • Ensure privacy, dignity and safety – separate the patient from any person who may be the perpetrator.
  • Explain the limits of confidentiality, including mandatory reporting for children and vulnerable persons.
  • Always obtain informed consent for examination, investigations, forensic evidence collection and treatment.
  • Document carefully and objectively; avoid blaming language or speculation.

Emergency Department Markers for Possible GBV / Sexual Assault

  • Physical injuries: bruises, cuts, abrasions, burns, strangulation marks, defensive injuries.
  • Genital or anal pain, bleeding or evidence of trauma.
  • Signs of recent struggle, torn or bloodstained clothing, dishevelled appearance.
  • Disclosure of sexual assault or intimate partner violence by the patient.
  • Inconsistent, vague or changing explanations for injuries.
  • Delay in seeking care after a significant injury or assault.
  • Repeated ED visits with non-specific complaints, chronic pain, self-harm or overdose.
  • Patient appears highly distressed, anxious, withdrawn, fearful or dissociated.
  • Partner or family member who is overly controlling, refuses to leave the room or speaks for the patient.
  • Pregnancy in a child or adolescent, especially where the history is unclear or concerning.

Atypical Features (Do Not Exclude Assault)

  • Denial or minimisation of violence due to fear, shame, dependence or stigma.
  • Reluctance to share details, fragmented history or “poor recall” in the context of trauma.
  • No visible injuries despite a clear allegation of sexual assault.
  • Patient declines or is unsure about forensic examination.
  • Patient expresses fear of retaliation or pressure not to report.

Practical Steps in the ED

  1. Stabilise and Ensure Immediate Safety
    Manage airway, breathing and circulation first. Treat life-threatening injuries and assess for strangulation, head trauma, internal injuries or suicidal ideation.
  2. Create a Private, Safe Space
    Move the patient to a private cubicle where discussions cannot be overheard. Ask any accompanying person to step out if appropriate and safe to do so.
  3. Trauma-Informed History
    Use open-ended, non-leading questions. Allow the patient to describe events in their own words. Avoid repetitive questioning. Ask about:
    • Time and nature of assault (including type of contact and condom use).
    • Use of alcohol, drugs or loss of consciousness.
    • Any washing, changing of clothes, urination or defecation since the assault.
    • Previous episodes of violence, children or others at risk in the home.
  4. Explain Consent, Options and Confidentiality
    Clearly explain available options:
    • Medical examination and treatment (injuries, pregnancy, infections).
    • Forensic evidence collection and completion of medico-legal forms.
    • Reporting to SAPS or delaying that decision while still receiving care.
    Explain the limits of confidentiality, including mandatory reporting for children and certain vulnerable adults, according to South African law.
  5. Forensic Examination and Evidence (If Indicated and Consented)
    If within an appropriate time window, and if the patient consents:
    • Follow your local protocol and sexual assault evidence collection kit instructions.
    • Document injuries carefully (size, colour, site) and emotional state.
    • Preserve clothing or other items in dry, labelled bags as evidence.
    • Complete required medico-legal forms (e.g. J88) when a case is reported.
    • Maintain chain of custody for all specimens and evidence.
    If the patient is not yet ready to report, you may still collect and store evidence where local policy allows.
  6. Medical Treatment
    Provide appropriate treatment, including:
    • Treatment of physical injuries and pain control.
    • Emergency contraception where pregnancy is possible, ideally within 72 hours (some methods up to 120 hours).
    • HIV post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) as soon as possible and within 72 hours of exposure.
    • Empiric STI prophylaxis and hepatitis B vaccination ± immunoglobulin as per protocol.
    • Baseline tests (HIV, pregnancy, syphilis and others according to local guidelines).
  7. Psychological and Social Support
    Provide empathic listening and validation. Where available, involve:
    • Social worker or psychologist experienced in trauma.
    • Local GBV/sexual assault services, shelters or crisis centres.
    • Trusted support persons identified by the patient.
  8. Reporting and Legal Options
    • For adults with capacity, explain options for reporting to SAPS; support but do not coerce.
    • For children (<18 years) and certain vulnerable adults, there is a legal duty to report suspected abuse to the authorities according to national law and hospital policy.
    • Clarify that medical care, PEP and other treatments are not dependent on laying a charge.
  9. Documentation
    Record:
    • Date and time of arrival, examination and any treatments given.
    • History in the patient’s own words where possible (use quotation marks).
    • Full physical findings, including negative findings (e.g. “no bruising noted on…”, etc.).
    • Emotional state, expressed fears and safety concerns.
    • Advice given, referrals offered and whether the patient agreed or declined.
  10. Follow-Up and Safety Planning
    Arrange or advise follow-up for:
    • PEP review, HIV and pregnancy testing at recommended intervals.
    • Review of injuries, mental health follow-up and social work support.
    • Safety planning, including shelters or alternate safe accommodation if needed.

Survivors may be experiencing intense fear, shame and confusion. Your role is to provide compassionate, competent care; safeguard their rights; and support their choices while ensuring protection for children and other vulnerable persons at risk.
