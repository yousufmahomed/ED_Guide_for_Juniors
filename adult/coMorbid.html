Past History and Co-Morbid Conditions

Co-Morbidities

The mnemonic D-E-A-T-H is useful; Diabetes, Epilepsy, Asthma, TB, Hypertension/HIV. In this section we've added a few more that can also be asked about.

  • Diabetes Type I

    • Try and get a history of medications and dosages especially

    Previous DKA episodes are very important to note down

  • Diabetes Type II

    • Try and find out age of onset, and the current medications used are valuable pieces of information.

    Dietary habits, body habitus and family history of the disease should also be asked

    Previous admissions related to the condition are also important.

  • Epilepsy

    • Age of onset is important.

    You should also try and ask about:-
    Medications used and dosages,
    If it was spontaneous onset or due to trauma.
    Past infections, vascular abnormalities and growths can also be a cause.
    If the patient is on treatment it is important to know how lng since their last seizure

  • Asthma

    • Again, you need to ask about medications and dosages

    Age of onset or triggering factors such as infections or exercise.

    Previous admissions and a history of having severe asthma attacks , or even ICU admissions for Status asthmaticus.

  • TB
    • Important!!! Make sure you can look at the TB DOTS (green) Card , or equivalent in your setting.
    • It gives you the timeline, dosages, changes and complications.
    • If you cannot see it, make sure you try and get as much information about this from the patient
    • And differentiate the type of TB
      • Pulmonary
      • Abdominal
      • Joint
      • Spinal
      • Meningitis
      • Intra-cranial
  • Hypertension

    • Medications and dosages are important.

    If the hypertension has an underlying cause like renal vascular disease, its extremely important to note

    Although, most patients who have secondary HPT they will inform you of the underlying cause.

    Previous admissions and any known target organ damage is important to know.

  • HIV

    • The doctor may ask whether the patient knows their HIV status, whether they have been tested for HIV in the past, and if they have been diagnosed with HIV previously.

    You may ask about any medications the patient is taking, including antiretroviral therapy (ART) or prophylaxis for HIV.

    You can ask about any allergies the patient has, as some medications used to treat HIV or related infections can cause allergic reactions.

    Compliance is also extremely important
