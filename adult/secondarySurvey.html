Trauma Secondary Survey

The trauma secondary survey is a systematic assessment of a patient who has experienced trauma. It is performed after the primary survey, which focuses on identifying and addressing any life-threatening injuries.

General

During the general examination, observe the patient's appearance, level of consciousness, and any visible signs of trauma. Pay attention to the following:

  • General appearance and level of consciousness
  • Skin examination for bruising, lacerations, and signs of trauma
  • Head and neck examination for signs of injury, including skull and facial fractures, and cervical spine injury
  • Chest examination for signs of injury, including flail chest, pneumothorax, and hemothorax
  • Abdominal examination for signs of injury, including distension, guarding, and rebound tenderness
  • Pelvic examination for signs of injury, including instability or pain with palpation
  • Extremity examination for signs of injury, including fractures, dislocations, and soft tissue injuries

Neurological

Evaluate the patient's neurological status, including level of consciousness, orientation, memory, pupillary response, and movement and sensation in all extremities. Use the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS) to assess the patient's level of consciousness:

  • Level of consciousness, orientation, and memory
  • Pupillary response and size
  • Movement and sensation in all extremities
  • Glasgow Coma Scale score (use the calculator by clicking here)

Other Tests

In addition to the general and neurological assessments, the trauma secondary survey may involve several other tests and diagnostic tools to further evaluate the patient's condition and identify potential injuries. Some of these tests include:

  • Diagnostic imaging, such as X-rays, CT scans, and ultrasound, to visualize internal injuries and fractures
  • Lab tests, including complete blood count (CBC), blood typing and crossmatching, coagulation studies, and arterial blood gas (ABG) analysis, to assess the patient's overall health and identify potential complications
  • Electrocardiogram (ECG) to evaluate the patient's heart function and identify any cardiac abnormalities or arrhythmias
  • Urinalysis to screen for urinary tract injuries, infection, or other issues
  • Serial vital signs monitoring to track the patient's response to interventions and detect any changes in their condition

Documentation and Monitoring

Documenting the findings from the trauma secondary survey is essential for maintaining accurate records of the patient's condition, treatment, and progress. Ensure that all assessments, interventions, and outcomes are thoroughly documented in the patient's medical record. Consider using a standardized documentation form or electronic medical record (EMR) template to ensure consistency and completeness.

After completing the secondary survey, continue to monitor the patient's condition closely, and reevaluate them as needed. If the patient's condition changes, notify the appropriate healthcare team members and modify the treatment plan accordingly.

Summary

The trauma secondary survey is a crucial component of evaluating and managing patients who have experienced trauma. By performing a systematic assessment that includes general, neurological, and other tests, healthcare providers can identify and treat potential injuries, monitor the patient's condition, and ensure appropriate documentation and communication among the healthcare team. Remember that the secondary survey should be performed only after the primary survey has been completed and any life-threatening injuries have been addressed.
