Secondary Survey

Trauma Secondary Survey

The secondary survey is a structured, head-to-toe evaluation performed after the primary survey and initial resuscitation (ABCDE) are complete and the patient is stable enough for a more detailed assessment.

The goal is to identify all important injuries, obtain a focused trauma history, and plan appropriate investigations and definitive management.

1. Trauma History – “AMPLE”

Obtain a concise trauma history using the ATLS AMPLE mnemonic:

  • A – Allergies: drugs, latex, food.
  • M – Medications: chronic meds, anticoagulants/antiplatelets, insulin, etc.
  • P – Past medical history: co-morbidities, previous surgery, pregnancy, prior trauma.
  • L – Last meal: time and nature of last oral intake (important for theatre).
  • E – Events / Environment: mechanism of injury, speed, height of fall, penetrating vs blunt, use of restraints/helmet, alcohol or drugs, time since injury.

2. General Examination

Perform a rapid general inspection before the detailed head-to-toe exam:

  • Overall appearance, distress, posture and level of consciousness.
  • Skin colour, temperature, bruising, abrasions, lacerations and obvious deformities.
  • Look for clues to mechanism (seat belt marks, steering wheel imprint, tyre marks, etc.).

3. Systematic Head–to–Toe Examination

Head and Face

  • Scalp: lacerations, boggy swellings, step-offs, foreign bodies.
  • Skull and face: deformities, tenderness, mid-face instability, peri-orbital bruising, CSF leak from nose/ears.
  • Eyes: pupils (size, equality, reactivity), visual acuity if possible, subconjunctival haemorrhage, hyphema.
  • Mouth: loose teeth, blood, foreign bodies, mandible/maxilla instability.

Neck

  • Maintain C-spine protection throughout.
  • Inspect for wounds, bruising, swelling, seatbelt marks, tracheal deviation.
  • Palpate cervical spine for tenderness or step-offs (if cleared as per local protocol).
  • Assess for neck vein distention or flattening (suggestive of shock or tension pneumothorax).

Chest

  • Inspect chest wall movement, symmetry, bruising, deformity, penetrating wounds.
  • Palpate for tenderness, crepitus, rib or sternal fractures, flail segments.
  • Percuss for dullness or hyper-resonance.
  • Auscultate breath sounds bilaterally and heart sounds (muffled, added sounds).

Abdomen

  • Inspect for distension, bruising (Cullen, Grey-Turner), wounds or seatbelt marks.
  • Palpate for tenderness, guarding, rigidity, masses.
  • Assess for rebound tenderness and peritonitis (if not contraindicated).
  • Consider rectal exam and vaginal exam when indicated (per local protocol and chaperone policy).

Pelvis and Perineum

  • Inspect for bruising, deformity, blood at urethral meatus, perineal or scrotal haematoma.
  • Assess pelvic stability gently once only if not already done; do not repeatedly rock the pelvis.
  • Look for limb length discrepancy or rotational deformity suggesting pelvic/hip injury.

Extremities

  • Inspect all limbs for deformity, swelling, bruising, wounds, and obvious fractures/dislocations.
  • Palpate bones and joints for tenderness and instability.
  • Assess pulses, capillary refill, warmth and colour (neurovascular status).
  • Check motor function and sensation in each limb.

Back and Posterior Surfaces

Log-roll the patient with spinal precautions, only when safe and enough staff are available.

  • Inspect entire back for bruising, abrasions, lacerations, penetrating injuries.
  • Palpate spine for tenderness, step-offs, deformity.
  • Assess sacrum, buttocks and posterior thighs.

4. Neurological Assessment

Reassess the nervous system in more detail (beyond the primary survey’s quick AVPU/GCS):

  • Level of consciousness, orientation (person, place, time, event).
  • Pupil size and reactivity, eye movements.
  • Motor power and tone in all four limbs.
  • Sensation (light touch, pain) in all limbs and dermatomes if feasible.
  • Glasgow Coma Scale – use the GCS calculator by clicking here.

Any deterioration in GCS, new focal deficit, or worsening headache/vomiting should prompt urgent reassessment and consideration of imaging or neurosurgical referral.

5. Adjuncts & Additional Tests

Adjuncts support the clinical exam and are guided by mechanism and local protocols:

  • Imaging: X-rays (C-spine, chest, pelvis, limbs), CT trauma (head/neck/chest/abdomen/pelvis), FAST/extended FAST ultrasound.
  • Laboratory tests: FBC, U&E, creatinine, glucose, coagulation, group and cross-match, lactate, ABG/VBG.
  • ECG: to assess for blunt cardiac injury, arrhythmias, ischemia, electrolyte disturbance.
  • Urinalysis: blood, protein, infection, myoglobin; screen for urinary tract or pelvic injuries.
  • Toxicology or alcohol levels when clinically indicated.
  • Serial vitals & observations: monitor response to fluids, blood products and interventions.

6. Documentation and Ongoing Monitoring

Thorough documentation is critical for continuity of care, handover, and medico-legal protection.

  • Record findings from the head-to-toe examination, neurological assessment and adjunct tests.
  • Document all interventions, medications, fluids and blood products given, with times.
  • Use a standard trauma sheet or EMR template where available for consistency.
  • Reassess the patient regularly and document trends in vitals and clinical status.
  • Update the team and hand over clearly if the patient goes to theatre, ICU, or another facility.

The secondary survey is not a once-off event — repeat elements whenever there is a change in the patient’s condition or after major interventions.

7. Summary

The trauma secondary survey is a structured, complete assessment performed after immediate threats to life have been addressed in the primary survey. By combining a focused trauma history, systematic head-to-toe examination, detailed neurological assessment and appropriate adjuncts, you can identify occult injuries early, prioritise management, and improve outcomes.
